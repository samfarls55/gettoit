## Choice Architecture / Nudge

_Source: `Choice_Architecture_Nudge.json`_

### Basic Info

**framework_name**: Choice Architecture / Nudge

**originators**: Richard H. Thaler and Cass R. Sunstein (with John P. Balz on the formal Choice Architecture paper)

**origin_year**: 2008

**discipline**: Behavioral economics (with strong roots in cognitive psychology and decision science; built on Kahneman & Tversky's dual-process / heuristics-and-biases tradition)

**one_sentence_summary**: Choice architecture is the deliberate design of the context in which decisions are presented (defaults, ordering, framing, friction, salience, social proof) so that predictable cognitive shortcuts steer people toward a chosen option without removing any alternatives.

### Mechanic

**core_mechanism**: The framework treats every decision environment as already-architected, then prescribes redesigning that environment so that System 1 (fast, automatic) processing lands on the desired option. The operation it performs is not aggregation or scoring; it is environmental redesign. Concretely it manipulates: (1) the default option (what happens if the chooser does nothing), (2) the order and salience of options (primacy/recency, prominence on screen), (3) the framing of options (gain vs. loss, descriptive labels, units), (4) friction (effort required to pick A vs. B), (5) feedback (showing consequences of past choices), and (6) social proof (showing what most people picked). It assumes choosers are bounded-rational and predictably biased, so structuring the environment is more reliable than asking them to deliberate.

**steps_or_components**: Thaler and Sunstein's NUDGES mnemonic (six principles of good choice architecture): (1) iNcentives - align cost/benefit with the desired choice and make incentives salient; (2) Understand mappings - translate options into terms the chooser can actually compare (e.g., MPG vs. gallons-per-mile); (3) Defaults - pre-select the option most people should land on; (4) Give feedback - tell the chooser how prior choices played out; (5) Expect error - design for the mistakes people predictably make; (6) Structure complex choices - chunk, filter, or stage long option sets (e.g., elimination-by-aspects, collaborative filtering) so they fit working memory.

**input_signals**: The choice architect (not the chooser) supplies most inputs: a model of the target chooser's biases, a normative target option (what 'better' means here), the option set, and the channel (menu, form, app screen). The chooser supplies only minimal signals - often nothing more than passive acceptance of the default, a click on a salient option, or a yes/no on a framed prompt. This is one of the framework's defining traits: it minimizes what it asks the decider to articulate.

**output_form**: A single chosen option for the individual, but the framework's direct output is actually a designed environment - a default plus an arrangement of alternatives. From the chooser's perspective the output looks like 'the option I picked'; from the architect's perspective the output is a behavior distribution shifted toward the target option.

**typical_use_cases**: Retirement-savings auto-enrollment, organ donation opt-out registries, healthier cafeteria/menu placement, default green-energy tariffs, calorie-labeling and menu re-ordering, sustainable-meal nudges in food-delivery apps, public health (vaccination reminders, smoking cessation prompts), tax compliance letters, and consumer-facing form design (e.g., insurance, loans, privacy settings).

### Evidence Base

**known_limitations**: (1) Effect sizes shrink dramatically under publication-bias correction and in pre-registered replications (d ~= 0.01 in pre-registered behavioral-intervention studies). (2) Durability is poorly established - most nudges measure one-shot behavior, not sustained change; default effects persist, most others may not. (3) Effects are heterogeneous across domains and populations; what works in cafeteria food does not transfer to financial decisions. (4) Ethical critiques: manipulation, infantilization, autonomy violation, 'fragmented self,' and the worry that the architect's preferences (not the chooser's) get encoded. (5) Backfire risk - nudges can trigger reactance when detected. (6) The framework is descriptive about 'how to design' but underspecified about how groups should aggregate preferences; it implicitly assumes a single architect and a single chooser.

### Group Decision Fit

**individual_vs_group**: Designed almost entirely for individual decisions. The chooser is modeled as one bounded-rational agent and the architect designs for that agent. Generalization to groups is not native to the framework - Thaler and Sunstein discuss it only obliquely (e.g., social-proof nudges that show what 'most people' did, which uses the group as input to influence an individual's choice). There is no built-in mechanism for combining multiple deciders into one verdict.

**aggregation_strategy**: None natively. The framework does not aggregate preferences - it shapes a single chooser's environment. To use it for a group, the product must add an aggregation layer on top (e.g., majority vote, default-then-veto, or one architect designing for the whole group). Social proof is the closest in-framework primitive: surface what most of the group already wants and let conformity pressure converge the rest.

**handles_disagreement**: Poorly, on its own. Choice architecture has no conflict-resolution rule; it presumes a single chooser. Applied to a group it must either (a) pick a smart default and let dissenters opt out, (b) use social-proof framing to surface the modal preference and pressure outliers to conform, or (c) escalate to a separate aggregation rule. Default-with-veto is the closest native pattern but tends to favor the vocal/contrarian member rather than producing a fair group verdict.

**procedural_fairness**: Mixed and depends heavily on transparency. A 'loser' may not realize a nudge happened, which sidesteps overt unfairness but raises manipulation/autonomy concerns. When detected, nudges can feel paternalistic or rigged - especially if the default visibly favored someone else's preference. For mid-stakes social decisions where the loser will eat the meal anyway, perceived fairness is fragile: the loser is likely to feel the result was 'set up' rather than chosen.

### Quiz Mapping

**fit_for_fixed_length_quiz**: Moderate fit, but as a design philosophy rather than as the verdict-producing mechanism. A 5-question parallel quiz cannot itself be a nudge, but every screen of GetToIt is a piece of choice architecture: question ordering, default answers, friction to dissent, and how the verdict is framed all shape follow-through. Nudge thinking is best applied as a meta-layer on top of whichever aggregation framework is chosen, not as the framework itself. As the primary verdict engine it underdelivers - it has no aggregation rule and would force the product to bolt one on.

**question_design_implications**: If used as a meta-layer: (1) make the most-likely-good option the default answer for each quiz question (lowers friction, exploits status-quo bias to surface real preferences only when they exist); (2) order options by social proof (show 'most of your group said X' once partial answers come in); (3) frame the verdict as a commitment ('You're in. Tap to back out') rather than a question, to leverage default effects on follow-through; (4) chunk the option space (5 questions = structuring complex choices into bite-size attribute filters); (5) give feedback at the end ('3 of 4 wanted spicy - here's the place'). If used as the primary mechanism: questions would have to extract a single architect's prediction of the group's bias, which is conceptually awkward.

**signal_type**: Mostly passive signals: defaults accepted, friction overcome (or not), salience-driven clicks. Secondary explicit signals: framed binary preferences (would you rather X or Y, where the framing itself is the nudge). The framework deliberately minimizes asked-for signal because every additional question introduces noise and bias.

**tiebreaker_rule_implication**: The native tiebreaker is 'whatever the default was,' i.e., status-quo wins. If no default has been set, social proof breaks ties (modal preference wins). Neither rule is principled for fairness - they reward whoever set up the architecture, not whoever has the strongest legitimate claim.

**implementation_complexity**: Low to medium. Adding nudge primitives (smart defaults, ordering, social-proof copy, framing) on top of an already-chosen aggregation method is cheap - mostly UX copy, ordering logic, and a default-selection rule. As a standalone verdict engine it is medium-to-high complexity because the team would have to invent the missing aggregation layer themselves. Recommended use: low-cost meta-layer.

### Recommendation

**strengths_for_GetToIt**: (1) Default effects are the single most replicable behavioral intervention and map directly to follow-through - if the verdict is presented as 'you're going, tap to back out' instead of 'do you want to go?' the north-star metric (commitment, not majority) gets a measurable lift. (2) Food domain is where nudges are strongest empirically (Mertens et al. 2022 - food shows the largest effects), exactly GetToIt's 0.1.0 vertical. (3) Social-proof framing of partial group answers ('3 of 4 want spicy') is cheap to implement and increases convergence. (4) Reduces signal burden on the chooser - aligns with a 5-question quiz philosophy. (5) Improves follow-through specifically (the metric the product cares about), separately from whether the verdict itself is good. (6) Composes well with any underlying aggregation rule, so it is non-exclusive with other candidates.

**weaknesses_for_GetToIt**: (1) Provides no aggregation mechanism - cannot be the primary engine. (2) Effect sizes are heavily contested; banking on a 'nudge will fix follow-through' could under-deliver if publication-bias-adjusted effects (d ~= 0.01) are closer to truth. (3) Procedural fairness risk: a 'loser' who realizes the verdict was nudged will feel manipulated, which can poison group trust and reduce future use - a serious risk in ongoing friend groups. (4) Manipulation/ethics critique applies most when the architect's interests differ from the group's; GetToIt has commercial incentives (restaurant promotions, partnerships) that could compromise this. (5) Defaults require the product to know what 'good' is for a given group - a non-trivial inference problem. (6) Reactance risk if the nudge is too obvious, especially among internet-savvy users.

**adoption_verdict**: secondary / sub-signal - reject as the primary verdict engine (no aggregation rule), adopt as a follow-through and UX layer on top of whichever aggregation framework wins, with explicit attention to default-design for the verdict screen and social-proof framing of partial group answers.

## Elimination by Aspects (EBA)

_Source: `Elimination_by_Aspects.json`_

### Basic Info

**framework_name**: Elimination by Aspects (EBA)

**originators**: Amos Tversky

**origin_year**: 1972

**discipline**: Mathematical psychology / cognitive psychology / behavioral decision theory (later adopted by marketing science and behavioral economics)

**one_sentence_summary**: A non-compensatory choice heuristic in which the decider repeatedly picks an attribute (with probability proportional to its weight), eliminates every option lacking that attribute, and continues until one option remains.

### Mechanic

**core_mechanism**: EBA reframes each option as a bundle of 'aspects' (binary or thresholded attributes such as 'open now', 'serves vegetarian', 'under $30/head'). At each step the decider selects an aspect, with selection probability proportional to that aspect's subjective weight or salience, then eliminates every alternative that does not possess that aspect. The process iterates until exactly one option remains. Mechanically it is sequential filtering on attributes ranked by importance; statistically it is a random-utility model where the order of aspect selection is stochastic, which lets the same decider produce different outcomes on repeated trials when several aspects are similarly weighted. Because it is non-compensatory, a high score on one attribute cannot rescue an option that fails on a more important one - failures on important attributes are 'deal-breakers'.

**steps_or_components**:

- 1. Enumerate alternatives and the aspects (attributes/features) that distinguish them.
- 2. Assign each aspect a weight reflecting its importance/salience to the decider.
- 3. Probabilistically (or deterministically by rank, in the simplified 'lexicographic' version) select the next aspect, with probability proportional to its weight.
- 4. Apply the cutoff: eliminate every alternative that does not possess the selected aspect.
- 5. If more than one alternative remains, return to step 3 with the remaining alternatives and unused aspects.
- 6. Stop when a single alternative remains; that is the chosen option.
- Components: alternatives set, aspect set, aspect weights/cutoffs, elimination rule, termination condition.

**input_signals**: (a) The choice set of candidate options. (b) A list of attributes/aspects each option does or does not possess (or a per-attribute cutoff to binarize continuous attributes such as price or rating). (c) An importance ranking or weight per attribute. Optionally (d) per-attribute thresholds that define 'acceptable'. EBA does not require trade-off weights or full utility values - it only needs ordinal importance plus pass/fail per aspect.

**output_form**: A single chosen alternative (the last one standing). Intermediate output is a shrinking consideration set, so EBA also naturally produces an 'eliminated set' and a 'survivor set' at every step, which can be useful for explaining the result.

**typical_use_cases**: Consumer product choice from large assortments (cars, apartments, laptops, restaurants, dining out), marketing-science models of brand choice from scanner data, screening of job candidates, transportation project selection (TEBA framework), feature-driven app/SaaS selection, and any setting where the decider faces too many options to evaluate compensatorily and uses cutoffs/deal-breakers to prune.

### Evidence Base

**known_limitations**: 1. Non-compensatory by design, so a fantastic option can be killed by a single missing aspect, even if it dominates on every other attribute. 2. Highly sensitive to attribute order: the sequence in which aspects are picked determines the outcome, so two deciders with identical preferences but different orderings can choose differently. 3. Can violate transitivity and stochastic transitivity under standard assumptions, which makes it 'normatively irrational' from an expected-utility standpoint. 4. Hard to incorporate continuous, time-varying attributes like price without arbitrary thresholding. 5. Does not have a clean error theory in its original form (Kohli & Jedidi 2015 propose one). 6. Aggregating EBA preferences across people in a group can mask which criteria are actually driving the decision, which is a documented problem in the TEBA literature. 7. Output is binary at each step, so it discards information about how badly an option misses a cutoff.

**replication_status**: Tversky's core results have been replicated and extended for 50+ years. Key downstream work: Fader & McAlister 1990 (Journal of Marketing Research) calibrated EBA on scanner data. Kohli & Jedidi 2015 (Operations Research) gave EBA a usable error theory. Empirical online-marketplace work (IEEE 2014) found EBA-consistent patterns in real purchase logs. Process-tracing replications consistently find a sizeable minority of subjects use non-compensatory screening, with the rest mixing it with compensatory rules. No major replication failures of the basic phenomenon, but the model's claim to be the choice rule is challenged - it is more accurately a choice rule that coexists with compensatory ones and dominates under load.

### Group Decision Fit

**individual_vs_group**: Designed for individual choice. Tversky's 1972 model has no notion of multiple deciders. It generalizes to groups only via an explicit aggregation layer bolted on top - typically by aggregating individual aspect weights/cutoffs into a group-level weight vector before running EBA, or by running EBA per person and then merging surviving sets. The Transportation EBA (TEBA) framework is the most developed group adaptation, where stakeholders' weights are elicited and aggregated and then a single EBA pass is run on the group weights. The framework adapts cleanly because the elimination operation is set-theoretic (intersection of survivor sets) rather than utility-theoretic.

**aggregation_strategy**: Two natural group-aggregation patterns: (1) Union-of-vetoes / intersection-of-survivors: elicit each member's hard cutoffs, then for each aspect an option must pass if any member treats it as a deal-breaker - effectively the group survivor set is the intersection of individual survivor sets. (2) Weighted-aspect aggregation: elicit each member's aspect importance ranking, sum or average to get group weights, then run a single EBA pass. Pattern (1) is the more common and tractable adaptation for product use; it maps directly onto 'deal-breaker' UX. Pattern (2) preserves more nuance but requires per-member weight elicitation. Tversky's framework does not prescribe either - both are post-hoc extensions.

**handles_disagreement**: Handles hard constraint disagreement very gracefully (a vegan in the group eliminates all-meat restaurants, full stop) but handles soft preference disagreement poorly. If two members weight different attributes as most important, the order of aspect selection matters and arbitrary tie-breaks decide the result. There is no native mechanism for compromise or compensation: an option that is 'second-best for everyone' can be eliminated early because no member rates it as their top aspect. In practice, EBA in groups privileges the union of vetoes, which is conservative (small survivor sets) and is fair to constraints but not to preferences.

### Quiz Mapping

**fit_for_fixed_length_quiz**: Strong fit. EBA is structurally a sequential filter, and a 5-question quiz is a natural fixed-length elimination chain: each question elicits one aspect (or one cutoff), and each question shrinks the survivor set. Because the quiz is parallel rather than sequential, the GetToIt product would aggregate per-person answers per question, then apply elimination once at the group level using the union of vetoes. The 5-question budget aligns with EBA's empirical claim that only 3-4 criteria typically drive a real consumer decision, so a 5-question quiz is roughly the right size and not artificially truncated. EBA also has a natural stopping rule (one option remains), so fixed-length truncation is not a forced fit - the quiz length and the framework's working memory both bottom out at a similar number.

**question_design_implications**: Each question should elicit either (a) a hard constraint/veto on a specific attribute ('Anyone allergic or strictly avoiding X?', 'Hard budget cap?', 'Open this late?') or (b) an importance vote on which attribute matters most this round. Questions must be answerable in parallel by all members and must produce binary or thresholdable signals. Recommended question slots for a food-vertical quiz: (1) hard dietary deal-breakers (allergy/vegan/halal/kosher), (2) hard budget cap, (3) hard logistics constraint (distance/time/open-now), (4) one cuisine-veto or vibe-veto, (5) tiebreaker preference signal among survivors. Questions should NOT ask for trade-off weights or 'how much do you care' on a Likert scale because EBA does not need that signal and it adds noise.

**signal_type**: Primarily hard constraints / vetoes (binary deal-breakers per attribute) plus an attribute importance ranking. Secondary signal: per-attribute thresholds for continuous attributes like price. Not needed: full utility values, pairwise comparisons, mood vectors.

**tiebreaker_rule_implication**: If the survivor set has more than one option after all 5 questions, EBA itself provides no compensatory tiebreaker - the model would simply pick the next-most-important unused aspect or, if none remain, pick randomly. For GetToIt, a sensible product-level rule would be: after the EBA chain, break ties on a single secondary signal (e.g., highest minimum-acceptance score across the group, or random pick to avoid one-member dominance). The framework's strict logic is 'union of vetoes wins; among survivors, run another aspect; if no aspects left, choose by rank or randomly'.

**implementation_complexity**: Low. EBA is one of the simplest validated frameworks to implement: per-restaurant attribute tags (already present in most food data sources), per-quiz-question binary or thresholded answers, server-side intersection logic, and a tiebreaker rule. No ML, no preference elicitation theory, no Likert calibration, no pairwise-comparison UI. The hard parts are not algorithmic but data-quality (do we know which restaurants are actually halal, gluten-free, open-now, under $30/head?) and UX framing (questions must feel like deal-breakers, not preferences, or the 'losers feel filtered out' fairness problem appears).

### Recommendation

**strengths_for_GetToIt**: 1. Sequential-filter shape is a direct structural match for a 5-question elimination quiz. 2. Group aggregation via union of vetoes is mathematically trivial and intuitively fair - if any one person can't eat there, nobody eats there. 3. Aligns with the food vertical's reality: in real groups, food choices are dominated by hard constraints (allergies, budget, distance, dietary) more than by nuanced preference trade-offs. 4. Output is a single committed verdict by construction, which directly serves the north-star metric of follow-through (no majority-vs-minority split to negotiate post-quiz). 5. Explainable: the verdict UI can show 'we picked X because we cut Y for budget and Z for distance' which boosts perceived legitimacy and follow-through. 6. Implementation complexity is low, which is right for 0.1.0. 7. Empirically validated for exactly this kind of choice (consumer screening from a large set).

**weaknesses_for_GetToIt**: 1. Pure vetoes can over-prune: with five group members each contributing hard constraints, the survivor set can collapse to zero. The product needs a graceful fallback (relax least-cited veto, widen geographic radius, etc.) - this is real engineering, not free. 2. Procedural fairness for preference losers (vs. constraint losers) is weaker: a member whose 'I really want sushi' was outvoted may feel filtered, not compromised with. 3. EBA is non-compensatory, so a restaurant that is everyone's solid second choice can be eliminated early, which can hurt follow-through if members feel the verdict ignored their actual preferences. 4. Sensitive to question order: the order in which aspects are elicited determines outcomes, so question design is high-leverage and needs iteration. 5. Requires high-quality structured restaurant attribute data (dietary tags, real opening hours, real price tiers) - data quality bottleneck. 6. Does not natively handle 'soft' mood signals well (e.g., 'we want somewhere fun'), so a hybrid with a compensatory or vibe-based stage may be needed.

**adoption_verdict**: primary candidate - structurally the closest match to the 5-question parallel quiz, group-aggregates via union of vetoes that maps to real food-vertical deal-breakers, low implementation cost, produces single committed verdict by construction. Pair with a tiebreaker rule and a relax-veto fallback for empty survivor sets.

## Maximizer vs. Satisficer Scale (Maximization Scale)

_Source: `Maximizer_Satisficer_Scale.json`_

### Basic Info

**framework_name**: Maximizer vs. Satisficer Scale (Maximization Scale)

**originators**: Barry Schwartz, Andrew Ward, John Monterosso, Sonja Lyubomirsky, Katherine White, Darrin R. Lehman

**origin_year**: 2002

**discipline**: Cognitive psychology / decision science (with roots in Herbert Simon's bounded rationality, 1956)

**one_sentence_summary**: An individual-difference scale that classifies people on a continuum from 'maximizers' (who exhaustively search for the absolute best option) to 'satisficers' (who pick the first option that crosses a 'good-enough' threshold), and predicts that maximizers feel worse about their choices despite often choosing as well or better.

### Mechanic

**core_mechanism**: It is a diagnostic trait scale, not a decision procedure. Respondents rate agreement (1-5) with statements such as 'I never settle for second best' and 'When I watch TV, I channel surf, often scanning through the available options.' The composite score places the person on a maximizing-satisficing continuum. The framework simplifies decisions indirectly: by identifying that a maximizing disposition causes worse decision experiences (regret, second-guessing, lower satisfaction, lower follow-through), it prescribes adopting a satisficing strategy - set a threshold up front, take the first option that meets it, stop searching, and commit. The simplification is therefore a STOPPING RULE imposed on search.

**steps_or_components**: 1) Measure trait via the 13-item Maximization Scale (Schwartz et al., 2002) or one of its successors: 6-item short form (Nenkov et al., 2008), 9-item Maximization Tendency Scale (Diab et al., 2008), 34-item Maximization Inventory (Turner et al., 2012) with three subscales (alternative search, decision difficulty, satisficing), or Short Maximization Inventory. 2) Three commonly-extracted factors of the original scale: (a) Alternative Search - tendency to keep looking; (b) Decision Difficulty - struggle to choose among options; (c) High Standards - wanting only the best. 3) Prescriptive payoff: lower the standard from 'best possible' to 'good enough,' set a stopping rule, and commit.

**input_signals**: Self-reported Likert agreement with 6-34 trait items per scale variant. To use it as a decision aid (not just a diagnostic), the decider also supplies: (a) an aspiration threshold ('what counts as good enough'), (b) a stopping rule (e.g., first option crossing threshold), (c) a precommitment to not revisit the choice once made.

**output_form**: Diagnostic output: a continuous trait score (sometimes also subscale scores for alternative search / decision difficulty / high standards). Applied output: a behavioral prescription rather than a single chosen option - 'satisfice: pick the first acceptable option and commit.' It does not, by itself, produce a verdict from a candidate set.

**typical_use_cases**: Consumer choice (jobs, college, products with many alternatives), online dating, shopping (Schwartz's TV-channel and jam-jar metaphors), career decisions, and well-being research. Widely used in personality and decision-making studies as a moderator variable. Also cited in product-design and UX writing as justification for limiting options and providing strong defaults.

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Moderate to strong as a personality construct, weaker as a unified measure. The original 13-item scale (Schwartz et al., 2002, JPSP) was validated across multiple samples and correlates with regret, depression, perfectionism, decreased life satisfaction, and reduced optimism/happiness. Replicated in dozens of studies and a wide cross-cultural set. Behavioral evidence: Iyengar, Wells & Schwartz (2006) found maximizing graduating students secured jobs with 20% higher starting salaries yet were less satisfied with the outcome. Polman (2010) and others show maximizers experience both more positive AND more negative outcomes due to broader search. The 'high standards' subscale tends to correlate with positive outcomes; 'alternative search' and 'decision difficulty' correlate with negative outcomes (regret, indecisiveness, neuroticism).

**known_limitations**: 1) Psychometric problems with the original 13-item scale: Cronbach's alpha was only .70 (range .54-.75), and confirmatory factor analyses do not consistently support a single underlying construct. 2) Diab, Gillespie & Highhouse (2008) argued the scale conflates 'pursuit of the best' with 'decision difficulty' and proposed the 9-item Maximization Tendency Scale (MTS) measuring only high standards; their MTS does NOT correlate with the maladaptive outcomes (depression, regret) that Schwartz's scale picks up - suggesting the original scale's negative-outcome story is partly driven by a 'decision difficulty' subfactor that resembles indecisiveness/neuroticism rather than a true optimizing trait (jingle-jangle critique by Cheek & Schwartz, 2016). 3) The behavioral validity of self-report maximizing is questioned - self-rated maximizers do not always behave as maximizers in lab tasks. 4) Cross-cultural variation: maximizer status appears to be more harmful in WEIRD individualist cultures than in collectivist ones. 5) Trait-level: it diagnoses a person, not a single decision, so it does not directly produce or aggregate decisions. 6) The 'paradox of choice' itself (more options -> worse outcomes) has had inconsistent meta-analytic support (Scheibehenne et al., 2010 meta-analysis found a near-zero average effect of choice overload).

**replication_status**: Mixed. The basic correlations between maximizing and lower well-being have replicated widely. However, the original scale's factor structure has not replicated cleanly (Nenkov et al., 2008; Diab et al., 2008; Turner et al., 2012; Cheek & Schwartz, 2016 'On the meaning and measurement of maximization'). Multiple successor scales now exist; there is no single field-standard instrument. The downstream choice-overload claim (the popular 'paradox of choice') was challenged by Scheibehenne, Greifeneder & Todd's 2010 meta-analysis (mean effect ~0).

### Group Decision Fit

**individual_vs_group**: Designed strictly for INDIVIDUAL decision-making and individual personality assessment. There is no native group version of the scale. It generalizes to groups only as a moderator: the proportion or presence of maximizers in a group is expected to slow consensus, increase reopening of decisions, and lower follow-through, but the framework does not specify how to combine multiple maximizer/satisficer signals into a group verdict.

**handles_disagreement**: Not directly. It explains WHY disagreement is hard to close (a maximizer in the group will resist any 'good enough' verdict and keep searching, lowering follow-through), but offers no procedure to resolve a maximizer-vs-satisficer conflict. The implicit prescription is to override the maximizer's standard by imposing a stopping rule on the group (a satisficing default).

**procedural_fairness**: Likely LOW for the maximizer 'loser' and HIGH for the satisficer. A maximizer who feels the group settled too quickly is the prototype of a regretful loser - they will second-guess, blame the process, and resist commitment, which directly attacks GetToIt's follow-through north star. A satisficer is comfortable with 'good enough' and rarely contests outcomes. The framework warns that any group decision system that lets a maximizer keep searching (e.g., 'one more option,' 'are you sure?') will erode the very commitment the product is trying to manufacture.

### Quiz Mapping

**fit_for_fixed_length_quiz**: Poor fit as the PRIMARY decision engine, but excellent fit as a SUB-SIGNAL or onboarding trait. Reasons: (a) The scale is a trait inventory (6-34 items), not a per-decision questionnaire; (b) it does not aggregate across users into a verdict; (c) using it directly as the 5 quiz questions would produce a personality readout, not a restaurant pick. However, 1-2 short-form items (e.g., from the 6-item Nenkov scale) could classify each member as more-maximizer or more-satisficer at signup or once-per-account, and the verdict engine could then use that trait to (1) cap the option set surfaced to maximizers, (2) tighten the stopping rule when the group contains maximizers, and (3) frame the verdict as 'good enough for tonight, not the best ever' to pre-empt regret.

**question_design_implications**: If used as a trait sub-signal: ask 1-2 onboarding questions adapted from the Nenkov 6-item short scale, e.g., 'No matter what I do, I have the highest standards for myself' and 'When I am in the car listening to the radio, I often check other stations to see if something better is playing,' rated 1-5. If repurposed as per-decision quiz items: replace trait phrasing with decision phrasing - 'For tonight, are you looking for the best possible spot or just somewhere good enough?' (single Likert item) and 'How willing are you to revisit the choice if a friend pushes back?' (commitment item). The latter doubles as a follow-through proxy.

**signal_type**: Trait disposition + threshold/aspiration level + commitment willingness. Not a constraint, attribute ranking, or veto signal. Best treated as a moderator that adjusts how strictly the product enforces the satisficing stopping rule.

**tiebreaker_rule_implication**: Imposes a satisficer-favoring tiebreaker: when the group cannot agree, pick the first option that meets every member's stated minimum threshold (the satisficing rule), rather than the option that is best on aggregate score. Equivalently: 'the maximizer does not get to keep searching.' This converts ties into commits and protects follow-through, at the cost of feeling unfair to the strongest maximizer in the group.

**implementation_complexity**: LOW as a trait sub-signal (one onboarding screen with 2-6 Likert items, store on profile, use as a moderator in the verdict engine). MEDIUM if integrated into per-decision logic (need to detect group composition, adjust option set size, modulate stopping rule, and tune the post-verdict copy). HIGH if used as the sole decision engine - which would require inventing an aggregation rule, validating it, and would still not produce a verdict from a candidate set. Recommended: ship as LOW-complexity sub-signal in 0.1.0.

### Recommendation

**strengths_for_GetToIt**: 1) Speaks directly to the north-star metric (follow-through). The maximizer trait is essentially a follow-through-killer profile: maximizers reopen decisions, second-guess, and suffer post-decision regret, all of which reduce 'whole group commits.' 2) Justifies aggressive option-set compression - if even one group member is a maximizer, surfacing more options actively hurts. 3) Justifies firm stopping rules and 'no take-backs' UX patterns (cool-off-free verdicts, locked-in framing). 4) Explains WHY a single committed verdict beats a ranked list - ranked outputs invite re-litigation, which is the maximizer failure mode. 5) Cheap to instrument: a 1-2-item onboarding sub-signal is enough to start personalizing the stopping rule. 6) Aligns with the food-vertical 0.1.0 framing: dinner decisions are mid-stakes, recurrent, and reversible-feeling - exactly the domain where maximizers waste the most relational capital.

**adoption_verdict**: secondary / sub-signal - excellent moderator and onboarding trait that protects follow-through, but cannot be the primary 5-question engine; use Nenkov-style short-form items at signup, then let it tune option-set size, stopping rule, and post-verdict copy.

## Pre-mortem / Regret Minimization

_Source: `Pre_Mortem_Regret_Minimization.json`_

### Basic Info

**framework_name**: Pre-mortem / Regret Minimization

**originators**: Pre-mortem: Gary Klein (formalized 2007), built on prospective hindsight research by Deborah J. Mitchell, J. Edward Russo, and Nancy Pennington (1989). Regret minimization: Jeff Bezos (popularized 1994/97); related regret theory by Graham Loomes & Robert Sugden (1982) and David Bell (1982). Daniel Kahneman champions pre-mortem in 'Thinking, Fast and Slow' (2011).

**origin_year**: 1989 (prospective hindsight research) / 2007 (Klein's HBR formalization of pre-mortem) / 1994 (Bezos's regret minimization framing)

**discipline**: Cognitive psychology, behavioral decision science, naturalistic decision making (Klein); behavioral economics and regret theory (Loomes, Sugden, Bell, Bezos)

**one_sentence_summary**: Test a current choice by mentally jumping to the future and either imagining the plan has already failed (pre-mortem) or projecting your older self looking back at the regret of not choosing it (regret minimization), then deciding based on what that future view reveals.

### Mechanic

**core_mechanism**: Both techniques exploit prospective hindsight: shifting the decider from a posture of forecasting under uncertainty (where optimism bias and planning fallacy dominate) into a narrative-explanation posture about an event treated as already settled. The pre-mortem assumes the chosen option failed and asks 'why?', surfacing concrete failure modes that conventional risk analysis misses; Mitchell, Russo & Pennington (1989) showed this raises identification of correct future-outcome reasons by ~30%. Regret minimization flips the time horizon further: it asks the decider to inhabit a future self (Bezos's 'age 80') and choose the option that minimizes anticipated regret rather than maximizes expected value, making the asymmetric pain of inaction or wrong choice the deciding signal. Neither framework actually computes a choice; both are perspective-shift heuristics that re-rank options by exposing hidden risk (pre-mortem) or by re-weighting options through future-self emotional projection (regret minimization).

**steps_or_components**: Pre-mortem (Klein 2007): 1) Brief team on the plan/option already tentatively chosen; 2) Facilitator declares 'Imagine it is [future date] and this plan has failed catastrophically'; 3) Each member independently writes plausible reasons for the failure (independent generation prevents groupthink); 4) Round-robin share, one reason per person per round, until exhausted; 5) Consolidate failure modes; 6) Revise plan or kill it. Regret minimization (Bezos): 1) Identify the candidate choice; 2) Project forward to a distant future self (age 80, end of life, or other long horizon); 3) Ask 'will my future self regret NOT having done this?' and 'will my future self regret having done this?'; 4) Choose the option whose anticipated regret of omission exceeds anticipated regret of action. Combined logic: pre-mortem stress-tests risk; regret minimization stress-tests preference asymmetry.

**input_signals**: Pre-mortem requires: a tentatively chosen option, a vivid future failure frame, and free-form reasons-for-failure from each participant. Regret minimization requires: a candidate option, an imagined future-self time horizon, and a comparison of anticipated regret-of-omission vs regret-of-commission. Neither requires explicit utilities, probabilities, attribute weights, or rankings; both rely on imaginative projection and qualitative reasons.

**output_form**: Pre-mortem outputs an unranked list of plausible failure modes plus an updated/abandoned plan; it does not by itself pick an option, it sharpens or kills the leading candidate. Regret minimization outputs a single chosen option (go / no-go on the candidate, or a winner among two). Combined output: a single committed choice with attached risk-mitigation notes, or a rejection of the current candidate forcing a new one.

**typical_use_cases**: Pre-mortem: high-stakes project planning, military and intelligence planning, product launches, medical/safety protocols, post-merger integration, software releases, policy roll-outs, classroom and HCI pedagogy. Regret minimization: career pivots (Bezos leaving D.E. Shaw to found Amazon), founding decisions, relocations, end-of-life and medical preference-sensitive decisions, marriage/relationship choices, large irreversible bets where expected-value calculation is intractable.

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Pre-mortem has moderate empirical support. Foundational study: Mitchell, Russo & Pennington (1989, J. Behavioral Decision Making) found prospective hindsight increased correct reason-identification by ~30% vs forward forecasting. Veinott, Klein & colleagues evaluated the pre-mortem in field and lab settings (e.g., Veinott et al. 2010-2017 series; Roose, Lehman & Veinott 2023 game-development study) and reported that pre-mortems reduce overconfidence and yield more course-of-action creativity than pros/cons or critical-review controls. Health-implementation field study (PMC12330140) found implementation pre-mortems improved contextual fit. Regret minimization / anticipated regret has stronger empirical support as a behavior-change driver: a meta-analysis (Brewer et al. 2016, Health Psychology, PMC5408743) of 81 studies showed anticipated regret reliably predicts and influences health behavior; Zeelenberg (1999) and follow-ups demonstrated anticipated regret shifts choice toward risk-averse and feedback-avoidance behavior. However, both bodies of evidence are dominated by lab studies, hypothetical scenarios, and self-report; large-scale field RCTs are sparse.

**known_limitations**: Pre-mortem failure modes: (a) candor collapses when senior/authority figures are present, surfacing only already-known risks; (b) responses can devolve into vague generic reasons ('we didn't do enough research') unless facilitated; (c) techno-optimist or high-status cultures resist imagining failure; (d) it presupposes a leading option already exists, so it sharpens rather than chooses among options; (e) it can degrade morale or kill momentum if poorly framed; (f) effort cost (~20-30 min and structured facilitation) is non-trivial. Regret minimization failure modes: (a) people are poor at predicting their own future emotional states (affective forecasting errors, Gilbert & Wilson); (b) susceptible to framing and hindsight bias; (c) oversimplifies multi-attribute trade-offs; (d) does not extend cleanly to choices among three or more options (Loomes & Sugden's transitivity problem); (e) biases toward action and assumes the decider can absorb downside (financial / health resilience); (f) anticipated regret can also induce paralysis or excessive risk aversion depending on framing; (g) deeply individual-self framed, not group-aware.

### Group Decision Fit

**individual_vs_group**: Mixed origin. Pre-mortem was designed explicitly as a group exercise: Klein's 2007 protocol assumes a team that has been briefed on a plan and uses independent writing followed by round-robin sharing to surface diverse failure modes and counter groupthink. It generalizes well to groups by design. Regret minimization was designed as an individual introspective tool (Bezos's framing is first-person 'will I regret') and rooted in regret theory, which is an individual-utility theory. It does not natively aggregate across people and must be extended to groups via separate elicitation per member followed by some aggregation rule.

**aggregation_strategy**: Pre-mortem aggregates by union of failure modes: every member's reasons enter a combined risk list, and the team revises the plan against the merged set. This is additive/inclusive aggregation, not voting. It does not aggregate preferences over options; it stress-tests one already-chosen option. Regret minimization has no built-in aggregation. To use in a group, each member must independently project their own future regret for each option, and the group needs an external rule (vote, veto, weighting) to combine those signals. In practice this collapses to either 'choose the option no one would regret skipping' (consensus rule) or 'choose the option with highest aggregate anticipated regret of omission' (additive rule) - neither is part of the original framework.

**handles_disagreement**: Pre-mortem handles disagreement well within the structure of risk surfacing: dissenting voices are explicitly invited to articulate failure modes, and the protocol legitimizes negative views by reframing them as constructive forecasts rather than complaints. It does not, however, resolve preference conflicts over which option to pick; it only tells you whether the current candidate is fragile. Regret minimization handles individual ambivalence well (it surfaces the asymmetry between regret-of-action vs regret-of-inaction) but handles group disagreement poorly: two members can have opposite regret projections for the same option (one would deeply regret going to sushi, another would deeply regret not going), and the framework offers no principled way to reconcile them. Combined, the pair is best at killing bad shared options, weak at choosing between competing preferred options.

**procedural_fairness**: Pre-mortem feels fair to a 'loser' because the protocol gives every member an equal voice in surfacing risks and treats all stated failure modes as legitimate; the loser whose preferred option is killed sees the killing reasons in writing, owned by the group. Regret minimization can feel unfair when used in a group because it weights options by the regret of whoever feels most strongly, which functionally resembles a veto by the most regret-loaded member; a quieter member whose mild regret is overridden by a louder member's vivid future-self story may feel railroaded. Procedural fairness is high when each member's regret is privately elicited and visibly counted, low when one charismatic regret narrative dominates discussion.

### Quiz Mapping

**fit_for_fixed_length_quiz**: Poor to mediocre fit for a 5-question parallel quiz in pure form. Pre-mortem requires open-ended generative output (free-text failure modes) plus a deliberative facilitation step, both of which break the fixed-length, parallel-elicitation, machine-aggregable model GetToIt needs. Regret minimization requires deep imaginative projection that does not compress to a single quiz item without becoming shallow ('would you regret skipping this?' as a Likert item loses most of the cognitive shift the framework relies on). However, BOTH can be adapted as one or two sub-signal questions inside a larger quiz: e.g., a single 'if we don't do X tonight, how much would you regret it tomorrow?' slider captures regret-of-omission; a single 'name one way tonight goes wrong if we pick X' free-text or multi-select captures lightweight pre-mortem signal. As primary mechanism: reject. As sub-signal questions in a quiz built around another framework: viable.

**question_design_implications**: To extract pre-mortem signal in a quiz format the questions would need to: (1) present each candidate option and ask 'imagine tomorrow we tried this and it flopped - pick the most likely reason' from a constrained multi-select list (price too high, vibe wrong, too far, too crowded, dietary mismatch, etc.); (2) optionally allow one free-text 'why this would be bad' input. To extract regret-minimization signal: (1) ask 'if the group chose X and you went along, how much would you regret it tomorrow?' on a 1-5 scale per option, and/or (2) ask 'if the group skipped X tonight, how much would you regret missing it?' as a separate scale to capture regret-of-omission asymmetry. The framework does NOT generate the candidate options; the quiz must seed options from elsewhere.

**signal_type**: Pre-mortem extracts negative-attribute signal (failure modes / risks / vetoes) about a specified option. Regret minimization extracts regret-asymmetry signal (anticipated regret-of-omission minus regret-of-commission) per option per member - a signed scalar that captures preference intensity weighted by loss-aversion. Combined: veto-strength + regret-weighted preference, both inherently per-option-per-member rather than ranking-style.

**tiebreaker_rule_implication**: Pre-mortem implies a strong-veto tiebreaker: if any member surfaces a credible failure mode that the group accepts, that option is deprioritized regardless of majority preference. This is a 'avoid the worst' rule, not an 'optimize the average' rule. Regret minimization implies a max-regret-of-omission rule: when options are tied on regular preference, pick the option whose absence would generate the most regret in the most members (or equivalently, the option whose presence generates the least regret-of-commission). Both rules bias toward loss-aversion and toward avoiding catastrophic disappointment rather than maximizing peak satisfaction - which aligns with GetToIt's follow-through north star (groups follow through on options that do not have a vivid loser).

**implementation_complexity**: Medium-to-high for full implementation, low for sub-signal use. Full pre-mortem implementation is high: requires open-ended text capture, NLP clustering of free-form failure modes, a UI for round-robin or merged display, and a post-quiz revision step - none of which fit a 5-question parallel quiz. Full regret minimization is medium: Likert sliders per option per regret-direction are easy to capture, but generating per-member time-projection prompts and aggregating signed regret scalars into a group choice requires a custom aggregation rule. Sub-signal use of either is LOW complexity: a single multi-select 'what could go wrong with X' item or a single 'regret if skipped' slider per option drops cleanly into an existing quiz schema and produces a structured numeric signal.

### Recommendation

**strengths_for_GetToIt**: (1) Both frameworks attack the exact failure mode GetToIt's north star cares about: a verdict that the group accepts in name but defects from in practice. Pre-mortem surfaces silent vetoes (a member knows the option will flop but does not say so) before the verdict is committed, raising follow-through. (2) Regret minimization captures preference INTENSITY and ASYMMETRY, not just preference rank - which is critical when a 4-1 majority hides one member who would actively regret the choice and ghost the plan. (3) Both frameworks operate well in mid-stakes social decisions where exact utility computation is silly; they are built for the 'good enough, no one mad' regime food choices live in. (4) Pre-mortem's procedural fairness (everyone names a risk) maps directly onto GetToIt's group-first commitment value: even the 'loser' feels heard. (5) Sub-signal versions are cheap to ship: a single regret-of-skipping slider per candidate option is one quiz question that adds a high-signal asymmetry channel to whatever primary aggregation the quiz uses. (6) Loss-aversion bias of these frameworks aligns with the social reality of group dining: avoiding the dinner that ruins the night is worth more than catching the perfect dinner.

**weaknesses_for_GetToIt**: (1) Neither framework picks options; they require an existing candidate set, so GetToIt still needs a separate option-generation and primary-ranking mechanism. (2) Pre-mortem's free-text generative format is a poor fit for a fixed-length parallel quiz; compressing it to multi-select dilutes its core mechanism (the unstructured imaginative leap is what surfaces non-obvious risks). (3) Regret minimization's time-projection mechanic loses force at the 'where do we eat tonight' time horizon - 'will my 80-year-old self regret skipping this taco truck' is absurd; the framework works best on irreversible high-stakes decisions, and tonight's dinner is reversible (next time). (4) Regret minimization has no native group-aggregation rule; GetToIt would have to invent one (max-regret-of-omission veto, sum, etc.) and that aggregation rule then becomes the actual decision algorithm. (5) Both frameworks are individual-introspection heavy; they assume a deciders willing to imagine futures, which is high cognitive load for a 5-question quiz that should be answerable in under 60 seconds while half the group is on a bus. (6) Anticipated-regret signals are noisy (affective forecasting is poor) and can be gamed by the most dramatic group member.

**adoption_verdict**: secondary / sub-signal. Reject as the primary decision mechanism for GetToIt 0.1.0 (poor quiz fit, no native option-aggregation, cognitive load too high), but adopt regret-of-omission as a one-question sub-signal per candidate option to capture preference INTENSITY and protect follow-through, and optionally add a single multi-select 'what could go wrong' pre-mortem-lite item as a veto channel.

## Satisficing

_Source: `Satisficing.json`_

### Basic Info

**framework_name**: Satisficing

**originators**: Herbert A. Simon

**origin_year**: 1956 (term coined); 1957 (Models of Man collection); concept rooted in Administrative Behavior, 1947

**discipline**: Behavioral economics / cognitive psychology / decision science (bounded rationality)

**one_sentence_summary**: Instead of evaluating every option to find the optimum, the decider sets an aspiration-level threshold and picks the first alternative that meets or exceeds it.

### Mechanic

**core_mechanism**: Replaces utility maximization with a stop rule. The decider defines an aspiration level (a minimum acceptable bundle of attributes) and searches the option set sequentially or in parallel; the first option that clears the threshold is chosen and search ends. If no option clears the bar after some search budget, the aspiration is adjusted (typically lowered) and search resumes. This converts an intractable optimization problem into a tractable threshold-check, which is Simon's response to bounded rationality: human attention, time, and information are scarce, so 'good enough' is procedurally rational.

**steps_or_components**: 1) Set aspiration level alpha (acceptability threshold across one or more attributes). 2) Sample/inspect options sequentially. 3) Accept the first option whose attributes clear alpha on every required dimension. 4) If no option clears alpha within search budget beta, adjust alpha by gamma (usually lower) and resume search. 5) Stop and commit when an acceptable option is found.

**input_signals**: An aspiration level (threshold) per relevant attribute (e.g., price ceiling, minimum quality, must-have features), a search/inspection order over the option set, and a stopping/adjustment rule for when no option clears the bar.

**output_form**: A single committed choice (the first option that clears the threshold). Not a ranking, not a score - just accept/reject and commit.

**typical_use_cases**: Used-car pricing (97% of dealers used satisficing rules in one field study), job acceptance, apartment search, managerial decisions under time pressure, consumer choice with information cost, everyday decisions like restaurant or meal selection, and any setting where exhaustive comparison is too costly.

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Substantial. Simon's original formulation has been validated in field and lab settings: (a) Field - analysis of 628 used-car dealers showed ~97% used a satisficing pricing rule, lowering the price by ~3% if a car had not sold after 24 days. (b) Lab - Caplin, Dean, and Martin (2011, AER) found strong support for sequential search with an acceptability stopping rule. (c) Aspiration-level adaptation (raising after failure, lowering after success) has cybernetic-feedback support in lab studies. (d) Schwartz et al. (2002) and follow-up work on the Maximization Scale show satisficers report higher life satisfaction, less regret, and more decision happiness than maximizers, even when maximizers achieve objectively better outcomes (e.g., 20% higher starting salaries but lower job satisfaction).

**known_limitations**: 1) Aspiration level is exogenous - the framework does not specify where the threshold comes from, and bad thresholds produce bad decisions. 2) Order-dependent: which option is encountered first changes what gets chosen, so satisficing is not invariant to presentation order. 3) Can leave large amounts of value on the table when option quality varies widely. 4) Defining 'acceptable' on multi-attribute options requires either compensatory tradeoffs (defeating simplicity) or non-compensatory minima (which can over-reject). 5) In group settings, members may have incompatible thresholds and the framework gives no native rule for reconciling them. 6) Some empirical work questions whether 'maximizers' really search more or just feel worse about choices (Diab et al.; short-Maximization-Scale debates).

**replication_status**: Core sequential-search-with-stopping mechanism replicates well in lab and field. The Schwartz Maximization Scale itself has had factor-structure issues - Diab, Gillespie, and Highhouse (2008) and Nenkov et al.'s short-form work split the original scale into sub-components (high standards, alternative search, decision difficulty), and 'maximization' is now treated as a multidimensional construct rather than a unitary trait. The behavioral satisficing rule (Simon) is robust; the personality construct (Schwartz) is contested.

### Group Decision Fit

**individual_vs_group**: Originally formulated for individual decision-makers under bounded rationality. Generalizes to groups in two ways: (a) consensus-as-satisficing - the group searches for a solution every member finds acceptable rather than the group's collective optimum; (b) aggregated thresholds - each member contributes a threshold and the group accepts the first option that clears every member's bar (intersection of acceptable sets). Wikipedia and consensus-process literature explicitly note: 'Satisficing also occurs in consensus building when the group looks towards a solution everyone can agree on even if it may not be the best.'

**aggregation_strategy**: Most natural aggregation: intersection of individual acceptable sets. Each member supplies thresholds (must-haves, vetoes, minimum quality bars); the group accepts the first option that clears every member's threshold. Variants: (a) k-of-n satisficing (option must clear thresholds for at least k of n members), (b) weighted thresholds (some members' bars are firmer), (c) aspiration-adjustment if intersection is empty (members agree to relax non-critical bars).

**handles_disagreement**: Strong on hard constraints (vetoes), weak on soft preferences. If members disagree on must-haves (e.g., one is vegan, one wants a steakhouse), the intersection is empty and the framework forces explicit aspiration adjustment - someone must lower a bar. This makes disagreement visible and negotiable rather than averaged away. But it does not natively rank options for which everyone is above threshold (no 'best of the acceptable'); it just commits to the first one found.

**procedural_fairness**: Reasonably high for the 'loser' case: because every accepted option clears every member's threshold, no member ends up with an option below their stated minimum. Nobody loses on a hard constraint. The losing experience is 'I didn't get my favorite' rather than 'the group picked something I find unacceptable', which is a much weaker grievance. Caveat: fairness depends on members honestly stating thresholds rather than gaming them.

### Quiz Mapping

**fit_for_fixed_length_quiz**: Strong fit. A 5-question parallel quiz can extract each member's thresholds in a few questions and then compute the intersection server-side. Threshold elicitation is well-suited to discrete quiz answers (price ceiling buckets, cuisine vetoes, distance caps, dietary must-haves, vibe minimums). The framework only requires accept/reject signals per attribute, not full preference rankings, which is exactly what a short quiz can deliver. The output (a single committed verdict) maps cleanly to GetToIt's north-star metric of follow-through, since 'option clears every member's bar' produces a verdict the group has implicitly pre-committed to.

**question_design_implications**: Questions should elicit thresholds and vetoes, not preferences. Examples: 'Hard no on which cuisines?' (veto), 'Max walk time?' (threshold), 'Max price per person?' (threshold), 'Must have vegetarian options? (yes/no/preferred)' (constraint with weight), 'Mood floor: how lively does it need to be at minimum?' (ordinal threshold). Avoid 'rank these' or 'rate from 1-10' - those are maximizer questions. Each question should produce a filter the system can apply.

**signal_type**: Thresholds and vetoes (non-compensatory minima per attribute). Secondary signal: aspiration-adjustment willingness (which bars can flex if the intersection is empty).

**tiebreaker_rule_implication**: Tiebreaker is implied by the search order: the first acceptable option wins. In a parallel-quiz product, 'first' must be defined - candidates: (a) randomize within the acceptable set (most fair, surprises group), (b) order by a neutral signal like distance or popularity, (c) order by a single soft preference aggregated across the group. If acceptable set is empty, the framework prescribes aspiration relaxation - product would surface 'these bars conflict, who can flex?'

**implementation_complexity**: Low to medium. Low because the core operation is filter-and-pick - server takes each member's thresholds, intersects acceptable option sets, returns the first survivor. No scoring, no ranking, no ML. Medium because (a) defining 'first' in the acceptable set requires a deterministic-but-fair ordering, (b) handling empty intersections requires a UX flow for aspiration relaxation (whose bar moves?), (c) threshold elicitation must be tight - users will not patiently set 8 sliders, so the ~5-question quiz must pick the highest-leverage thresholds for the food vertical.

### Recommendation

**strengths_for_GetToIt**: 1) Native fit for the parallel-quiz format - threshold questions are exactly what a 5-question quiz can ask. 2) Produces a single committed verdict, not a ranking, which is what the product needs. 3) Strong follow-through prior: because every member's hard constraints were cleared, nobody has standing to reject the verdict, raising the chance the whole group commits (the north-star metric). 4) Procedurally fair - 'loser' didn't get their favorite but did get something above their stated minimum. 5) Bounded-rationality-honest - matches how groups actually choose food in low-stakes social settings ('anywhere we can all live with'). 6) Cheap to implement in 0.1.0 - filter logic, no ranking model.

**weaknesses_for_GetToIt**: 1) Quality of verdict depends entirely on threshold quality - if a quiz fails to elicit a real veto, the verdict can flop. 2) No native answer when the intersection is empty - product must build a relaxation flow, which is extra UX. 3) Order-of-search matters and is not specified by the framework, so the product must defend a tiebreaker rule that feels fair (random vs. distance vs. popularity). 4) Underwhelming when one option is dramatically better than another acceptable one - satisficing will not differentiate. 5) Members may state inflated thresholds to bias the outcome (strategic gaming); product needs guardrails like default-reasonable bars or social transparency about who is the binding constraint.

**adoption_verdict**: primary candidate - Satisficing is arguably the closest theoretical match to GetToIt's quiz-to-committed-verdict flow for mid-stakes group food decisions; it converts cheaply to threshold questions and produces a verdict the whole group has implicitly pre-accepted, which directly serves the follow-through north star.

## WRAP

_Source: `WRAP.json`_

### Basic Info

**framework_name**: WRAP

**originators**: Chip Heath and Dan Heath

**origin_year**: 2013

**discipline**: Applied behavioral decision science / popular decision-making (synthesis of cognitive psychology, behavioral economics, and organizational behavior)

**one_sentence_summary**: WRAP is a four-step process (Widen options, Reality-test assumptions, Attain distance, Prepare to be wrong) designed to counter four common decision-making biases: narrow framing, confirmation bias, short-term emotion, and overconfidence.

### Mechanic

**core_mechanism**: WRAP is a process-driven debiasing protocol rather than a calculation. Instead of telling deciders how to weight or score options, it forces them to expand the option set, stress-test their assumptions against reality, get emotional and temporal distance from the choice, and pre-plan for failure. The simplification comes from countering specific named biases at each stage, with the underlying claim that 'process trumps analysis' (the Heaths cite a Lovallo/Sibony study finding process mattered six times more than analysis quality in business decisions).

**steps_or_components**: 1) Widen Your Options - counters narrow framing; tactics include the Vanishing Options Test ('what would you do if none of these options were available?'), multitracking (consider options simultaneously, not sequentially), framestorming, and finding people who have solved your problem. 2) Reality-Test Your Assumptions - counters confirmation bias; tactics include considering the opposite, zooming out (base rates) and zooming in (close-up texture), and 'ooching' (small experiments / pilots before full commitment). 3) Attain Distance Before Deciding - counters short-term emotion; tactics include the 10/10/10 rule (how will I feel in 10 minutes / 10 months / 10 years), advising a friend, and identifying core priorities. 4) Prepare to Be Wrong - counters overconfidence; tactics include premortems ('it's a year from now, the decision failed - why?'), preparades (success scenario), bookending (best/worst case), setting tripwires (predefined triggers to revisit the decision), and building in a margin of safety.

**input_signals**: Requires the decider(s) to supply: the original decision frame, candidate options (which the framework then expands), assumptions and predictions about outcomes, current emotional state, core long-term priorities/values, and forecasts of how the decision could fail or succeed. Notably it requires reflective inputs, not just preference rankings.

**output_form**: A single committed choice plus a contingency plan (tripwires and margin of safety). The output is not a ranked list or score - it is a chosen action paired with predefined signals that would trigger reconsideration. WRAP is process-oriented, so the output is as much a better-quality decision as it is a specific selection.

**typical_use_cases**: Consequential personal and organizational decisions: career changes, hiring/firing, business strategy, capital allocation, M&A, product launches, major life choices (marriage, relocation), and high-stakes individual choices. Used in corporate training, executive coaching, leadership development, and as a personal decision journal practice. Less commonly applied to low-stakes everyday choices.

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Mixed and indirect. The framework itself has not been validated as a holistic intervention in peer-reviewed RCTs. However, its constituent tactics draw on a substantial empirical base: premortems (Klein, 2007), confirmation bias and consider-the-opposite (Lord, Lepper, Preston 1984), 10/10/10 (Suzy Welch, popular), construal-level/psychological distance (Trope & Liberman), base-rate neglect and outside view (Kahneman & Tversky; Kahneman & Lovallo 1993), and the Lovallo & Sibony McKinsey study (2010) of 1,048 business decisions finding process explained ~53% of variance in outcomes versus ~8% for analysis. The Heaths synthesize these; they did not run validation studies of WRAP as a packaged process.

**known_limitations**: 1) The package itself is not empirically tested as a unit - it is a synthesis of separately-validated tactics. 2) High cognitive and time cost; not designed for fast or low-stakes decisions. 3) Designed primarily as an individual or small-leadership-team protocol; lacks a built-in aggregation rule for groups with conflicting preferences. 4) Some reviewers note the book is more anecdotal than rigorous and that case studies feel cherry-picked. 5) Devil's advocate prescription has been challenged in subsequent research (Nemeth et al.) showing assigned dissent is less effective than authentic minority dissent. 6) 'Attain distance' assumes a single decider with personal emotions - awkward to apply when emotions belong to multiple group members. 7) The 'ooch' / pilot prescription does not fit decisions that must be one-shot and irreversible.

### Group Decision Fit

**individual_vs_group**: Designed primarily for individual deciders and small executive teams led by a single decision-maker. The Heaths illustrate it with both personal choices (career, relationships) and organizational ones (CEO decisions, hiring). It generalizes to groups by being run as a structured group process - e.g. team framestorming sessions, group premortems, assigning a devil's advocate - but it does not natively define how to combine multiple parties' preferences. Group use depends on a facilitator who can run each step collectively.

**aggregation_strategy**: No explicit aggregation rule. WRAP assumes a final decider (individual, CEO, leader) who runs the four steps and ultimately picks. When applied to groups, aggregation is done implicitly through facilitated discussion, dissent-seeking (devil's advocate), and consensus or leader-decides. There is no voting rule, weighted scoring, or formal social-choice mechanism baked in.

**handles_disagreement**: Treats disagreement as a feature, not a bug, in the Reality-Test stage: actively seek disconfirming views, assign devil's advocates, find people who disagree, and surface contradictory evidence. However, this is about improving decision quality, not resolving who wins. When parties have genuinely conflicting preferences (e.g. one person wants Italian, another wants sushi), WRAP gives no rule for resolution - the leader still decides after hearing dissent.

**procedural_fairness**: Moderate to high on voice and information fairness (everyone is heard, dissent is solicited, assumptions are tested), but low on outcome fairness for a 'loser' because there is no transparent aggregation rule - the final pick can feel arbitrary or leader-driven. Process can feel fair if facilitation is good and dissent is genuinely surfaced; can feel unfair if it amounts to 'we discussed your view, then picked the other option anyway'.

### Quiz Mapping

**fit_for_fixed_length_quiz**: Poor to weak fit for a 5-question parallel quiz. WRAP is sequential and reflective: each step requires deliberation, expansion, and revisiting earlier steps. The 'Widen' step alone typically takes longer than a full 5-question quiz. WRAP also assumes iterative loops (ooching, premortems happen after initial selection) that a parallel one-shot quiz cannot accommodate. It could inspire individual quiz questions (e.g. one premortem-style question) but cannot be implemented as a whole.

**question_design_implications**: To partially feed WRAP, quiz questions would need to: 1) Surface options the asker did not consider (Widen) - hard to do with fixed-length structured questions. 2) Test assumptions ('what would have to be true for X to be a good choice?') - requires open-ended input. 3) Get emotional distance ('would you recommend this to a friend?', 'which option will you regret in a week?') - feasible. 4) Pre-mortem ('if this night flops, why?') - feasible as one question. The framework's Widen and Reality-Test steps resist compression into multiple-choice format.

**signal_type**: Reflective and meta-cognitive signals: option breadth, assumption confidence, emotional state vs core values, and predicted regret/failure modes. Not preference rankings, not constraints, not single-attribute thresholds - WRAP needs richer, more deliberative input than a quick parallel quiz typically extracts.

**tiebreaker_rule_implication**: Implies a 'leader/facilitator decides after dissent has been heard' tiebreaker, optionally informed by a premortem and tripwires. There is no native quantitative tiebreaker (no median, no veto, no Borda count). For GetToIt's group context this would translate to something like 'after the quiz, one person is appointed decider this round' - which is a meaningful design choice but one WRAP itself does not specify.

**implementation_complexity**: High. WRAP requires open-ended reflection (framestorming, premortems), iterative loops (ooching, tripwires), and facilitator-style prompts that do not compress into a 5-question structured quiz. Engineering-wise the steps demand free-text input and multi-step UX. UX-wise the time investment exceeds what a mid-stakes social food decision warrants. Adapting only one or two tactics (e.g. a premortem question, a 10/10/10-style time-distance question) is medium complexity; faithful full implementation in 0.1.0 is high.

### Recommendation

**strengths_for_GetToIt**: 1) The 'Prepare to be wrong' step maps directly onto follow-through, GetToIt's north-star metric - a premortem-style question ('if this dinner flops, why?') could surface group hesitations before commitment and improve commitment quality. 2) 'Attain distance' tactics like 10/10/10 and 'advise a friend' translate well into single quiz questions that defuse short-term emotion (hangry, tired) which is a real failure mode for food decisions. 3) The framework's emphasis on widening options counters the binary 'this place or that place' trap groups fall into - GetToIt could generate 3+ options rather than 2. 4) Its bias-naming language ('narrow framing', 'confirmation bias') is well-known to product-savvy users and gives the app a credible intellectual frame.

**weaknesses_for_GetToIt**: 1) WRAP is process-heavy and reflective - opposite of a fast 5-question parallel quiz; full implementation does not fit 0.1.0. 2) No native aggregation rule means WRAP cannot by itself produce a 'single committed verdict' from conflicting group input - GetToIt still needs an aggregation mechanism on top. 3) 'Widen' fights against GetToIt's UX of presenting a small finite option set; expanding mid-quiz is structurally awkward. 4) WRAP is designed for consequential decisions; using it for 'where to eat' may feel over-engineered to users. 5) 'Ooching' (pilot tests) does not apply to one-shot evening plans. 6) Procedural fairness for the loser is mediocre because there is no transparent aggregation rule.

**adoption_verdict**: secondary / sub-signal - reject as the primary engine (no aggregation rule, too reflective for a 5-question quiz), but borrow specific tactics: a premortem-style commitment question and a 10/10/10-style distance question can directly serve the follow-through north-star metric.
