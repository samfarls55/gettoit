## Distributive Justice — Equity, Equality, and Need (three-rule typology)

_Source: `Distributive_Justice_Equity.json`_

### Basic Info

**construct_name**: Distributive Justice — Equity, Equality, and Need (three-rule typology)

**originators**: John Stacey Adams (equity theory, 1963/1965); Morton Deutsch (three-rule typology, 1975/1985); Gerald S. Leventhal (contribution rule and multi-principle allocation, 1976); Elaine Walster, G. William Walster, & Ellen Berscheid (Equity: Theory and Research, 1978); historical roots in Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics on distributive vs. corrective justice and George Homans 1961 'Social Behavior: Its Elementary Forms'

**origin_year**: 1965 (Adams, 'Inequity in Social Exchange', Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, vol. 2); 1975 (Deutsch, 'Equity, Equality, and Need', Journal of Social Issues, vol. 31); 1976 (Leventhal, 'The distribution of rewards and resources in groups and organizations', Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, vol. 9); 1978 (Walster, Walster & Berscheid, Equity: Theory and Research)

**discipline**: Social psychology (social exchange tradition); organizational behavior; sociology of justice; close-relationships research (Clark & Mills communal/exchange extension); cross-cultural psychology (Leung, Bond, Murphy-Berman); downstream applications in HRM, organizational citizenship behavior, and platform/algorithmic fairness

**one_sentence_summary**: Outcome fairness is judged not against an absolute standard but against an applied distribution rule — equity (proportional to contribution), equality (equal shares), or need (proportional to need) — and the choice of rule is itself a fairness signal that depends on the relational context (productivity, solidarity, or welfare).

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1: Members evaluate an allocation against an implicit distribution rule, not against absolute outcome goodness. Adams's 1965 formulation says inequity exists when person P perceives that the ratio of own outcomes to inputs (Op/Ip) differs from the comparison other's ratio (Oa/Ia); perceived inequity produces tension whose direction (anger vs. guilt) depends on whether P is under- or over-rewarded. Step 2: The applicable rule is not fixed. Deutsch (1975) argues members select among three rules based on the *primary goal of the relationship*: equity when productivity is salient (work, competition), equality when solidarity / harmonious relations are salient (friends, peers, fraternal groups), need when welfare / fostering personal development is salient (family, support relationships). Leventhal (1976) generalizes this — multiple rules can be combined or applied sequentially to determine the just allocation. Step 3: A verdict (the algorithmic outcome) is read by each member as evidence of *which rule the system is running*. The same outcome can feel fair under one rule and unfair under another. A member whose preference loses asks not 'is the outcome good?' but 'was the rule the right rule for this group?' Step 4: If the implicit rule matches the relational context (equality among friends, need where constraints exist, equity where contribution differs), the outcome is accepted even by the loser. If the implicit rule mismatches the context (equity-by-input-tracking among friends, equality where one member has a true allergy), the loser perceives an injustice independent of outcome utility and withholds buy-in. Step 5: Withheld buy-in is not loud rejection — it is delayed acceptance, sandbagged enthusiasm, and reduced follow-through. Empirical justice research (Cohen-Charash & Spector 2001; Colquitt 2001) finds distributive justice is most strongly tied to *outcome-specific satisfaction* (am I happy with this verdict?), whereas procedural justice is most strongly tied to *system evaluation* (do I trust this group/tool to decide for me again?). Both matter for GetToIt — distributive failure damages the current verdict, procedural failure damages return-use.

**behavioral_signature**: Observable signs that distributive justice is producing acceptance: (a) the loser of the verdict still arrives, on time, without strategic objections; (b) post-decision conversation does not relitigate the rule ('why did it pick that?'); (c) members reference the rule approvingly in subsequent sessions ('we did Maria's pick last time, it's Sam's turn'). Observable signs of distributive injustice: (a) the loser arrives late or with a hedged commitment ('I'll come for one drink'); (b) audible counter-counting after the verdict ('that's the third time we've done sushi'); (c) members start tracking inputs that the system ignored ('I drove last time, I'm not driving again'); (d) a member with a hard constraint discovers post-verdict that the option violates it (need-rule failure on hidden allergy/budget); (e) explicit accusations of the wrong rule ('this app keeps picking *your* favorite — it must be biased toward whoever opens it first'); (f) silent attrition — the member quietly stops participating in future sessions rather than confronting. Distinct from procedural injustice (which would manifest as 'I had no voice') and from interactional injustice (which would manifest as 'I wasn't treated with respect during the process'). Distributive injustice is specifically about the rule applied to outcomes.

**typical_triggers**: Effect strongest when: (1) the group has *visible heterogeneity in inputs* (one member always travels furthest, one always pays more) so an equity calculus is psychologically available; (2) one or more members have *hard constraints* (allergies, dietary, religious, budget) that make the need rule applicable and salient; (3) the verdict is *recurring across sessions*, so members can track distribution patterns over time and detect rule violations longitudinally; (4) the group has mixed in-group / out-group composition (a friend brings a date, a co-worker is added) which destabilizes the default solidarity rule and reactivates equity reasoning per Leung & Bond 1984; (5) outcome stakes are mid-to-high (tonight's dinner with friends, not a coffee preference); (6) the relational frame is ambiguous (is this work-friends or real-friends?) so the rule is contestable. Effect muted when: (1) the group is small (n=2) and turn-taking norms are obvious; (2) stakes are trivial; (3) one member has unambiguous authority (host, birthday person) which licenses dictatorship; (4) the group is highly homogeneous and the same outcome serves everyone's preference equally, so no rule needs to be invoked.

### Evidence Base

**known_limitations**: (1) The three-rule typology is descriptive, not prescriptive — Deutsch describes which rule groups *use*, not which rule is *correct*; the framework gives no purchase on adjudicating rule disputes. (2) Rules are not mutually exclusive — Leventhal 1976 explicitly allows hybrid and sequenced rules (need filters first, then equity allocates remainder), which is empirically realistic but theoretically messy. (3) The equity-as-desert critique (Current Psychology, 'Three principles of justice or one?') argues all three rules can be reduced to a single underlying desert principle with different inputs counted, undermining the typology's parsimony claim. (4) Cross-cultural moderators are real: collectivists emphasize equality and need with in-group members more than individualists do (Leung & Bond 1984); Indians weigh need more heavily than Americans, especially in negative allocations (Murphy-Berman et al. 1984). (5) Adams's 1965 ordinal-ratio formulation under-specifies how members aggregate multiple inputs (effort, time, seniority, skill) into a single 'input' term — different aggregations can flip the perceived equity, so the theory is harder to falsify than it looks. (6) Most empirical work is in workplace or bargaining contexts; transfer to friend-group consumer decisions (GetToIt's regime) is theoretically warranted but empirically thin. (7) The construct says little about *who decides which rule applies* — in algorithmic settings, the system designer is implicitly making this choice, which itself becomes a fairness object.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: Direct and high-leverage fit. GetToIt's core artifact is a verdict for a small friend-group; every verdict implicitly enacts a distribution rule whether the team chooses one or not. Three diagnostic facts about the use case: (a) Friends in a 'where to eat' decision are a *solidarity context* in Deutsch's typology — relational maintenance, not productivity, is the goal — so the default applicable rule is **equality**, not equity. (b) Hard-constraint members (vegans, celiacs, halal/kosher, severe budget) introduce a localized *welfare* sub-context where the **need rule** must dominate for those slots; ignoring need produces a verdict the constrained member literally cannot consume, which is the most catastrophic possible distributive failure for follow-through. (c) Equity (input/output ratio matching) is *actively wrong* for this group — friends do not maintain ledgers of who-cared-most-last-time the way a workplace tracks effort, and a system that overtly tracks 'whose turn is it' converts a communal relationship into an exchange relationship in Clark & Mills's 1979 sense, which damages the relationship itself. The verdict screen must therefore enact a hybrid: NEED-rule veto layer for hard constraints (no option appears that violates anyone's hard veto), then EQUALITY across sessions for soft preferences (rotation, randomization, or score-balanced selection that does not require members to surface or argue inputs), and EXPLICITLY NOT EQUITY (no input-tracking copy, no 'you got your way last time so this is fair' framing). This is not just a UX preference — it is what the literature predicts will produce acceptance in this relational frame.

**dominance_estimate**: High for follow-through, medium-high for return-use. Rationale: distributive justice is the construct most strongly tied to *outcome-specific* satisfaction (Cohen-Charash & Spector 2001), and follow-through is an outcome-specific behavior (do I show up to *this* dinner). When the rule is wrong for the context — particularly when need is violated by a hard-constraint mismatch — distributive injustice is plausibly the single largest preventable driver of follow-through failure, because the loser cannot rationalize the outcome away the way they might rationalize a procedurally-justified equity loss. Procedural justice (Voice, Leventhal's six rules, Tyler's group-value model) carries more weight for *return-use* and trust in the system. So the estimate is: high dominance for the immediate verdict's follow-through, medium for whether the group opens GetToIt again next week. Worth noting: the *correct* rule (need-then-equality) is unobtrusive — when applied well, it disappears as a felt construct and members never name it. The construct's dominance shows up mainly in failure modes, which is consistent with how distributive justice generally operates (taken-for-granted when met, intensely salient when violated).

**group_size_specificity**: Effect is consistent across the 2-6 friend-group range with modest moderation. At n=2, distributive justice collapses into bilateral reciprocity ('I picked last time, you pick this time') and the rule is usually transparent, so the construct is muted; the equality rule across sessions is the dominant pattern and easy to track in conversation. At n=3-6, distributive justice strengthens because: (a) longitudinal rule-tracking becomes harder for humans to do honestly (members systematically misremember their own previous wins), so the algorithm doing it transparently is genuinely valuable; (b) hidden constraints multiply (one veggie, one allergic, one budget-strapped), making need-rule failures more frequent; (c) the equity temptation grows — members rationalize input-based claims ('I drove') even when the underlying relationship is communal, so the system must actively suppress equity framings. At n>6, the regime shifts toward formal procedure (organizer model) which is outside scope. Net: distributive justice is moderately amplified in the 3-6 sweet spot relative to dyads, with need-rule violations being the most catastrophic failure mode at any size.

**ingroup_friend_specificity**: Substantially changed by the friend frame. Among friends (high prior trust, repeated interaction, communal relationship in Clark & Mills 1979), the *default* applicable rule is equality, with need as a second-layer constraint, and equity is suppressed or actively avoided — surfacing equity ledgers in a communal relationship is a relational violation. This contrasts sharply with the workplace contexts where most empirical justice work was done, where equity is the default and equality/need are exceptional. Implication: research from organizational settings *under-predicts* the harm of equity framing for GetToIt's user base, because workplace subjects have already accepted the equity frame as legitimate. Friends will have a stronger negative reaction to equity-style copy ('you got your way 4/10 times this month') than employees would. Conversely, friends will accept seemingly arbitrary equality ('we randomized, it's tacos') more readily than employees would, because in communal frames the *fact of fair process* substitutes for outcome-input matching. The repeated-interaction nature of friend groups also means rule violations compound — a single need-rule miss is recoverable, but pattern violations across 3-4 sessions corrupt the trust frame. Cross-cultural note (Leung & Bond 1984): collectivist friend-groups will lean even more heavily toward equality / need than individualist ones, but the rule-by-context structure is the same.

### Verdict-Screen Implication

**design_signal_to_express**: The verdict screen must signal that the outcome was produced by a rule appropriate to the relational frame, and must do so without invoking equity. Concretely, four signals: (1) **Need-rule visibility for hard constraints**: when a hard veto pruned options, surface that fact ('Sarah's gluten-free, so we filtered to options with celiac-safe kitchens') so the constrained member sees their constraint honored and others see the legitimate basis for the pruning. This is the highest-priority signal because need violations are the most catastrophic failures. (2) **Equality-rule signal for soft preferences**: when the verdict picks among options that all cleared everyone's constraints, communicate that the selection was balanced across members — language like 'this option scored highest across the group' or 'this is the option closest to everyone's middle ground' or 'we rotated — last time was Alex's pick, this is Maya's' — never language that scores members against each other. (3) **Suppress equity language**: never display input-output ratios, never frame the verdict as 'you got your turn now I get mine', never quantify a member's historical wins. Equity framing converts communal to exchange, and the cost outweighs any transparency benefit. (4) **Rule legibility, not rule defense**: state the rule once, briefly, in plain language; do not argue for it. The rule becoming visible is sufficient for acceptance in a solidarity context — defending it surfaces the very ledger-keeping it should be hiding.

**copy_framework_implications**: Concrete copy patterns. (1) **Name the constraint, not the constrainer when possible**: 'filtered for celiac-safe kitchens' beats 'filtered because Sarah has celiac' if the constrained member would prefer not to be repeatedly named — though for one-off allergies, naming is often welcome ('we picked this one because it has Tom's halal options'); the constrained member should ideally control whether their name is invoked. (2) **Use solidarity language, not exchange language**: 'closest to what your group wanted tonight' beats 'highest weighted score across users'. (3) **For rotation / equality across sessions**, frame as 'last time we did X, this time it's Y' — present the rotation as a fact, not as compensation owed. Avoid 'you didn't get your way last time, so…' framing entirely. (4) **For the loser** (the member whose top choice didn't win): acknowledge once, briefly, without grovelling — 'we know sushi was your top — saving it for next time' is acceptable; 'sorry your choice lost, but…' is not. (5) **Never display win-counts** ('Alex: 3 picks, you: 1 pick'); this surfaces the equity ledger and damages the communal frame even if the numbers favor the reader. (6) **One sentence per rule layer**: one line on the need filter (if active), one line on the selection rule. More than that and the screen starts arguing for itself, which signals defensiveness. (7) **Cross-cultural hedge**: copy should default to solidarity framing globally; collectivist users will accept it with no friction, individualist users will accept it as 'casual / friend-appropriate' — both work. The reverse (equity copy for collectivists) does not.

**ux_mechanic_implications**: (1) **Hard-constraint capture step**: the quiz must collect hard vetoes (allergies, dietary, religious, hard budget cap) per member privately and persistently across sessions, so the need rule can run without re-asking and without surfacing the constraint to the group unless necessary. Members should be able to mark a constraint as private (filter happens silently) or shared (group sees 'gluten-free options only'). (2) **Need-filter pass before scoring**: the algorithm must apply need-based pruning *before* preference aggregation, and the verdict screen must be able to show 'X options ruled out for hard constraints' without naming who. (3) **Equality-across-sessions ledger that the user does not see**: the system can internally track who-got-their-pick and balance over time using rotation or score-handicapping, but this ledger is *internal only* — never surfaced as scorecards. (4) **No equity inputs**: do not ask 'who drove last time', 'who chose last time', 'who paid last time'. These questions invite equity reasoning that damages the communal frame; if the system needs to balance, it should do so silently from session metadata. (5) **Constraint-violation alarm**: if the algorithm somehow returns an option that violates a stored hard veto (data freshness failure, restaurant policy change), the verdict screen must catch and re-roll rather than letting the constrained member discover it on arrival — a need-rule miss at the venue is the worst failure mode in the construct. (6) **Constraint-edit at every session start**: constraints can change ('I'm doing keto this month'), so the prompt should briefly let each member confirm/edit before the verdict pass. (7) **Rule-display toggle**: a 'why this pick' tap-target on the verdict screen that expands to a one-paragraph rule explanation, hidden by default — most members will not need it, the loser sometimes will, and on-demand explanation is procedurally protective without making the rule loud.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: Failure cascades by severity. Worst case: the verdict serves an option that violates a stored hard constraint (vegan member sent to a steakhouse, celiac to a flour-heavy bakery). The constrained member cannot eat, follow-through collapses, the group either splits or relocates, and the trust frame is damaged for several future sessions. Second-worst case: equity framing surfaces in copy ('you got your way last time, so…'), converting a communal friend-group dynamic into a transactional one — members start tracking inputs the system invited them to track, the warmth of the group's decision-making erodes, and the product feels like a corporate scheduler. Third-worst case: silent equity in the algorithm (system rotates fairly but doesn't communicate it), producing wins that look arbitrary to losers — acceptance still happens but at lower rates, and a subset of users who *would* have accepted with one line of rule-context will instead disengage. Mildest failure: equality is run correctly but the rule is not made legible at all, producing modest losses on first-time users who haven't learned to trust the system yet. The product can survive all of these except the first, but the cumulative effect on follow-through is meaningful — even a 5-10% follow-through hit per failure mode compounds across the 2-3 sessions a new group plays before deciding whether to keep using GetToIt.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Must-have for verdict screen. Specifically: the NEED-rule veto pass is non-negotiable (hard-constraint capture + filter + visible-on-demand justification); the EQUALITY-across-sessions balancing is must-have but invisible; the suppression of EQUITY framings in copy is must-have. Failing any of these damages follow-through directly, which is the north-star metric. Rule legibility on the verdict screen itself can be a should-have if compressed for time, but the underlying mechanics cannot be deferred.

**dependency_on_other_constructs**: Strong interactions with the rest of the fairness stack. (a) **Substitutes partially for Voice_Effect**: when the rule is correct for the context, members do not need to have voiced an opinion to feel fairly treated — the right rule produces acceptance without process-control. But Voice covers the residual when the rule is contested or members' constraints have changed. (b) **Amplified by Informational_Justice (Bies & Moag, Greenberg, Colquitt)**: a brief account of which rule was applied turns silent fairness into legible fairness, which is especially valuable for losers. The combination of correct-rule + brief-explanation outperforms either alone. (c) **Conflicts with crude Equity formulations**: any feature that shows a 'wins ledger' is in direct opposition to this construct's recommendations for the friend frame; if a future product surface requires equity (workplace mode, expense-splitting), it must be a separate UI mode with explicit relational re-framing. (d) **Requires Public_Commitment_Ratification only for need-rule edge cases**: when a need filter dramatically narrows the option set and the constrained member would prefer not to feel singled out, a quick group ratification ('all good?') redistributes ownership of the constraint and prevents post-hoc grumbling. (e) **Amplifies Outcome_Favorability_Interaction**: Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 show fair process buffers unfavorable outcomes — analogously, fair *rule selection* buffers a member who lost on their preference, by reframing the loss as 'rule applied correctly' rather than 'I lost'. (f) **Tyler_Group_Value_Model overlap**: Tyler's relational signals (respect, neutrality, trust) are conveyed in part by which rule the system picks — choosing equality among friends *is* a relational respect signal, choosing equity among friends *is* a respect violation.

**interaction_with_p1_p2**: P1 (EBA + Satisficing) interactions: EBA's veto-first design *natively implements the NEED rule for hard constraints* — this is a tight, almost coincidental fit. Tversky 1972's Elimination-by-Aspects sequentially eliminates options on aspect-thresholds; if the first aspects evaluated are the group's hard constraints (allergies, dietary, budget), the algorithm executes Deutsch's need-rule veto layer as a structural property of the heuristic, not as a bolted-on filter. This means the P1 framework and the recommended distributive-justice mechanic are aligned at the algorithmic level, not just compatible. Satisficing then handles the equality layer downstream — among options that cleared the need pass, satisficing accepts any option above an aspiration threshold rather than maximizing, which suppresses the maximizer-style equity calculus that would re-introduce input-tracking. P2 dominant interactions: (a) **Misaligned_Preferences** is where this construct has its home turf — Arrow-style preference aggregation is fundamentally a question of which distribution rule the aggregation rule encodes, and score voting / approval voting / veto-then-score implement different blends of equity, equality, and need. The recommended GetToIt aggregation (private intensity scores + hard vetoes, then sum-minus-veto) executes need-then-equality, which is the rule-fit prescribed for this relational frame. So Misaligned_Preferences and Distributive_Justice are not separate problems — they are two views of the same problem, one structural (Arrow), one normative (Deutsch). (b) **Social_Friction**: equity framing in copy *amplifies* social friction by inviting members to surface inputs they were politely suppressing; equality framing *dampens* it by removing the input-comparison dimension entirely. Pick equality and friction drops. (c) **Fear_of_Regret_FOMO**: distributive justice does not directly address regret — but a verdict produced by a rule the loser perceives as legitimate makes regret easier to absorb (it was fair, not random or rigged), so the constructs are weakly complementary.

## Informational Justice / Accounts and Explanations

_Source: `Informational_Justice_Explanation.json`_

### Basic Info

**construct_name**: Informational Justice / Accounts and Explanations

**originators**: Robert J. Bies & Joseph F. Moag (1986, interactional justice as parent construct, four communication criteria — truthfulness, justification, respect, propriety); Jerald Greenberg (1990, 1993, split interactional into informational and interpersonal justice); Jason A. Colquitt (2001, four-factor structural validation isolating informational justice as a distinct dimension). Adjacent: Sim B. Sitkin & Robert J. Bies on social accounts (1992, 1993); Folger & Cropanzano (2001) fairness theory as accountability lens.

**origin_year**: 1986 (Bies & Moag interactional-justice paper); 1993 (Greenberg's split into informational vs. interpersonal); 2001 (Colquitt's four-factor validation of informational justice as a standalone dimension).

**discipline**: Organizational behavior / industrial-organizational psychology, with roots in social psychology of accounts (Scott & Lyman 1968 'accounts'), procedural-justice theory (Thibaut & Walker 1975; Leventhal 1980), and equity theory (Adams 1965). Downstream applications in HR communication, change management, customer-service recovery, legal-procedural perception, and (most recently) explainable AI / algorithmic decision-making.

**one_sentence_summary**: When an authority gives an adequate, truthful, timely, and sincere explanation of how and why a decision was made, recipients judge the decision as more procedurally fair, are less likely to retaliate, and are more likely to comply — even when the outcome itself goes against them.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: An informational account moves a decision along three psychological axes simultaneously. (1) Epistemic legitimacy: the explanation tells the recipient which inputs were used, which decision rule was applied, and why this option won, which converts the verdict from an arbitrary fiat into an instance of a stated rule. Folger & Cropanzano's (2001) fairness theory frames this as collapsing the 'would' counterfactual: once the recipient sees that any reasonable alternative procedure would have produced a similar result, blame on the agent dissolves. (2) Trust signal: a sincere account is interpreted as a relational gesture — the authority spent effort explaining itself, which is a costly signal of respect (Tyler & Lind 1992 relational model overlap). Colquitt et al.'s (2013) meta-analysis shows informational justice predicts trust in the decision-maker more strongly than distributive justice does. (3) Resentment defusal in the loser: when the recipient's preferred option lost, the explanation answers the implicit question 'why not mine?' by naming the input or constraint that ruled it out. Without that name, the loser fills in the blank with an attribution to bias, dictatorship, or being ignored, which is the proximate driver of post-decision retaliation, withdrawal, and (in the GetToIt context) low follow-through. Bies & Sitkin (1992) decompose the account itself into four sub-types — causal ('this happened because X'), ideological ('we did it because the rule says X'), referential ('compared to alternative Y, this is better/standard'), and penitential ('we're sorry for the cost to you') — each working a different lever. The sincerity moderator is critical: insincere or boilerplate accounts not only fail to repair fairness, they actively worsen perception below the no-explanation baseline (Greenberg 1990 employee-theft study showed a 'thorough and sensitive' explanation cut theft from a 15% pay-cut roughly in half, while a perfunctory explanation produced theft levels indistinguishable from no explanation). The mechanism therefore requires: (a) name the inputs, (b) name the rule, (c) name why this option won, (d) deliver it in a register the recipient reads as honest, not corporate.

**behavioral_signature**: Acceptance markers: recipient nods/says 'okay that makes sense' rather than 'wait, why?'; recipient repeats the explanation back to others ('we picked X because Y'), which is a strong signal the account has been internalized as the decision's legitimating story; recipient defends the verdict to absent group members rather than relitigating it; reduced post-decision questioning, reduced 'are we sure?' messages, faster commit to logistics. Rejection markers (when explanation is missing or feels insincere): recipient asks 'why X and not Y?' meta-questions, demands a re-vote, drops out of follow-through ('you guys go without me'), surfaces grievance to a third party ('they always pick what Alice wants'), or — most diagnostic — re-interprets the verdict as evidence of an out-group ('the algorithm is biased toward expensive places'). The classic experimental signature is the explanation-mitigates-outcome pattern: when subjects receive an unfavorable outcome with no explanation, satisfaction and cooperation drop sharply; when the same unfavorable outcome is paired with an adequate explanation, satisfaction recovers most of the way, often to within 10-20% of the favorable-outcome baseline (Bies 1987; Shaw, Wild & Colquitt 2003 meta-analysis).

**typical_triggers**: Effect strongest when (a) the outcome is unfavorable to the recipient — explanations matter most for losers, much less for winners (this is the procedural × outcome interaction Brockner documents and Shaw et al. 2003 confirm meta-analytically); (b) the recipient had voice/input into the process and now wants to know whether that input was actually used (informational justice is the close-the-loop signal on voice); (c) the decision rule is non-obvious or potentially contested, so a naked verdict reads as arbitrary; (d) the relationship between decider and recipient is ongoing (repeated interaction raises the value of trust repair); (e) the recipient cares about the procedure as a precedent for future decisions in the same group; (f) stakes are mid-range — at very low stakes recipients don't bother to evaluate, at very high stakes outcome dominates. Effect is muted when the outcome is favorable, when the rule is publicly known and obvious, or when the recipient has a low-trust prior on the decider that the account cannot overcome in one shot.

### Evidence Base

**replication_status**: Robust and durable. The four-factor justice model (Colquitt 2001) has been validated in dozens of cross-cultural samples (Argentina, Spain, France, China, India, public-health, manufacturing, students). Shaw, Wild & Colquitt 2003 is the canonical meta-analysis on explanations and has not been overturned; the 2013 Colquitt et al. decade-later update extended it with consistent direction and similar effect magnitudes. The mechanism is one of the few in the post-2010 organizational-behavior literature that survived the replication crisis largely intact, plausibly because (a) effect sizes are moderate-to-large and (b) the construct is operationalized concretely (Colquitt's 5-item informational-justice subscale is short and reliable, alpha typically .85+). Failure modes in the literature are not failures of the underlying effect but failures of poorly delivered explanations: insincere accounts, post-hoc accounts, or accounts that name the wrong rule do not just have null effect — they actively harm fairness perception, an asymmetry that Colquitt et al. (2015) 'Adding the In to Justice' formalize as the difference between justice-rule adherence (positive effect) and justice-rule violation (larger negative effect, distinct construct).

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: Very strong fit. The GetToIt verdict screen is structurally an informational-justice surface: it is the moment when an algorithmic agent (the app) communicates a binding decision to a small group, including the member whose preferred option lost. Every reason the literature has identified for explanations to matter compounds in this context: (a) the verdict is potentially unfavorable to at least one member by construction (single committed verdict, multiple input preferences), and the literature is unanimous that explanations matter most for losers; (b) the decision rule (EBA + satisficing hybrid) is non-obvious to users and could be perceived as arbitrary without exposition; (c) the relationship is ongoing (the same friend group will use the product again, so trust repair has compounding value); (d) members had voice in the quiz and now want to verify their input was actually used — informational justice is the literal close-the-loop on voice; (e) the loser's resentment is the proximate threat to the north-star follow-through metric (a reluctant member arrives late, complains, or bails). The construct also has unusually clean structural alignment with the chosen P1 framework — EBA's sequential filter naturally produces an informational account by construction ('we eliminated X because of constraint A, then Y because of constraint B, leaving Z'), so the fit is not a retrofit, it is intrinsic to the decision rule. The main caveat: most of the empirical base is organizational/hierarchical, not symmetric peer-group, so magnitudes should be discounted somewhat, and the optimal account-type for friends-deciding-dinner is not directly validated.

**dominance_estimate**: High — likely top-2 or top-3 fairness construct for the verdict screen specifically, alongside Voice Effect (which informational justice closes the loop on) and Outcome-Favorability Interaction (which informational justice is the principal moderator of). Rationale: (1) it is the only fairness construct that is *enacted by the verdict screen itself* rather than by upstream quiz mechanics — voice happens during the quiz, distributive rule is set by the algorithm, but the explanation is something the verdict UI either does or fails to do, so it is the construct most directly under verdict-screen control; (2) the dominant P2 cause Misaligned_Preferences inherently produces a 'loser' on every verdict, and the loser is precisely the case where informational justice has the largest effect; (3) the loss of follow-through (north-star metric) is the exact behavioral outcome — withdrawal, sandbagging, retaliation — that the explanation literature shows is most reduced by adequate accounts. Discount: in a friend group with high prior trust, members may extend the algorithm benefit-of-the-doubt and need less explanation than the literature's strangers/employees do, so the effect could be smaller in absolute terms but still dominant relative to other constructs because the alternatives (formal voice, distributive rule selection, public ratification) all bottleneck through the explanation surface anyway.

**group_size_specificity**: Effect should be amplified rather than muted in 2-6 person friend groups, with one structural caveat. Amplification reasons: (a) at small group size each member's individual reaction matters disproportionately to follow-through (one bailer in a group of 4 is 25% defection, vs. one bailer in a group of 40 is 2.5%), so the marginal value of defusing each member's resentment is high; (b) small groups have lower procedural infrastructure (no formal vote, no documented rule), so the explanation IS the procedure from the recipient's perspective; (c) repeated-interaction value is highest at small group size where the same friends decide together weekly. Caveat: at very small group size (n=2) the loser is identifiable to everyone immediately, so a generic explanation may feel patronizing — naming inputs that everyone already knows is wasted. The optimal account length probably scales sub-linearly with group size: short and rule-naming for n=2-3, slightly more elaborated for n=4-6.

### Verdict-Screen Implication

**design_signal_to_express**: The verdict screen must enact, in a single moment, four signals: (1) the decision RULE used ('we picked the spot that everyone could eat at, that fit the budget cap, and that the most people had as a top pick'), so the verdict reads as an instance of a stated procedure rather than a fiat; (2) the INPUTS that were considered ('budget under $30, Maya is vegetarian, no one had been there in the last month'), so each member sees their own input was actually used — this is what closes the loop on Voice Effect and converts informational justice into voice-confirmation; (3) the WHY-THIS-WON statement ('Tacos El Sol cleared all four constraints and was Sam and Jordan's top pick'), so the loser sees a positive case for the winner rather than just an absence of objection to it; (4) the optional WHY-NOT statement (which alternatives were eliminated and on which constraint), which is the riskiest signal because it can name losers but is also the most defusing of resentment when done right. The construct does NOT require the screen to expose individual quiz answers by name — it requires the screen to expose that constraints were applied. The friend-group specific design choice is whether to identify the source of each veto (Alice → Mexican) or whether to anonymize ('one person had a hard no on Mexican'). Default to anonymize unless the source is non-judgmental (allergies, dietary restrictions, recent visit) and the recipient member opted in to attribution.

**copy_framework_implications**: Concrete copy patterns the verdict screen must support: (a) Name the inputs in plain language: 'Here's what the quiz heard: 4 of you said under $30, Maya can't do meat, you're all walking distance from Mission Street.' (b) Name the rule, not the algorithm: 'We picked the place that worked for all four of those.' Avoid 'our algorithm computed a weighted utility score' — that is informational justice in form but interpersonal-justice failure in tone, and reads as insincere/corporate, which the Greenberg 1990 finding warns triggers backfire. (c) Lead with the positive case for the winner: 'Tacos El Sol — vegetarian-friendly, ~$22/head, 6 min walk, top pick for Sam and Jordan.' Recipients process the affirming case before the negating case. (d) For eliminated options, prefer aggregate-level naming over personal-level naming: 'Sushi Garden — out because someone had a budget cap below $40' is safer than 'Sushi Garden — out because Alex said too expensive.' This protects the loser from being publicly identified as a constraint-imposer, which preserves friendship-level trust at the cost of slightly less procedural precision. (e) Use first-person plural ('we', 'you all'), not third-person ('the system'), to maintain peer-group voice. (f) Include a brief penitential/empathic acknowledgment when the verdict is potentially disappointing to a specific member — 'Maya, your top pick (Sushi Garden) didn't fit the budget tonight; it scored second' — which is the penitential-account move from Bies' typology and is the single most defusing copy element for a known loser. (g) Avoid hedging ('we tried our best', 'this is just a suggestion') — Colquitt et al. (2015) findings on rule-violation asymmetry show that uncertain language reads as the absence of a rule, not the humble application of one, and reduces perceived informational justice.

**ux_mechanic_implications**: (1) Default verdict screen surfaces three sections in priority order: WINNER + 1-line affirming case → RULE used (one short sentence) → INPUTS that drove it (collapsible bulleted list of the constraints that fired, anonymized by default). Eliminated options shown only if user expands. (2) 'See why' or 'Why this one?' affordance — the explanation must be available but not demanded, because forced consumption of the rationale reads as self-justifying (the literature on apologies finds the same: short and on-demand beats long and proactive). (3) Per-member 'your input was used' confirmation — small text near the verdict telling each viewer 'you said: under $30, walking distance, no Indian — all three applied' — closes the voice loop privately for each user without exposing other members' inputs. (4) Loser-aware copy slot — the screen knows whose top pick lost (each member's #1 was captured in the quiz) and can render a one-line acknowledgment to that specific member only, not broadcast to the group. This is the most product-leveraged informational-justice mechanic and has no academic precedent because the literature did not have personalized rendering. (5) Correctability path — Leventhal's correctability rule and Colquitt's informational-justice items both include 'were the explanations reasonable' and implicitly 'could you contest them'; a small 'something missing? add a constraint' affordance preserves correctability without inviting full re-litigation. (6) Avoid showing the algorithm's internal scoring numbers — quantitative weights read as false precision and undermine sincerity. Show binary applied/not-applied for each constraint instead. (7) Optional shared-attribution toggle — for groups that opt in, allow members to choose whether their constraints are named to the group or kept anonymous; this respects the friend-group privacy boundary the literature does not address.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the verdict screen returns only the winner with no account: the loser fills the explanation gap with attribution to bias, randomness, or the algorithm favoring someone else, which is exactly the retaliation-driver that the Shaw, Wild & Colquitt 2003 meta-analysis identified — without an explanation, retaliation/withdrawal rates roughly double in the lab analog. For GetToIt this maps to the loser arriving late, complaining about the choice during the meal, sandbagging the experience, or skipping next week's group decision entirely (the cumulative version is friend-group churn off the product). A more subtle failure: if the verdict screen DOES give an explanation but it is generic, formulaic, or visibly machine-generated ('our recommendation engine determined that this option provided the highest aggregate utility'), the Greenberg 1990 sincerity-moderator finding kicks in and perceived fairness drops below the no-explanation baseline, which is the worst possible outcome — the product is paying for a feature that actively harms the metric. Third failure mode: if the explanation names individuals as constraint sources without their consent ('eliminated because Alex said no'), it converts a private friend-group decision into an exposure event, which damages the friendship layer that the product is built on top of and is plausibly worse for retention than no explanation at all.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Must-have for verdict screen. Rationale: it is the construct most directly enacted by the verdict surface itself, the strongest defuse for the loser-resentment that threatens follow-through, and structurally free with the EBA P1 (the elimination chain produces the explanation as a byproduct). Skipping it is the highest-EV mistake the product could make on the verdict screen.

**dependency_on_other_constructs**: Strong dependencies in both directions. (a) Amplifies Voice Effect — informational justice closes the loop on voice; if voice is given in the quiz but not confirmed in the verdict, the voice was decorative, and Tyler-Lind relational research indicates decorative voice is worse than no voice. The two constructs are best thought of as a pair: voice (input) → explanation (confirmation). (b) Substitutes partially for Outcome-Favorability buffering — Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996's fair-process effect is operationally driven in large part by informational justice, so a strong explanation does double duty as procedural-justice signal and as outcome-favorability buffer for the loser. (c) Conflicts with Distributive Equity if mishandled — naming the rule that produced the verdict can expose that the rule was 'whoever's top pick had the most votes' (a plurality rule), which can read as unfair under Adams-equity intuition; better to use rules that explain well (constraint satisfaction, satisficing thresholds). (d) Requires Leventhal's consistency and bias-suppression rules — an explanation only works if the rule it describes is actually one the group endorses; explaining a biased rule transparently does not repair it. (e) Reinforces Public Commitment / Ratification — once an explanation is on the screen and visible to all members, the commitment to the verdict is socially anchored to the explanation, which makes subsequent renegotiation harder (Cialdini consistency mechanism) — informational justice and ratification compound.

**interaction_with_p1_p2**: The fit with P1 (EBA + Satisficing hybrid) is unusually clean — it is the strongest case in this research package for structural compatibility between framework and fairness construct. EBA's sequential filter produces a natural informational account by construction: at each step the algorithm has eliminated options on a named aspect, so a verdict-screen explanation is just a readable rendering of the elimination trace ('we cut Sushi Garden for budget, Pizza Place for distance, Indian Wala for recent visit, leaving Tacos El Sol'). The satisficing layer adds the affirmative case on the survivor (it cleared everyone's threshold). This is why informational justice should be elevated to first-rank verdict-screen construct in this product specifically — the explanation is not a layer the team has to build on top of the algorithm, it is the algorithm's own state surfaced. With P2 dominant causes: (1) Misaligned_Preferences — informational justice is the principal defuse for the loser created by every aggregation pass; without it, the loser interprets misalignment as bias, with it, the loser interprets misalignment as honest constraint-resolution. (2) Social_Friction — this is the one place where informational justice can either help or hurt depending on copy: a personal-causal account ('eliminated because Alex said no to Mexican') exposes intra-group friction and worsens it; an aggregate-rule account ('eliminated because the budget constraint cut it') defuses it. The product-design lever here is anonymization-by-default. (3) Fear_of_Regret_FOMO — explanations reduce regret partly by replacing the counterfactual 'maybe we should have picked X' with 'X was eliminated for reason R, which I agree with', collapsing the would-counterfactual that Folger & Cropanzano identify as the engine of perceived unfairness. Net interaction: informational justice amplifies P1's strengths and is the strongest single defuser of all three P2 dominant causes, with the explicit caveat that copy must use aggregate/rule-level attribution rather than personal attribution to avoid backfire on Social_Friction.

## Leventhal's Six Procedural-Justice Rules (consistency, bias suppression, accuracy, correctability, representativeness, ethicality)

_Source: `Leventhal_Six_Rules.json`_

### Basic Info

**construct_name**: Leventhal's Six Procedural-Justice Rules (consistency, bias suppression, accuracy, correctability, representativeness, ethicality)

**originators**: Gerald S. Leventhal (Wayne State University); building on Thibaut & Walker (1975) but generalizing from legal-dispute settings to all allocation procedures.

**origin_year**: 1980 (the rules; the underlying framework was sketched earlier in Leventhal 1976 'Fairness in social relationships' and Leventhal, Karuza & Fry 1980 'Beyond fairness: A theory of allocation preferences').

**discipline**: Social psychology / social exchange theory; later canonized in organizational behavior (organizational justice) and adapted into legal psychology, public-policy procedural-fairness audits, and HCI/algorithmic-fairness work.

**one_sentence_summary**: A procedure is perceived as fair to the extent it satisfies six rules — applied consistently across people and time, suppresses the decider's self-interest and prejudice, uses accurate information, is correctable when it errs, takes the views of all affected parties into account, and aligns with prevailing moral/ethical standards.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Leventhal models the perceiver as running an implicit audit on the procedure that produced an outcome. Inputs to the audit are the seven structural components of an allocation procedure (selection of decision agents, setting of ground rules, gathering of information, decision structure, appeals, safeguards, change mechanisms). The perceiver scans each component against the six normative rules. Each rule is a dimension on which the procedure earns or loses fairness credit; a procedure is judged 'fair' to the degree the dimensions average out positively, with strong violations weighing heavier (a single grossly biased decider can sink an otherwise-clean process). The fairness judgment then drives downstream behavior via two pathways: (1) instrumental — fair procedures over time produce fair outcomes, so accepting them is rational; (2) relational/group-value (Lind & Tyler 1988, Tyler & Blader 2003) — fair procedures signal respect, status, and trustworthy authority, so accepting them affirms group membership. In GetToIt terms: a member who lost their preferred restaurant runs an automatic audit on the verdict mechanism. If the audit clears (same rule applied to everyone, no member's vote secretly weighted, quiz inputs honored, retry path exists, every member voiced, no manipulative tactics) they accept the loss and follow through. If a rule is conspicuously violated (e.g., the loud member's preference dominated, or the algorithm 'felt rigged') they retract commitment, vent, or sandbag the meal.

**behavioral_signature**: Acceptance signature when rules are met: members ratify the verdict quickly, do not relitigate after the screen, show up on time, do not post-hoc complain about the process. Rejection signature when rules are violated: (a) consistency breach -> 'why did you pick differently last time, this is unfair' or 'you always do this'; (b) bias-suppression breach -> accusations of favoritism ('the app picked Alice's choice because she opened it'); (c) accuracy breach -> 'but I said I was vegetarian and it picked steakhouse'; (d) correctability breach -> sullen acceptance because 'we already locked it' coupled with refusal to use the product again; (e) representativeness breach -> the member who didn't get a quiz prompt or whose answer didn't visibly land complains they were ignored; (f) ethicality breach -> the verdict feels coerced ('this is creepy, I don't want to be guilt-tripped into committing'). Diagnostic tell: complaints target the *process* not the *restaurant*. When members say 'I'd have been fine with X but the way we picked it was BS' you have a procedural-justice failure rather than a misaligned-preferences failure.

**typical_triggers**: Effect strongest when: (1) outcome is unfavorable to the perceiver — the loser of the verdict runs the audit hardest (Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 fair-process effect, k=45 samples, interaction confirmed); (2) decider is an out-group authority or untrusted agent — but in friend groups the algorithm itself becomes the audited authority; (3) stakes are non-trivial enough to care about (mid-stakes social commitments qualify); (4) repeated interaction — repeated rule violations erode trust faster than one-off violations; (5) prior comparisons exist — when members can compare the current procedure to a prior session, consistency violations become salient; (6) moral or identity-relevant content — dietary, religious, or value-laden food preferences raise the stakes on accuracy and ethicality.

### Evidence Base

**replication_status**: Robust. The Colquitt 2001 scale is the most-cited procedural-justice instrument and has been validated in dozens of cross-cultural samples (Argentine workers — Patlan-Perez et al.; Norwegian public health — Enoksen 2015; meta-analytic Reithel et al. 2007 on cultural moderators). Colquitt et al. 2013 is a deliberate decade-later replication of Colquitt et al. 2001 and confirms the main effects with social-exchange and affect-based mediation added. The Brockner-Wiesenfeld interaction has been replicated repeatedly (Brockner 2002, regulatory-focus extensions, etc.) though the interaction's strength varies with trust in authority (Brockner & Bianchi). The general finding that procedural fairness predicts acceptance independent of outcome is one of the better-replicated effects in social/organizational psychology, and unlike ego depletion or some priming effects it survived the post-2010 replication-crisis scrutiny intact. Boundary: most replications are in workplace/legal/policing contexts; small informal friend-group replication is sparse.

**known_limitations**: (1) The six rules were posited theoretically, not derived empirically; factor analyses of Leventhal-derived items typically collapse them into one or two factors rather than recovering six distinct dimensions (Colquitt 2001 treats procedural justice as unidimensional in scale form). (2) The framework was built for organizational/legal allocation; informal friend-group decisions may activate a different mix of rules (representativeness and ethicality may dominate; consistency is hard to assess on first use). (3) Cultural moderators: Hofstede-style power-distance moderates voice-effects (Brockner et al.); individualist cultures weight some rules differently than collectivist cultures, though the rules themselves appear universal in qualitative form. (4) The framework predicts perceptions, not behavior; the gap from 'perceived fair' to 'showed up at the restaurant' is mediated by commitment, identity, and exit options. (5) Correctability is in tension with other commitment-inducing mechanics (public ratification, hard close) — a procedure that is too easily reversible undermines the lock-in that drives follow-through. This is the central design tension for GetToIt. (6) Algorithmic deciders raise rules the original framework didn't address (explainability, opacity, training-data bias) — see Lee 2018 and the algorithmic-fairness literature for adaptations.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: High structural fit, moderate priority. The GetToIt verdict screen is exactly the kind of single-decision artifact Leventhal's audit is run against. Friends are flat-power, so no human authority absorbs the legitimacy load — the algorithm and the verdict screen jointly *are* the procedure being audited. Each rule maps cleanly onto a verdict-screen decision: consistency = same algorithm and same surface across sessions; bias suppression = no member's vote secretly weighted higher (no 'host advantage', no recency bias on who opened the app); accuracy = the inputs the quiz captured are visibly the inputs that drove the result; correctability = a defined path to redo or escalate without nuking the session; representativeness = every member's voice visibly entered the calculation; ethicality = no dark patterns, no shame-based commitment pressure, no manipulative scarcity. Caveat: friend groups have high prior trust and short interaction stakes, so rule violations forgive faster than in workplace/legal settings. A single mild violation is unlikely to torch a session; repeated violations across sessions or one egregious violation will. The construct's main contribution is as a *checklist* — it lets the verdict-screen designer audit their own surface before shipping.

**dominance_estimate**: Medium for v1. Rationale: P1 (EBA + Satisficing hybrid) and P2 (Misaligned_Preferences, Social_Friction, Fear_of_Regret) capture the *why decisions stall* problem; Leventhal captures the *why a stalled-then-resolved decision fails to convert into follow-through* problem. The construct is downstream of the core paralysis fix — it only matters once a verdict has been produced. But conditional on producing a verdict, it is plausibly the dominant determinant of the loser's follow-through (Brockner-Wiesenfeld: procedural justice matters most when outcome is unfavorable, which is exactly the loser's situation). Net: medium dominance overall, high dominance for the specific subgroup whose preference lost. Higher than Distributive_Justice_Equity (because food has no clear contribution metric to distribute by) and probably comparable to Voice_Effect and Informational_Justice — which are themselves derivatives of the Leventhal framework.

**group_size_specificity**: Largely unchanged across 2-6 person groups. The audit is run individually by each member, so group size mostly affects how many auditors are watching, not the audit itself. Subtle moderators: in 2-person groups, representativeness is trivially satisfied (both voices visibly counted) so that rule's marginal contribution is low; in 5-6 person groups, representativeness and bias suppression become harder to demonstrate and their marginal contribution rises; consistency across time (rule 1) becomes more important as group size grows because heterogeneous groups have more comparison points. Friend groups stay flat-authority across the 2-6 range, so no rule is dropped due to hierarchy. Key inflection: the 'who lost?' surface is empty in 2-person groups (you can't anonymize the loser) and becomes meaningful only at 3+; bias-suppression evidence carries more weight at 3+.

**ingroup_friend_specificity**: Friendship raises the floor and lowers the ceiling. High prior trust means small rule violations get forgiven (friends extend procedural benefit-of-the-doubt that strangers don't), so the construct's *negative* effect on follow-through is muted at the margin. However, the same trust raises the ceiling on betrayal cost: a procedure that feels rigged among strangers is annoying; among close friends it feels personal — 'the app played favorites' becomes 'you played favorites'. Repeated interaction across sessions is the GetToIt default, which makes consistency (rule 1) more diagnostic than in single-shot stranger encounters: friends will notice if the algorithm reaches different verdicts from similar inputs. Net effect: friend-group specificity makes the construct *less* important for any single session, *more* important for retention across sessions.

### Verdict-Screen Implication

**design_signal_to_express**: The verdict screen must legibly satisfy each rule with at most one element per rule, ordered by per-session salience: (1) Accuracy — surface the actual inputs each member contributed and show how they entered the calculation ('you said: low-key, near home, no sushi -> we filtered down to 4'); (2) Representativeness — show that every member's input was counted ('5/5 quizzes received'); (3) Bias suppression — show the rule, not the result, and demonstrate it would produce the same answer regardless of who opened the app or whose turn it is; (4) Consistency — name the algorithm by a stable handle ('Top-pick rule: highest sum of yes-scores minus any veto'); (5) Correctability — provide an explicit but friction-bearing redo path (not a free re-roll, see ux_mechanic_implications) so the procedure is reversible-in-principle without being reversible-in-practice on a whim; (6) Ethicality — no dark patterns, no fake scarcity, no shame-based copy ('your friends are waiting' is a violation), no manipulative defaults.

**copy_framework_implications**: Concrete patterns: (a) 'Name the inputs' line — list what the quiz captured per member, anonymized if needed but visibly complete ('5 of you weighed in: cravings, distance, vetoes'); (b) 'Name the rule' line — one sentence describing the algorithm in plain language ('We picked the spot with the highest combined score that nobody vetoed'); (c) 'Name the loser, gently' — when a member's top pick didn't win, acknowledge it without re-litigating ('Sushi was close — 4 yes, but Sam vetoed raw fish tonight'); (d) Avoid passive voice that hides the algorithm ('a decision was made') — use active, mechanical voice ('the app picked Tacos El Sol because...'); (e) Avoid moralizing copy ('be a good friend and commit') — that violates ethicality; (f) Avoid anything that implies one member 'won' over another (status framing) — frame as 'the group chose X' not 'Alice's pick won'; (g) For correctability copy, prefer 'you can redo this' over 'you cannot change this' — the *option* signals fairness even if it's rarely exercised; (h) For consistency, use the same verdict-screen template every session and refer to the rule by the same name ('Top-pick rule') across sessions so members can verify the audit holds.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the verdict screen ignores Leventhal: the loser of the verdict accepts grudgingly, shows up late, complains en route, vetoes future sessions, or drops the product. Worse, repeated procedural opacity ('the app just picks something') accumulates as distrust of the algorithm — members start lobbying outside the app ('let's just text', 'app's bad tonight'), which kills the product's core value. The Brockner-Wiesenfeld interaction predicts the failure is concentrated in the loser subgroup: members who got their preference will follow through regardless of process quality, but the loser will only follow through if the process feels fair. Since at least one loser exists in every multi-option verdict, every session has a fairness audit happening — the cost of ignoring it scales linearly with usage.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Should-have. Rationale: the construct is a checklist for the verdict-screen surface rather than a feature in itself — most rules are cheap to satisfy if designed in, expensive to retrofit. Three of the six rules (accuracy, representativeness, bias suppression) are essentially free given a competent quiz->verdict pipeline. Two (consistency, ethicality) are pure design discipline. One (correctability) creates a real tradeoff with hard-close commitment and needs an explicit decision (recommended: friction-bearing quorum redo). Skip in v1 only if shipping speed forces it and the verdict screen is a placeholder; otherwise must-have.

**dependency_on_other_constructs**: Substitutes for / overlaps with: Voice_Effect (representativeness rule is voice rebranded); Informational_Justice (ethicality + accuracy rules overlap heavily with informational justice's adequate-explanation requirement — informational justice is essentially a sharpened subset of Leventhal); Tyler_Group_Value_Model (relational pathway from fair-procedure -> acceptance is the same mechanism, Tyler's neutrality/respect/trust map onto bias-suppression/ethicality/consistency). Amplifies: Public_Commitment_Ratification (a procedurally fair verdict is a more legitimate target for ratification — the public lock-in works because the procedure earned it). Conflicts with: Public_Commitment_Ratification (correctability rule directly tensions hard-close lock-in — see interaction_with_p1_p2). Required by: any verdict-screen design that uses an algorithmic decider — without procedural fairness signaling, the algorithm reads as a black box and acceptance collapses for the loser.

**interaction_with_p1_p2**: With P1 (EBA + Satisficing hybrid): EBA's elimination steps are themselves a procedure that can be audited against Leventhal — accuracy (each member's vetoes were applied), bias suppression (vetoes weighted equally), representativeness (every member contributed criteria). Satisficing's threshold cutoff also benefits from procedural transparency (name the threshold, show it cleared). The P1 mechanic is *very* legible to a Leventhal audit and largely passes by construction. With P2 dominant causes: defuses Misaligned_Preferences's procedural-fairness aftermath — when the loser's preference loses, procedural fairness is what converts the loss into commitment rather than resentment. Defuses Social_Friction by making the procedure (not the loud member) the visible decider, removing the social cost of advocating. Partially defuses Fear_of_Regret by reducing post-decision second-guessing — a fair procedure short-circuits the 'we should have picked X' rumination because the procedure, not the chooser, gets credit/blame. CRITICAL TENSION with P2 synthesis 'hard close / no re-open' UX prescription: Leventhal's correctability rule says the procedure must be reversible-in-principle, but the follow-through metric rewards lock-in that makes the verdict reversible-in-practice. The resolution is that the two requirements are not actually contradictory if friction is dialed correctly: a procedure that requires (e.g.) 50%+ quorum to redo is correctable enough to clear the audit (no rigged feel, no trapped feel) while still costly enough that idle second-guessing does not unwind the verdict. The 'hard close' framing should therefore be 'hard but not sealed' — the close is firm by default, opens on collective dissent, never opens on individual second-guess. If hard-close is interpreted as truly irreversible (no path back), Leventhal predicts loser-side follow-through suffers; if correctability is interpreted as cheap re-rolls, Public_Commitment_Ratification predicts overall follow-through suffers. The friction-bearing quorum-redo design splits the difference.

## Outcome Favorability x Procedural Justice Interaction (the Fair-Process Effect)

_Source: `Outcome_Favorability_Interaction.json`_

### Basic Info

**construct_name**: Outcome Favorability x Procedural Justice Interaction (the Fair-Process Effect)

**originators**: Joel Brockner and Batia M. Wiesenfeld (1996 meta-analytic synthesis); Brockner (2002) extension. Theoretical antecedents in Folger's referent cognitions theory (1986), Thibaut & Walker (1975) process-control work, and Lind's group-value model (1992).

**origin_year**: 1996

**discipline**: Organizational behavior / social psychology of justice. The construct lives at the intersection of distributive justice (Adams 1965) and procedural justice (Thibaut & Walker 1975; Leventhal 1980), and was canonized as a meta-analytic finding rather than a single-experiment construct.

**one_sentence_summary**: Procedural justice and outcome favorability combine multiplicatively rather than additively: procedural justice matters MOST when the outcome is unfavorable to the perceiver, because a fair process buffers the loss and prevents blame, exit, and retaliation; when outcomes are favorable, fairness perception is high regardless of process.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: When a person receives a favorable outcome, their fairness judgment and behavioral acceptance are saturated — they have little reason to scrutinize how the decision was reached, and procedural variation barely moves the needle. When the outcome is unfavorable, motivational and cognitive systems activate: the perceiver searches for an attribution. Procedural justice (voice, neutrality, consistency, explanation, correctability) supplies a non-blame attribution — 'the system was legitimate, the loss was the luck of a fair draw' — which preserves perceived control, self-esteem, and the relational standing of the decision-maker. Without procedural justice, the unfavorable outcome gets attributed to bias, malice, or disrespect from the decider, triggering frustration-aggression dynamics: anger, blame, exit, retaliation, and refusal to comply. The interaction is therefore not symmetric but takes the form of a crossover or fan-shape: the slope of the fairness-on-outcome line is steep under low procedural justice and flat under high procedural justice. Brockner's 2002 AMR refinement adds an attribution moderator: when the dependent variable is system-directed (commitment, support for the decider, acceptance), high procedural fairness *reduces* the effect of outcome favorability (the classic buffer). When the dependent variable is self-directed (self-esteem, self-blame), high procedural fairness *heightens* the effect of outcome favorability, because a fair process implies the loss was not the system's fault and therefore reflects on the self.

**behavioral_signature**: Activation: a member who lost on their preference still talks positively about the decision, defends it to others, follows through on the committed action (shows up, eats the food, pays their share), does not bring up the loss in subsequent decisions, and recommits to future group decisions. The loser may verbally acknowledge the loss ('I wanted Thai but pizza is fine') without resentment. Absence (failure mode): the loser exits silently, ghosts on the meet-up, free-rides on others' enthusiasm without committing, sandbags ('whatever, I don't care'), starts a parallel sub-decision ('what if we just go to two places'), brings up the loss as a grievance in the next decision ('we did your thing last time'), or escalates to retaliation (refuses to participate in the next round). In aggregate group data, the gap between 'majority agreed' and 'whole group showed up' is the behavioral signature of fair-process effect strength: a wide gap = process is failing the loser; narrow gap = the construct is working.

**typical_triggers**: Effect is strongest when: (1) the outcome is materially or symbolically unfavorable to the perceiver (their preference lost, they got the worse share, the rule went against them); (2) stakes feel non-trivial — though notably the effect appears at low stakes too as long as the loss is psychologically real; (3) the perceiver is identified with the group or relationship (Tyler & Lind relational concerns are activated, so process signals matter more); (4) the procedure is observable and auditable to the perceiver — they can actually see whether voice, neutrality, and consistency obtained; (5) outcome and process information arrive close in time so the buffering can occur in the same judgment moment; (6) the perceiver has an interdependent self-construal (Brockner et al. 2000, 2005). Effect attenuates or inverts when: outcome touches a moral mandate (Skitka & Mullen 2002 — moral conviction overrides procedural concerns), trust in the authority is already very high or very low (Brockner & Bianchi — trust dictates the form of the interaction), or the perceiver has a strongly independent self-construal that reads fair process as evidence of personal responsibility for the loss.

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: The canonical meta-analysis is Brockner & Wiesenfeld (1996, Psychological Bulletin, 120(2), 189-208), synthesizing 45 independent samples across organizational, legal, educational, and laboratory contexts. The headline finding: a robust, statistically reliable interaction between procedural justice level and outcome favorability/fairness on a wide family of reactions (organizational commitment, trust, support for authorities, intent to comply, satisfaction, withdrawal, retaliation). Pattern: the simple correlation between outcome favorability and reaction is large under low procedural justice and substantially smaller under high procedural justice; symmetrically, the correlation between procedural justice and reaction is larger under unfavorable outcomes. Brockner & Wiesenfeld report the interaction as a moderator-of-correlations pattern (differences in r across high vs. low procedural-justice subsets) rather than a single d, but the differences in correlation magnitude are typically in the r ~0.2-0.4 range across samples, and the interaction is significant in the large majority of the 45 samples. Subsequent work substantially extended this: Colquitt et al. (2001, JAP, 'Justice at the Millennium') established the four-factor justice model and reported large meta-analytic correlations of procedural justice with commitment (rho ~0.43) and trust (rho ~0.6); Colquitt et al. (2013, JAP, 'Justice at the Millennium, a Decade Later') replicated and extended these on hundreds of additional samples post-2000, reaffirming procedural justice's buffering role. Lind (2001) showed the fair-process effect mediates the move from arbitration outcome to award acceptance more strongly than the outcome itself. The fair-process effect has been demonstrated in legal (Tyler), tax-compliance (Murphy, Wenzel), layoff (Brockner), educational, medical, and policing contexts.

**replication_status**: Highly robust as a directional finding; one of the better-replicated effects in organizational psychology. Colquitt et al. 2013 'decade later' meta-analysis (Journal of Applied Psychology) confirmed and extended the 1996 pattern with substantially larger sample base and the social-exchange and affect-based theoretical framings. The interaction itself appears in dozens of post-2010 studies (Brockner & Bianchi on trust as a moderator of the *form* of the interaction; van den Bos uncertainty-management work; the regulatory-focus moderation by Brockner et al. 2007 OBHDP). Two important refinements have changed the picture since 1996: (1) Brockner (2002 AMR) showed the interaction reverses for self-evaluation outcomes (self-esteem, self-blame) — high procedural fairness *amplifies* the impact of an unfavorable outcome on the self, which is a non-trivial dark side; (2) the interaction is moderated by self-construal, regulatory focus, and trust in the authority. No major replication failures of the basic buffering effect, but Skitka & Mullen (2002, PSPB) and Mullen & Skitka (2006, JPSP) demonstrated a true boundary condition: when outcome touches a moral mandate, procedural fairness fails to buffer and is itself judged through the lens of whether the outcome aligned with the moral conviction. This is contested by Napier & Tyler (2008) but the boundary condition is now widely accepted.

**known_limitations**: (1) Moral-mandate boundary (Skitka & Mullen 2002, 2006): when the perceiver holds a strong moral conviction about the outcome, fair process does not buffer the loss; instead, the procedure is itself judged by whether it produced the morally-mandated result. For GetToIt, this means a vegan in a steakhouse-vs-vegan-bowl decision will not be procedurally bufferable — the moral identity overrides. (2) Self-evaluation reversal (Brockner 2002): under fair process, an unfavorable outcome bites harder for self-esteem because there's no system-blame escape route. Implication: a too-fair-feeling process can leave the loser feeling 'I just have bad taste / my friends don't like what I like'. (3) Cultural moderation (Brockner, Chen, Mannix, Leung & Skarlicki 2000, ASQ): the buffering effect is stronger under interdependent self-construal (collectivist cultures, relationship-oriented individuals) and weaker — sometimes reversed — under strong independent self-construal. (4) Trust-as-moderator (Brockner & Bianchi): under very high or very low pre-existing trust, procedural variation matters less; the buffer requires moderate baseline trust. (5) Procedural fairness can backfire if framed as performative or token (the 'sham voice' problem — voice without genuine consideration is worse than no voice). (6) The classic studies are largely organizational/legal; ecological validity for ephemeral peer-group decisions like dinner-tonight is plausible but underwritten. (7) Effect sizes for the interaction term are modest — the construct's value is robust direction, not large magnitude per study.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: Extraordinarily high fit. In every GetToIt decision among 2-6 friends, by mathematical necessity at least one member's preference loses (n>1 preferences, single committed verdict, almost always non-Pareto). The core threat to follow-through is the loser's silent disengagement: they nominally agreed in the chat, but they are the leak in the funnel between 'majority approved verdict' and 'whole group showed up and ate'. The fair-process effect is the single best-evidenced mechanism in social psychology for closing exactly that gap. The verdict screen is the moment of truth: it must perform procedural justice loudly enough that the loser walks away with a non-blame attribution ('the rule was fair, I had voice, my friends weren't biased, my preference just didn't win this time'). Without that, the loser attributes the loss to social dynamics ('they always pick what Sara wants'), which then poisons the next decision and cumulatively breaks group cohesion. Note the moral-mandate caveat: dietary restrictions (vegan, kosher, halal, allergy) are NOT defeasible by procedural justice — they must be encoded as hard constraints upstream of the verdict, never positioned as a preference that lost.

**dominance_estimate**: HIGH dominance for the follow-through metric specifically — arguably the single most important construct in this entire research priority for GetToIt's north star. Rationale: the metric is 'whole group commits and shows up', not 'majority agrees', so the loser is the rate-limiting step. Tyler's group-value model and Leventhal's six rules describe *what* procedural justice consists of; the outcome-favorability interaction is the construct that explains *why those features matter most precisely for the person whose follow-through is most at risk*. Voice effect (Thibaut & Walker) and informational justice (Bies/Greenberg/Colquitt) are the levers; this construct is the load-bearing causal claim that those levers move follow-through. A verdict screen that ignores this construct will achieve majority-agreement verdicts and still leak follow-through.

**group_size_specificity**: Effect is fully present in 2-6 person groups and likely amplified relative to large-group or hierarchical contexts. Reasoning: in a small friend group the loser is identifiable, identifies themselves, and is identifiable to others — there is nowhere to hide. In a 200-person organizational decision the average member's loss is statistically diluted; in a 4-person dinner the loser knows exactly what they wanted, knows the others know, and the social signal of acceptance-or-exit is unambiguous. Relational and group-value channels (Tyler) are also strongest in small in-groups, which means procedural-justice signals load more on relational standing and therefore on acceptance. One muting factor: in groups of 2 the dynamics shift toward dyadic negotiation rather than group decision, and the construct's full architecture (voice + neutrality + consistency across multiple parties) compresses. v1 should assume amplified effect for n=3-6, slightly attenuated for n=2.

**ingroup_friend_specificity**: Friend-group context changes the construct in two opposing directions. AMPLIFIES: friends have repeated interaction (the next decision is coming next weekend), share dense relational stakes, and process-fairness signals double as relational-respect signals (Tyler's group-engagement model overlaps directly with the buffering mechanism). High prior trust means moderate baseline trust — exactly where the interaction is strongest (Brockner & Bianchi). The cost of a bad attribution is much higher than with strangers because it persists. ATTENUATES: friends already have a fairness history, so a single unfair-feeling decision may be absorbed into the running average rather than triggering acute frustration-aggression. Also, friends often have informal alternation rules ('it's your pick this time') that pre-empt the interaction by making outcome unfavorability already accounted for. Net: the construct still applies, but the verdict screen does NOT need to over-explain to a degree that would feel weird among friends. The signal must be present and legible but lightweight — heavy procedural ceremony would itself violate friend-group norms.

### Verdict-Screen Implication

**design_signal_to_express**: The verdict screen must communicate, in the same moment as the result, that the procedure satisfied procedural justice for the LOSER specifically. Concretely it must: (1) make the rule visible — show the decision rule that produced this verdict (e.g., 'highest combined score among options nobody vetoed', 'random pick weighted by votes'), so the loser sees a neutral mechanism rather than a social outcome; (2) acknowledge the loss by name — show the loser's preference and its standing, signalling that their input was registered (voice); (3) give an account — a one-line, non-defensive explanation of why the verdict won, not why the alternatives lost; (4) preserve correctability — a short, time-boxed window in which the verdict can be questioned or vetoed before it locks (this is the 'correctability' rule from Leventhal); (5) ratify publicly — once the window closes, lock the verdict and make every member's commitment visible, which converts the loser's procedural acceptance into a public commitment. The screen must NOT pretend the loser won, must NOT obscure the rule, and must NOT make the loser feel singled out (no 'sorry, Sara' copy — the rule is the actor, not the friends).

**copy_framework_implications**: Concrete copy patterns derived from this construct: (1) 'Name the rule, not the people' — replace 'Sara, James, and Liam picked Thai' with 'Thai won — highest combined rating with no vetoes'. The rule is the agent. (2) 'Name the inputs' — show what each person wanted in a compact, non-shaming reveal so voice is visibly registered. (3) 'Name the loser's preference without naming the loser as a loser' — e.g., 'Pizza was 1 vote behind' rather than 'Maya wanted pizza but was outvoted'. (4) 'Account, don't apologize' — 'we go with the rule, not the loudest' beats 'sorry it didn't go your way'. (5) Avoid moralizing the outcome — never imply the winning option is objectively better; the rule produced this option, that is all. (6) Forward-frame — 'next decision starts fresh' or 'next pick alternates' explicitly, to prevent grievance carry-over. (7) Tone: neutral and procedural, not celebratory — celebration excludes the loser and signals that the group identifies with the winning preference, which inverts the buffer.

**ux_mechanic_implications**: (1) Public who-wanted-what reveal — a compact widget showing each member's input alongside the verdict, so voice is documented and the loser can see their preference was counted. (2) Visible rule chip — a single line stating which rule produced the verdict, tappable for detail. (3) Correctability window — a time-boxed (e.g., 30-90s) opportunity for any member to flag the verdict; if no one flags, it locks. This satisfies Leventhal's correctability rule and gives the loser a real, not symbolic, exit valve. (4) Per-member ratification taps — each member explicitly taps to commit, converting acceptance into Cialdini-style public commitment. The loser's tap is the high-signal moment for follow-through. (5) Alternation memory — the verdict screen surfaces 'last 3 decisions went X, Y, Z' so members can see fairness across time, not just within this decision (consistency rule, Leventhal). (6) Veto-and-replace mechanic for hard constraints (allergies, restrictions) that bypasses the procedural layer entirely, preventing moral-mandate boundary violations. (7) Defer self-evaluation hits — do NOT show 'your pick scored lowest' style data to the loser; the Brockner 2002 reversal warns that fair process can make the unfavorable outcome bite the loser's self-image harder if they're given attributional ammunition against themselves.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: Verdict screens that show only the result and hide the rule will leak follow-through specifically through the loser. Symptoms: the loser nominally agrees in the moment, then ghosts the meet-up, downgrades enthusiasm, free-rides without commitment, brings up the loss in the next decision as a grievance ('we did your thing last time'), or quietly defects to a parallel sub-plan. At group level, the gap between majority-agreement and whole-group-show-up widens, the north-star metric stalls, and groups churn after 2-3 cycles because cumulative unbuffered losses corrode trust. The dangerous failure mode is silent: the metric will show 'verdict reached' as success while the actual follow-through fails downstream, making the failure invisible to engagement metrics that don't track behavior beyond verdict-screen-tap.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Must-have for verdict screen. Rationale: this construct is the canonical mechanism for converting a non-Pareto group decision into whole-group follow-through, which is GetToIt's north star — no other single construct covers the same causal ground.

**dependency_on_other_constructs**: REQUIRES voice effect (Thibaut & Walker) — the buffer needs a real voice signal to work; performative voice backfires. REQUIRES informational justice (Bies/Greenberg) — the account/explanation is one of the buffering ingredients. AMPLIFIED BY public commitment / ratification (Cialdini, Kiesler) — a fair-process-buffered acceptance, when publicly committed, becomes consistent follow-through behavior. SUBSTITUTES PARTIALLY for distributive justice (Adams/Deutsch) — a procedurally fair process can stand in for a distributively unfavorable outcome (that is the whole point of the construct). CONFLICTS WITH naive 'soften the blow' UX patterns that hide the rule or apologize for the outcome — those patterns destroy the buffer. ANCHORED IN Tyler's group-value model — the relational respect signals from group-value are part of why procedural fairness buffers in the first place, especially in friend groups. BOUNDED BY moral mandates (Skitka & Mullen) — for outcomes touching dietary or moral constraints, this construct does not apply; those must be encoded as upstream hard constraints, not preferences.

**interaction_with_p1_p2**: Interaction with P1 (EBA + Satisficing hybrid): EBA narrows the option set by sequential elimination on attributes; Satisficing locks the first 'good-enough' result. Both frameworks produce a final verdict that is, by construction, NOT every member's first choice — they explicitly trade Pareto optimality for closure. That trade-off is exactly the territory the outcome-favorability interaction covers: P1 delivers the loser, this construct delivers the loser's commitment. Without the fair-process buffer, EBA/Satisficing ship verdicts that are mathematically clean and behaviorally fragile. With it, they ship verdicts that lock and follow through. Interaction with P2 dominant causes: PERFECT FIT for Misaligned_Preferences — that is literally the situation this construct addresses (multiple incompatible preferences, one verdict). Strong fit for Social_Friction — the construct prevents post-decision friction by replacing social blame with procedural attribution. Moderate fit for Fear_of_Regret/FOMO — fair process reduces regret over the loss because the rule, not the self or the friends, owns the outcome (counterfactual blame is dampened). Net: this construct is the load-bearing complement to P1+P2 — they explain the pathology, this construct explains the cure.

## Public Commitment / Ratification

_Source: `Public_Commitment_Ratification.json`_

### Basic Info

**construct_name**: Public Commitment / Ratification

**originators**: Kurt Lewin (group decision and social change, 1947); Charles A. Kiesler (commitment as binding to behavioral acts, 1971); Robert Cialdini (commitment-and-consistency principle in Influence, 1984); supporting lineage: Leon Festinger (cognitive dissonance, 1957) and Jack W. Brehm (post-decisional spreading-of-alternatives, 1956); Daryl Bem (self-perception theory, 1967); Morton Deutsch & Harold Gerard (public vs. private commitment in conformity, 1955); Gerald Salancik (commitment and the control of organizational behavior, 1977); Peter Gollwitzer & Paschal Sheeran (implementation-intention meta-analysis, 2006); Beshears, Choi, Laibson, Madrian (commitment-device field experiments, 2010s).

**origin_year**: 1947 (Lewin, group decision and social change); 1955 (Deutsch & Gerard, public commitment in conformity); 1956 (Brehm, post-decisional dissonance); 1971 (Kiesler, Psychology of Commitment); 1977 (Salancik, organizational commitment); 1984 (Cialdini, Influence — commitment & consistency popularization); 1999 (Gollwitzer, implementation intentions); 2006 (Gollwitzer & Sheeran meta-analysis).

**discipline**: Social psychology (Lewin, Kiesler, Festinger, Brehm, Bem, Deutsch & Gerard); persuasion / applied social psychology (Cialdini); organizational behavior (Salancik); behavioral economics / commitment-device design (Beshears, Choi, Laibson, Madrian; Rogers, Milkman, Volpp); goal-pursuit and self-regulation (Gollwitzer, Sheeran).

**one_sentence_summary**: Once a person publicly, voluntarily, and effortfully ties themselves to a decision, downstream consistency pressure, reputational stakes, and closed exit options all push them to actually follow through — and group settings amplify the binding because the audience is already in the room.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1 — Binding act: the chooser performs an observable act (a tap, a signature, a verbal 'in', a button press in the verdict screen) that selects one option and is observable to other agents. Kiesler (1971) defines this as the moment commitment is incurred — the person becomes 'bound by actions to beliefs that sustain activities' (Salancik 1977 follow-on definition). The act has to be (a) voluntary, (b) public, (c) explicit, and (d) at least somewhat effortful or costly; weakening any of these four moderators weakens the downstream effect (Cialdini 1984; Kiesler 1971). Step 2 — Consistency pressure: once bound, two psychological mechanisms apply downstream pressure to follow through. (i) Cognitive-dissonance / post-decisional dissonance (Festinger 1957; Brehm 1956): not following through after explicit commitment generates an inconsistency between the public act ('I said I'm in') and the behavior ('I bailed'), which is aversive and motivates either follow-through or attitude change. Brehm's 1956 'spreading of alternatives' result is the key downstream effect — after committing, the chosen option is rated more desirable and the rejected options less desirable, so the counterfactual collapses on its own. (ii) Self-perception (Bem 1967): the chooser observes their own commitment act and infers 'I must want this' from the behavior, which then drives consistent follow-through behavior. The two mechanisms are empirically hard to distinguish but make the same prediction. Step 3 — Reputational / face cost: the public component adds Goffman-style face management. Reneging on a public commitment costs reputational standing with the witnesses; even among friends, low-grade face-loss accumulates ('Sam always bails'). Deutsch & Gerard (1955) showed empirically that public commitment in an Asch-style conformity paradigm dramatically reduced subsequent change in judgments compared to private commitment, even when the original judgment was demonstrably wrong — the public binding was that strong. Step 4 — Exit-option closure: when the commitment is structured as a hard close (no easy reversal), the chooser stops counterfactual-shopping and the regret comparison loop collapses. Beshears et al. (2015, 2020) show in savings-account field experiments that the LEAST-liquid commitment account (no early withdrawal at all) attracted the largest deposits — people prefer hard closes over soft ones because the hard close eliminates the temptation surface entirely. Step 5 — Group amplification (Lewin's mechanism): when commitment is made in a group context with mutual observation, three independent forces stack. (a) Each member's commitment is witnessed by every other member, so every dyad has reputational stakes. (b) The group jointly observes the verdict, so the verdict becomes common knowledge in the Lewin sense — not just 'I committed' but 'I committed AND everyone knows everyone committed', which is much harder to defect from than a private commitment. (c) Lewin's 1947 housewives study found that groups who jointly DISCUSSED and decided on adopting organ-meat cooking were ~5x more likely to actually serve organ meats than groups who heard the same nutrition lecture passively — the group-decision condition activated mutual public commitment, the lecture condition did not. Step 6 — Implementation specification: layering an if-then implementation intention on top of the commitment ('on Friday at 7pm we go to Place X', not 'we agreed Place X is good') adds the Gollwitzer mechanism — the situational cue (Friday, 7pm) automatically retrieves the planned action. Gollwitzer & Sheeran's 2006 meta-analysis of 94 independent tests, ~8000 participants, found a medium-to-large effect of d ≈ 0.65 on goal attainment — so the if-then specification is doing substantial work on top of the bare commitment. Collective implementation intentions ('cII', Wieber et al. 2017) extend the effect to small-group goals and produce more cooperative within-group behavior than individual plans, which is exactly the GetToIt regime.

**behavioral_signature**: Tell-tale signs that public commitment is producing follow-through: (1) members visibly tap / type / speak a binding action (an 'I'm in' button, a check-in, a name on a list) BEFORE the verdict is consumed, not after; (2) the verdict screen visibly displays who has committed and who has not yet, so each member can verify mutual commitment in one glance (mutual-knowledge condition, Lewin); (3) the act of committing is described by participants as 'locking it in' or 'we said we'd go' rather than 'we discussed going' — language shifts from deliberative to performative; (4) post-commitment, members' verbal references to un-chosen options decrease sharply (Brehm spreading-of-alternatives); (5) bail rate / no-show rate measurably drops vs. matched non-ratified groups; (6) re-litigation attempts ('actually what about...') face social friction from the group rather than being entertained — the commitment artifact (the visible verdict + commitment record) becomes a coordination point that dissents have to overcome; (7) when one member tries to bail, others reference the prior commitment ('but you said you'd go') rather than re-opening the choice — a sign the binding is operating at the group level, not just the individual level; (8) the time from verdict to actual departure / arrival shrinks because no one is privately renegotiating in DMs.

**typical_triggers**: Effect is strongest when: (1) the commitment is explicit and observable, not implicit ('I tap I'm in' vs. 'I didn't object'); (2) it is voluntary — perceived coercion (forced ratification, peer-pressure ratification) collapses the dissonance pathway because the commitment is externally attributable, not self-attributable (Kiesler 1971; Wicklund & Brehm 1976); (3) it requires some effort or friction — a single tap is weaker than typing a confirmation, which is weaker than a written contract; (4) it is irrevocable or hard-to-reverse — Beshears et al. show the hardest close attracts the strongest commitment; (5) it is made in front of an audience whose opinion the chooser values (friends and ingroup members satisfy this directly); (6) stakes are mid-range — too low and consistency pressure is overwhelmed by indifference, too high and the chooser refuses to commit at all; (7) implementation cues are specified (when, where, with whom — Gollwitzer if-then); (8) the time between commitment and execution is short (commitment effects decay over time as memory and salience fade); (9) the commitment is bundled with a publicly visible plan, not just an attitude statement — 'we're going to Joe's at 7' is binding, 'Joe's sounds good' is not.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: Very high fit, and arguably the single most direct lever for the north-star follow-through metric. The GetToIt regime is purpose-built for public commitment: (a) the group is small (2-6) and high-trust (friends), so the audience for the commitment is exactly the people whose witness matters most; (b) the verdict is a specific action plan (Place X, time T) rather than an identity statement, dodging the Gollwitzer 2009 substitution boundary; (c) the time-to-execution is short (same evening) so commitment-effect decay is minimal; (d) the binding act can be made cheap to perform but expensive to reverse (single tap to commit; visible group-state to renege publicly), exactly the asymmetry Beshears et al. show maximizes commitment uptake; (e) the construct directly targets the failure mode the rest of the construct cluster cannot fix on its own — a perfectly fair, voice-respecting, well-explained verdict that nobody actually shows up for; (f) commitment also defuses Fear_of_Regret_FOMO post-hoc by collapsing the counterfactual through Brehm's spreading-of-alternatives effect, so the same mechanic does double duty against P2's third-largest cause; (g) the friend-group context is the empirically strongest version of public commitment (mutual observation, repeated future interaction, shared reputational stakes) modulo the friend-absorption caveat in known_limitations. The only fit limitation is that GetToIt cannot generate effortful or written commitments at v1 without burdening the UX — but a single tap on a 'I'm in' button, mutually visible, with a hard close behind it, captures most of the empirical effect.

**dominance_estimate**: High — primary lever for follow-through. The other constructs in this cluster (Voice, Tyler group-engagement, Leventhal six rules, Brockner outcome-favorability, informational-justice explanations, distributive equity) all serve verdict-ACCEPTANCE — they make the loser feel the procedure was fair. Public commitment is the only construct in the cluster that targets verdict-EXECUTION — actually showing up. Since the north-star metric is follow-through, not satisfaction, this construct is functionally the load-bearing one for the v1 metric definition. Rationale for 'high' rather than 'must-have': empirical effect sizes (d ≈ 0.65 for implementation intentions, g ≈ 0.68 for commitment devices) are medium-to-large, not enormous, so commitment alone won't carry the metric — but the combination of public ratification + hard close + if-then specification is the empirically supported stack with the strongest claim on the metric. Dominance is high relative to other constructs in this cluster but not relative to the upstream P1 framework choice (EBA + Satisficing) — if the verdict itself is incoherent, no commitment mechanic saves it.

**group_size_specificity**: Amplified, not muted, in 2-6 person groups. Three reasons: (1) every member is observed by every other member, so the commitment graph is fully connected — every dyad carries reputational stakes (in a 100-person crowd, individual commitment is largely anonymous and the effect dilutes). (2) Lewin's housewives groups were ~13-17 people and produced ~5x adoption; smaller groups should produce at least as strong an effect because the social density per dyad is higher. (3) Mutual common-knowledge condition is easier to satisfy in small groups — in 6 people, one verdict-screen view confirms that everyone knows everyone has committed; in 30, it is fragmented. (4) Collective implementation intentions (Wieber et al. 2017) show enhanced cooperation specifically in small-group settings, and their experimental groups were of exactly the GetToIt size range. The one place small groups MIGHT mute the effect is when one defector is enough to collapse the plan (3 friends, one bails, the other two cancel) — large groups are more robust to single defections. But for follow-through-rate-per-member, small groups with mutual public commitment are the empirical sweet spot.

### Verdict-Screen Implication

**design_signal_to_express**: The verdict screen must enact a binding act — not announce a result. Concretely it must communicate three things in this order: (1) 'this is the verdict' (verdict shown, not negotiated); (2) 'here is your slot to commit, visibly to your group' (an explicit ratification act, mutually observable); (3) 'this is now closed' (a hard-close artifact — the option set is gone, not minimized). The signals to express, in order: explicitness (you are doing a specific thing, not just nodding); voluntariness (you can decline, and declining is itself observable to the group rather than coerced into a yes); mutual observability (you can see that everyone else has also committed); irrevocability (re-opening is friction, not a tap); and time-binding (when, where, who — implementation-intention specification). The screen must NOT communicate: 'this is a suggestion', 'you can change your mind later', 'we'll figure out the details', 'X% of people agreed' — every one of these dilutes one of the four Cialdini moderators (active, public, voluntary, effortful).

**copy_framework_implications**: Copy patterns implied: (1) Verdict line is performative, not deliberative — 'You're going to Joe's. 7:00 PM' rather than 'How about Joe's around 7?' (Lewin / Salancik: the verdict itself must read as a behavioral act, not an opinion). (2) Ratification CTA is first-person and explicit — 'I'm in' rather than 'OK' or 'Confirm' (Bem self-perception: the chooser must observe their own act as a self-statement of commitment, not as procedural acknowledgment). (3) Mutual-state line names the witnesses — 'You + Sam + Priya are in. Waiting on Alex.' rather than '3/4 confirmed' (Lewin / Deutsch & Gerard: the audience must be named to activate reputational stakes). (4) Time-and-place line specifies the if-then cue — 'Joe's, 7:00 PM, meet at the door' rather than 'Joe's tonight' (Gollwitzer: the situational cue must be concrete enough to auto-trigger retrieval). (5) Hard-close line is short and definite — 'Locked in.' or 'Done.' rather than 'You can change this in settings' (Beshears: the closure must read as final). (6) Bail copy frames defection as costly — 'Tell the group you're out' rather than 'Cancel' (Goffman face cost is preserved by making the defection an act with an audience, not a private toggle). (7) Avoid 'optional', 'flexible', 'whenever' — every soft-close word costs measurable follow-through. (8) Avoid identity framing — 'You're the kind of group that follows through' triggers the Gollwitzer 2009 substitution boundary; stick to plan framing.

**ux_mechanic_implications**: Concrete mechanics: (1) Pre-commit ratification — each member taps 'I'm in' BEFORE the venue is revealed. This is the strongest empirical lever because (a) the commitment is to the procedure / verdict-mechanism, not to the specific outcome, which means even the loser is bound (procedural commitment generalizes; outcome commitment doesn't); (b) it dodges the post-verdict bargaining loop where the loser tries to renegotiate; (c) it directly mirrors Cialdini's low-ball mechanism — commit first, learn the cost (the venue) second; (d) Brehm's spreading-of-alternatives can then operate on the revealed venue without competition. The committed member's brain has already moved to consistency mode by the time the venue appears. (2) Mutual-state visibility — the verdict screen shows each member's commitment status in real time. Lewin's mutual-observation mechanism requires this; without it, commitment is private-feeling even if technically public. (3) Hard close — once the last member ratifies, the option set is gone from the UI; it cannot be re-opened with a tap. Reopening should require explicit group action ('un-lock for the group') so the cost of reopening is borne by the requester, not the system. (4) If-then time/place specification — the verdict line includes when AND where AND meet-point; this is the Gollwitzer cue specification and is a free win because the data is already in the verdict. (5) Public bail mechanic — if a member tries to back out, the bail is announced to the group rather than silent (preserves reputational stake). (6) Optional second-stage commitment for high-stakes decisions — written 'we'll be there' message in the group chat, written > spoken (Cialdini moderator), but skip for v1 unless a second beat is needed. (7) Avoid: opt-out windows, 'change your mind anytime' settings, soft confirms, individual private nudges (these all defeat one of the four Cialdini moderators). (8) DO NOT add identity language ('You're committers!') — Gollwitzer 2009 substitution risk.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the verdict screen ignores public commitment, the predicted failure mode is precisely the failure-mode the GetToIt product exists to prevent: verdict produced, group says 'sounds good', nobody actually goes. Mechanism: (a) without an explicit binding act, members are in the deliberative-attitude state, not the committed-behavior state — 'sounds good' is a mood report, not a commitment; (b) without mutual observability, each member privately re-litigates ('actually maybe I'll just stay in') because they don't know others have actually committed; (c) without a hard close, the option set is psychologically still open — counterfactual regret (P2's Fear_of_Regret) keeps firing; (d) without if-then specification, the situational cue (it's now 7pm) doesn't auto-retrieve the planned action, so the default behavior (do nothing / order in / scroll) wins; (e) at the group level, one private bail in DMs cascades because the others learn about it sequentially and each updates 'plan is collapsing'. The follow-through rate collapses to roughly the rate it was at without the product — the verdict was a suggestion, not a commitment, and the product becomes a fancy poll. This is the single biggest predicted failure mode for GetToIt v1, and ignoring this construct is the way it materializes.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Must-have for verdict screen. Rationale: this is the only construct in the cluster that directly targets the north-star metric (follow-through) rather than verdict satisfaction. Pre-commit ratification + mutual-state visibility + hard close is the empirically supported minimum stack and is cheap to implement.

**dependency_on_other_constructs**: Amplifies all other constructs in the cluster (Voice, Tyler group-engagement, Leventhal six rules, Brockner outcome-favorability, informational-justice explanations, distributive equity) by converting their acceptance gains into follow-through gains — a fairer-feeling verdict that is also publicly committed produces strictly more behavior change than a fairer-feeling verdict alone. Substitutes for: nothing in this cluster (commitment cannot replace fairness signals; an unfair verdict that is publicly committed produces resentment AND face-trapped attendance, which damages the relational substrate over time and kills the product on a different axis). Requires: a verdict that the loser can still publicly commit to without major face cost (so Voice and Informational_Justice_Explanation are de-facto prerequisites — public commitment to a verdict that erased your voice is reactance bait). Conflicts with: any UX mechanic that softens the close (opt-out windows, 'still figuring it out' state, optional confirms) — these are mutually destructive with this construct and must be removed wherever they appear.

**interaction_with_p1_p2**: With P1 (EBA + Satisficing): strongly compatible and amplifying. EBA produces a single verdict by elimination, Satisficing accepts the first good-enough option without re-comparison; both frameworks already commit to the no-recompare regime that public commitment also requires. The ratification is the procedural close that makes the EBA + Satisficing output behaviorally durable rather than just computationally clean. With P2 dominant causes: (a) Misaligned_Preferences — public commitment doesn't directly defuse misalignment (it can mask it), but the procedural-justice cluster (Voice, Informational_Justice) handles that; commitment then locks the negotiated outcome in. Caveat: forced public commitment to a verdict that crushed someone's preference can backfire as reactance — this is why pre-commit ratification (committing to the procedure before the outcome) is empirically better than post-commit (committing to the outcome after). (b) Social_Friction — public commitment is the dominant defuse mechanism here; once everyone has visibly committed, the social-friction loop ('I don't want to be the one who...') cannot operate because nobody is the one who picked — the procedure is, and everyone has bound themselves to the procedure. (c) Fear_of_Regret_FOMO — public commitment is one of the two strongest defuse mechanisms (along with option compression) because Brehm's spreading-of-alternatives effect operates on committed choices specifically: post-commitment, the chosen option becomes more attractive and the rejected options less attractive, automatically. The hard close prevents the regret comparison from re-opening. This is the empirical justification for the 'Pre-commit ratification + hard close (no re-open)' mechanic identified in P2 synthesis.

## Group Value Model / Relational Model of Authority / Group Engagement Model (Tyler)

_Source: `Tyler_Group_Value_Model.json`_

### Basic Info

**construct_name**: Group Value Model / Relational Model of Authority / Group Engagement Model (Tyler)

**originators**: Tom R. Tyler with E. Allan Lind (Group Value Model and Relational Model of Authority); Tom R. Tyler with Steven L. Blader (Group Engagement Model). Theoretical roots in Thibaut & Walker's procedural justice work (1975) and in social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner 1979).

**origin_year**: 1988 (Lind & Tyler 'The Social Psychology of Procedural Justice' book — group-value model first formalized); 1989 (Tyler JPSP article 'The Psychology of Procedural Justice: A Test of the Group-Value Model'); 1992 (Tyler & Lind 'A Relational Model of Authority in Groups' chapter, Adv. Exp. Soc. Psy. vol. 25); 2000 (Tyler & Blader 'Cooperation in Groups' book); 2003 (Tyler & Blader 'Group Engagement Model' Pers. Soc. Psy. Review article).

**discipline**: Social psychology (procedural justice / intergroup relations) with heavy uptake in organizational behavior, criminology and law (police legitimacy), and political psychology (legitimacy of government decisions). Theoretical lineage: procedural-justice law-and-society tradition (Thibaut & Walker) plus social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner).

**one_sentence_summary**: People accept authoritative decisions and cooperate with the group not primarily because outcomes are favorable, but because the procedure communicated that they are respected, that the decider was neutral, and that the decider can be trusted to have benevolent motives — three relational signals that tell the member they are a valued part of the group.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: The chain runs in four stages. (1) The decision procedure emits relational signals — three are canonical in the Tyler & Lind 1992 relational model: respectful interpersonal treatment / status recognition (the procedure treats the member as a full and dignified group member), neutrality (the decider is unbiased, applies rules consistently, gives accurate information), and trustworthy motives (the decider is benevolent, ethical, and considers the member's needs). (2) These signals are decoded as identity information: 'how the group treats me tells me how the group values me' (Tyler 1989; Tyler & Lind 1992). The Group Engagement Model (Tyler & Blader 2003) sharpens this: relational treatment shapes social identity — pride in the group and respect within the group — independent of outcome favorability. (3) Social identity then drives attitudes (legitimacy, perceived fairness, commitment to the group) and behavior (rule-following, cooperation, willingness to defer when one's preference loses). (4) Crucially, this pathway operates in parallel to and largely independent of the outcome-instrumental pathway: a member can lose on the outcome and still accept and cooperate if the relational signals were strong, because identity needs were met. The model's central empirical claim — relational signals drive acceptance independent of outcome favorability — is what distinguishes it from earlier control-based theories (Thibaut & Walker's voice / process control) that explained voice effects in instrumental terms.

**behavioral_signature**: Acceptance present: (1) The member whose preference lost still publicly endorses the verdict, shows up, and follows through (the diagnostic 'I lost but it was fair' pattern). (2) Spontaneous explanations of fairness reference treatment ('they listened', 'it was even-handed', 'no one was playing favorites') rather than outcome ('I got what I wanted'). (3) Willingness to defer to the same group/decider on future decisions — the legitimacy halo. (4) Reduced disputing or post-decision lobbying. (5) Higher follow-through commitment proportional to perceived relational fairness, especially when outcome was unfavorable to the member (the Brockner outcome-favorability × procedural-justice interaction: procedural justice matters MOST when you lost). Acceptance absent: (1) The losing member technically agrees but later flakes, undermines, or relitigates ('fine, whatever'). (2) Complaints reference disrespect, bias, or hidden agendas ('they didn't even ask me', 'this was rigged for X', 'you guys always pick what Y wants'). (3) Withdrawal from future decisions ('I don't care, you decide' next time, with bitterness rather than indifference). (4) Sub-group formation against the verdict.

**typical_triggers**: Strongest when: (a) the member identifies with the group (high in-group identification — Tyler & Blader 2003 explicitly: identity-relevance is the moderator); (b) the outcome is unfavorable to the member, so the relational signal is what's left to read (Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 fair-process effect interaction: r boosts up to ~.40 in unfavorable-outcome conditions vs ~.15 in favorable); (c) the decision is from an authority or rule-system perceived as legitimate (police, employer, court, captain, host); (d) ongoing rather than one-shot relationships, where future cooperation is at stake; (e) decisions where status / standing in the group is observable (public decisions, peer-visible). Weakest when: (a) the relationship is purely transactional with no shared identity (anonymous market exchange), (b) the member doesn't care about the group (low identification), (c) only outcomes matter (one-shot, no future, no audience), (d) relational signals can't be read clearly (faceless / anonymous procedure with no human cue).

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Strong and durable across decades and domains. Tyler 1989 JPSP (the seminal group-value test) showed neutrality, trust, and social-standing judgments uniquely predict procedural-justice perceptions even after controlling for outcome and process control. Lind & Tyler 1988 book and Tyler & Lind 1992 chapter (Advances in Experimental Social Psychology vol. 25) consolidate dozens of supporting field and lab studies. Colquitt et al. 2001 'Justice at the Millennium' meta-analysis (183 studies, Journal of Applied Psychology) confirms procedural justice has a substantial unique relationship with outcomes such as satisfaction, commitment, and authority evaluation; the interpersonal-treatment dimension (a Tylerian construct in disguise) shows correlations with authority evaluation typically in the r=.40-.60 range. Colquitt et al. 2013 'Justice at the Millennium, a Decade Later' (JAP) replicated and extended these patterns and confirmed multidimensionality (procedural / distributive / interpersonal / informational), with interpersonal justice particularly tightly tied to identity-related and relational outcomes. Mazerolle, Bennett, Davis, Sargeant & Manning 2013 Campbell Systematic Review of policing experiments (30 studies, 41 evaluations) found procedurally just dialogue significantly improves citizen satisfaction, compliance, and perceived legitimacy — direct support for the group-value mechanism in a real-stakes authority context. Tyler & Blader 2003 themselves and the 2009 'Testing and Extending the Group Engagement Model' paper (Blader & Tyler, JAP) showed social identity mediates procedural-justice effects on extra-role behavior (β ≈ .42 for identity → behavior, ~19% variance explained). Effect sizes are typically medium to large by Cohen's conventions in the workplace and policing domains.

**replication_status**: The core relational-signals-drive-acceptance-independent-of-outcome finding has replicated reliably across 35+ years, dozens of countries, and multiple authority contexts. Notable replications and extensions: (1) Lind, Kanfer & Earley 1990 — non-instrumental voice (voice that demonstrably cannot affect the outcome) still raises perceived fairness, ruling out pure instrumental explanations and supporting the relational interpretation. (2) Tyler, Degoey & Smith 1996 JPSP — relational concerns matter even when no material outcome is at stake. (3) Lind, Ohbuchi, Sugawara & Huo 1998 — replicated cross-culturally (Japan vs USA), with the wrinkle that procedural justice mattered more in within-group than cross-group negotiations in BOTH cultures (boundary condition: in-group identification is the moderator, not nationality). (4) Mazerolle et al. 2013 Campbell review — procedurally just policing interventions replicate effect on legitimacy and cooperation across Australia, USA, England. (5) Blader & Tyler 2009 JAP — replicated and extended group engagement model in workplaces with field data. (6) Recent (2023) systematic review and meta-analysis of procedural justice and police legitimacy (J. Exp. Criminology) confirms procedural justice → legitimacy effect is robust but moderated by social identity and social context. No notable replication failures of the core finding; the active debate is over moderators and mechanism specifics, not whether the effect exists. Has held up well through the 2010s replication crisis because the core findings are large-effect and have been demonstrated in real-world field experiments, not just lab vignettes.

**known_limitations**: (1) THE CRITICAL LIMITATION FOR GETTOIT: nearly all the empirical base is on hierarchical authority — police, courts, managers, government, teachers — where there is a clear authority figure, a clear authority-recipient, and the decision-maker has both formal power and a stable role. Friend-group lateral decision-making (peers, no formal authority, rotating informal deciders, the 'authority' is an algorithm or a group consensus) is an extrapolation. The relational-signals theory should plausibly transfer because the underlying mechanism is identity-based, but quantitative effect sizes from policing/HR are not directly portable to peer groups. (2) Relational signals require something to read them off of — a person, a procedure with humanlike characteristics, or a system that telegraphs respect/neutrality/trust. A bare algorithm output ('We picked Sushi Place. Done.') with no readable relational signal will not activate the construct; the design has to do explicit work. (3) Cultural moderators exist — power-distance and individualism-collectivism shape which relational signals are most weighted (e.g., status-recognition signals weigh more in higher-power-distance cultures), though the basic effect replicates broadly. (4) Outcome favorability dominates when the member doesn't identify with the group — relational signals are conditional on caring about the group's verdict. (5) The model predicts acceptance, not enthusiasm — a member can accept a verdict as fair and still not be excited to follow through, so it must be combined with something that creates positive engagement. (6) Tylerian procedural justice can be 'gamed' by performative respect that is not backed by neutrality or trust — over time members notice and the legitimacy collapses (the 'token voice' failure mode). (7) Most measures are self-report; behavioral validation is heaviest in compliance/extra-role-behavior contexts, lighter in 'will I show up to dinner' contexts.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: High fit on the core mechanism, moderate-to-strong fit on the deployment context, with one well-defined caveat. Why high fit: the GetToIt verdict screen is functionally an authority decision being delivered to group members — the algorithm is the 'authority' and each member is a recipient who may or may not have lost on the outcome. The product's north-star is follow-through, which is exactly the kind of post-decision compliance/cooperation outcome the Tyler tradition has spent 35 years studying. The losing-member problem (member who wanted X but got Y) is the prototypical Tyler scenario: relational fairness is what determines whether they accept and show up. Why moderate caveats: (a) the canonical Tyler context is hierarchical (police, employer, court); friend groups are lateral and the 'authority' is an algorithm, so relational signals must be deliberately constructed by the UI rather than emerging from a human authority's behavior; (b) friend groups have high prior trust and shared identity, which is a strong tailwind for the model (members already identify with the group) but also raises the bar — relational missteps in a friend group land harder because identity stakes are higher than with a stranger authority. The product context (mid-stakes, observable outcome, ongoing relationship, repeated interaction, group identity already strong) checks every amplifier the Tyler literature lists. Stack with this: the verdict screen is a procedure, the algorithm is the decider, the friends are an in-group, and the metric is acceptance/follow-through. The construct fits like a key.

**group_size_specificity**: Effect should be amplified rather than muted in 2-6 person friend groups, with one structural shift. Amplified because: (a) every member is identifiable and visible to every other member, so relational treatment is salient and read by all, not diffused as in large groups (a 2-6 person group is the band where each individual's status signals are most legible to peers); (b) the losing member is unambiguously identifiable, which means the procedural-justice question 'did the procedure respect them' has a clear referent rather than being a statistical aggregate; (c) follow-through is observable per-member (in a 4-person group, one flake breaks the dinner; in a 400-person organization, individual flake is invisible) so the mechanism's behavioral payoff is concentrated. Structural shift: most Tyler research is on group-vs-authority dyad (citizen + police, employee + manager); 2-6 friend groups have no fixed authority — the 'authority' rotates or is delegated to the algorithm. So the relational signals must be borne by the procedure itself rather than by a human who can adjust treatment in real time. This actually plays to GetToIt's strength because UI affordances (copy, reveal sequencing, ratification mechanics) can deliver consistent relational signals more reliably than a human host whose mood varies. Note: at the smaller end (n=2), the model still applies but Voice Effect and direct dyadic negotiation may dominate; the relational-signal channel is most important for n=3+.

### Verdict-Screen Implication

**design_signal_to_express**: The verdict screen must telegraph all three relational signals simultaneously, weighted toward whichever is weakest in the specific verdict context. (1) RESPECT / status recognition: every member's input must be visibly counted and named, especially the input from the member whose preferred option didn't win. The losing member must see 'we heard you' as a first-class element of the screen, not buried in a 'see details' link. Concrete: name the loser's preference back to them ('Maya, you wanted Thai — the group went with sushi this round'). (2) NEUTRALITY: the rule that produced the verdict must be made legible, simple, and visibly applied to everyone the same way. The screen must show that no one's input was weighted more than anyone else's, that the rule was deterministic given the inputs, and that the same rule applied to all options. Concrete: show the elimination chain or the score breakdown so members can verify the rule was symmetric. (3) TRUSTWORTHY MOTIVES: the algorithm's purpose must be visibly aligned with the group's interest (find the option that maximizes whole-group follow-through), not with platform interests, advertiser interests, or any single member's interests. Concrete: state the rule the algorithm is optimizing for in plain language ('we picked the option that got the fewest hard-no votes'). Combined effect: the verdict screen must be a visible procedure, not an opaque output. The output ('Sushi Place') is one element; the procedure exposition is the other three-quarters of the screen.

**copy_framework_implications**: Six concrete copy patterns implied by the construct. (1) NAME THE LOSER. Reference by name the member whose top preference didn't win, in a one-line acknowledgment. Example: 'Maya, your pick (Thai Garden) didn't win this round — but it had support.' This is the single highest-leverage copy move; the procedural-justice literature is unambiguous that being named and acknowledged drives acceptance more than any abstract fairness language. (2) NAME THE RULE in plain language, before showing the result. Example: 'We pick the option with the fewest hard-no votes from the group. Here's how it shook out.' This delivers the neutrality and trustworthy-motives signals simultaneously. (3) NAME THE INPUTS. Show what each member contributed, without weighting any one above another. Example: 'Sam wanted: Thai or sushi. Ari wanted: anything not Indian. Maya wanted: Thai. Jordan wanted: sushi or pizza.' Names matter; anonymized inputs ('one of you said...') destroys the respect signal. (4) AVOID FORMAL LANGUAGE — friend register. The literature comes from courts and HR; GetToIt is a friend group. 'In accordance with the group's preferences' = court-register, lethal. 'Here's how it landed' = friend-register, alive. (5) ACKNOWLEDGE TRADE-OFFS, don't paper over them. 'Sushi Place won, but Thai Garden was a close second — let's do that next week' is more legitimacy-generating than 'Sushi Place — perfect for everyone!' Honest accounting is a trustworthy-motives signal; over-claiming destroys it. (6) NO BLAME. Don't name a member as the cause of the loser's loss ('Sam vetoed Thai'); name the rule ('Thai got one hard-no, sushi got zero'). The rule is the actor; members are inputs. Anti-patterns: anonymous outputs, pure deterministic phrasing without acknowledgment, marketing-style enthusiasm ('Get ready for an amazing dinner!'), and any copy that elides the loser.

**ux_mechanic_implications**: Six UX mechanics implied. (1) WHO-WANTED-WHAT REVEAL. Default-on display showing each member's input, named, before showing the verdict. This is the structural respect-signal mechanic. Optional toggle to 'show details' is too weak — it must be the default. (2) RULE EXPOSITION PANEL. A visible (not hidden behind a link) one-paragraph explanation of the rule, ideally with a small diagram or score breakdown, so members can verify neutrality. Treats the algorithm as a glass box, not a black box. (3) RATIFICATION / ACKNOWLEDGE BUTTON per member. Each member taps 'got it' or 'I'm in', creating a public-commitment artifact. The group sees a row of confirmations (or not), which both delivers the trustworthy-motives signal (the procedure waits for everyone to engage) and creates the public commitment that drives follow-through. Crucially, this must be opt-in confirmation, not opt-out — silence does not count as acceptance. (4) DISPUTE / FLAG-AS-UNFAIR PATH. A small, low-stakes 'this didn't feel right' button that surfaces concerns to the group thread without forcing a redo. The mere existence of the path is the relational signal (the system respects you enough to give you a way to push back); usage rate will be low but the legitimacy boost is high. Tyler & Lind 1992 explicitly cite correctability as a relational fairness criterion. (5) LOSER-AWARE COPY VARIATION. The screen detects which member's top option didn't win and personalizes the named acknowledgment to that member's view of the screen. Each member sees their own loss called out (or, if they got their preference, sees a slightly different framing). This is operationally simple (the algorithm already knows who voted what) and is the highest-payoff personalization the screen can do. (6) HISTORICAL FAIRNESS LEDGER (light). A small chip or footer noting 'last round Maya's pick won, this round Sam's' — over time this delivers neutrality-across-decisions, which is the relational signal that legitimates the algorithm as a recurring authority for the group. Anti-mechanics: anonymized 'group has decided' outputs, hidden rules, no per-member acknowledgment, no dispute path.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: Two-stage failure. STAGE 1 (single decision): the losing member technically agrees but follows through at a lower rate. They see 'Sushi Place — Confirmed' with no explanation, no acknowledgment of their Thai preference, no visible rule, and read it as 'the algorithm chose for us, and it didn't seem to know or care that I wanted something else.' They go from active preference (Thai) to passive accommodation (fine, sushi) without the procedure helping bridge that gap. Predicted behavior: same-day flake, last-minute 'I'm tired actually', or showing up but disengaged. STAGE 2 (repeat decisions): legitimacy collapses cumulatively. After 3-4 verdicts where their input felt anonymized or their loss un-acknowledged, the member starts gaming the input ('I'll just say I'm fine with anything, the app doesn't seem to listen anyway'), which produces low-information signal and degraded verdicts, which produces more flakes, which the group reads as the app not working — death spiral. Specific predicted patterns the metric will show: (a) follow-through gap concentrated in members whose top preference lost (the diagnostic Brockner pattern); (b) decline in input quality over time (members hedge harder, see Social_Friction failure mode); (c) drop in repeat-use among members who lost in their first or second use; (d) qualitative complaints clustering around 'it just picks something' rather than around the specific options chosen. The single most diagnostic measurable signal: follow-through rate, conditional on member having lost their top preference, in the first three uses of the product.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: must-have for verdict screen — directly addresses the losing-member follow-through problem (the central north-star failure mode), maps to operationalizable copy/UX moves rather than research-only insights, and is well-supported empirically across decades. The verdict screen cannot afford to be a bare output; the relational procedure surrounding the output IS the v1 design problem.

**dependency_on_other_constructs**: Sits at the architectural center of the verdict-screen fairness stack. (a) AMPLIFIED BY Voice Effect: relational signals require members to perceive that their voice was heard in producing the verdict; Tyler's group-value model and the Thibaut-Walker voice tradition were merged in Tyler & Lind 1992 because they are mechanistically intertwined. Voice during input + relational signals at output are the two halves of the same pipeline. (b) AMPLIFIED BY Informational Justice (Bies, Greenberg, Colquitt): the 'name the rule' and 'name the inputs' copy patterns are themselves accounts/explanations, so the construct delivers informational justice as a side-effect. The two are operationally fused on the verdict screen. (c) AMPLIFIED BY Public Commitment Ratification: the per-member ratification button delivers both the relational acknowledgment (you matter enough that the procedure waits for you) AND the consistency-pressure follow-through mechanism. (d) PARTIALLY SUBSTITUTES for Outcome Favorability: the whole point of relational fairness is to make acceptance possible WITHOUT the member getting their preferred outcome. (e) REQUIRES Leventhal's Six Rules to be visibly satisfied (consistency, bias-suppression, accuracy, correctability, representativeness, ethicality) because Leventhal's rules are the structural preconditions that the relational signals report on. (f) CONFLICTS WITH Distributive-Justice Equity if outcome distribution is over-emphasized: foregrounding 'who got what they wanted' as the metric undermines the relational story. The screen must lead with procedure, follow with outcome.

**interaction_with_p1_p2**: Strong positive interaction with the chosen P1 framework (EBA + Satisficing hybrid) and direct mitigation of the P2 dominant causes. P1 (EBA + Satisficing): EBA's elimination chain is the natural raw material for the neutrality signal — 'we cut these because of X, then these because of Y' is a transparent-rule narrative that delivers the trustworthy-motives signal almost for free. The verdict screen can literally show the EBA elimination steps as the rule-exposition panel (UX mechanic 2). Satisficing's 'good-enough' framing supports honest trade-off acknowledgment ('not everyone's first pick, but everyone's good-enough'). Group-value model elevates EBA from 'cold algorithm' to 'fair procedure' by adding the relational layer EBA itself doesn't have. P2 cause Misaligned_Preferences: the construct is the core mitigation — it converts 'I didn't get my preference' from an outcome failure into a procedural-acceptance event, breaking the link between losing and flaking. P2 cause Social_Friction: amplifying interaction in BOTH directions. Positive: the 'name the inputs' move surfaces preferences members hedged in private, partially defusing the suppression dynamic. Negative risk: if the 'name the loser' copy goes too far and creates public face-loss for the named member ('Maya wanted Thai but got overridden'), it can re-trigger the very face concerns Social_Friction describes. The design must name with respect (Maya's preference was heard and counted, not 'overridden') to avoid this. P2 cause Fear_of_Regret_FOMO: indirect mitigation. Group-value relational signals don't directly address FOMO (which is about the un-chosen option's value, not the procedure's fairness), but they reduce the secondary FOMO of 'we picked the wrong thing AND nobody listened to me', which is the compounded version of the failure mode. Net: this construct is the mortar that binds the P1 mechanism and the P2 defuse moves into a verdict screen members will accept.

## Voice Effect (Process Control / Voice in Procedural Justice)

_Source: `Voice_Effect.json`_

### Basic Info

**construct_name**: Voice Effect (Process Control / Voice in Procedural Justice)

**originators**: John W. Thibaut & Laurens Walker (process control / decision control framework); Robert Folger (referent cognitions and the voice manipulation); E. Allan Lind, Ruth Kanfer & P. Christopher Earley (instrumental vs value-expressive voice; pre- vs post-decision timing); Kees van den Bos (no-voice procedures, fair-outcome substitution, timing); Tom R. Tyler (group-value model, voice as relational signal); James E. Hunton & James C. Beeler (voice and decision acceptance in MIS / accounting contexts)

**origin_year**: 1975 (Thibaut & Walker, 'Procedural Justice: A Psychological Analysis'); 1977 (Folger, 'Distributive and procedural justice: Combined impact of voice and improvement on experienced inequity'); 1990 (Lind, Kanfer & Earley voice-control study); 1996 (van den Bos consistency/voice paper); 1999 (van den Bos no-voice / fair outcome paper); 1997 (Hunton voice and procedural justice in user-participation)

**discipline**: Social psychology (Thibaut & Walker, Folger, Lind, van den Bos); legal psychology / dispute resolution (Thibaut & Walker original — adversary vs inquisitorial procedure); organizational behavior and organizational justice (Tyler, Colquitt, Brockner, Cohen-Charash & Spector); information systems / accounting (Hunton & Beeler — voice in user participation and budget participation)

**one_sentence_summary**: Letting people express their views before a decision is made raises perceived fairness and acceptance of that decision — even when the voice has no power to change the outcome — because voice signals respect, status, and group standing as well as instrumental control.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1 (antecedent): a decision affecting two or more parties is about to be made by an authority, algorithm, or group process. Step 2 (voice opportunity): each party is invited to express preferences, evidence, arguments, or reactions before the decision is finalized. Step 3 (dual interpretation): per Lind, Kanfer & Earley 1990, the voice opportunity is processed simultaneously through (a) an instrumental channel — 'my input may shift the outcome toward what I want' (process-control branch from Thibaut & Walker 1975) — and (b) a value-expressive / relational channel — 'I am being treated as a full member of this group whose perspective matters' (group-value model, Tyler & Lind 1992; Tyler & Blader 2003). Step 4 (fairness inference): both channels independently raise procedural-fairness ratings; Lind et al. show value-expressive voice raises fairness judgments even when participants are explicitly told their voice will not affect the outcome — the relational signal is sufficient. Step 5 (acceptance and follow-through): heightened procedural fairness causes the recipient to accept the verdict, comply with it, commit to follow-through, and reduce retaliation/exit even when their preferred outcome lost (Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 fair-process effect — voice/PJ matters most when outcome is unfavorable to the perceiver). Step 6 (timing gate): the mechanism only fires reliably when voice precedes the binding decision (Lind, Kanfer & Earley 1990; van den Bos 1999); voice offered after the decision is locked is interpreted as either tokenistic (silence/mute substitute) or as evidence the authority is overconfident, and can produce a backfire effect (post-decisional voice reduces fairness vs pre-decisional voice, especially under high-confidence leadership; De Cremer 2005). Step 7 (failure mode): if voice is invited but visibly disregarded — input collected and ignored — the frustration effect activates and trust drops below the no-voice baseline; if some members are heard while others are denied voice in the same procedure, the mute effect produces the largest fairness penalty of all (denied-voice-when-others-have-it is worse than uniform no-voice).

**behavioral_signature**: (1) Acceptance asymmetry — recipients of unfavorable outcomes (lost their preferred option) rate fairness, satisfaction, and willingness-to-comply nearly as high as winners when voice was provided pre-decision (Folger 1977 'fair process effect'; Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 — PJ × outcome interaction). (2) Compliance independent of agreement — members say 'I disagree but it is fair' and follow through anyway. (3) Reduced retaliation and exit — fewer post-decision complaints, fewer renegotiation attempts, fewer no-shows. (4) Linguistic signature in feedback — 'at least I was heard', 'I had my say', 'they listened' — these are the diagnostic phrases that the value-expressive channel fired. (5) Fairness ratings rise even when participants explicitly know their voice was non-instrumental (Lind, Kanfer & Earley 1990 — pure value-expressive condition still beats no-voice on fairness measures). (6) When voice is denied unilaterally, recipients show heightened distress relative to outcome-matched control (mute effect). (7) When voice is offered after the decision, fairness ratings often fall below no-voice baseline — the backfire signature (silence effect / post-decisional voice). (8) When voice is offered but ignored, trust ratings collapse and reactance behaviors increase (frustration effect).

**typical_triggers**: Effect is strongest when: (a) outcome is unfavorable to the focal party (Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 — interaction d roughly 0.30-0.40 in the meta-analysis of 45 samples); (b) the decider is or appears to be an authority with discretion (so voice has plausible instrumental value); (c) the participant has not yet committed or locked into the decision (pre-decision timing); (d) low-power-distance cultures (US, Germany, Northern Europe — Brockner et al. 2001 culture × voice study showed voice effect markedly larger in low-power-distance samples than in PRC, Mexico, Hong Kong); (e) outcomes are at least moderately consequential (very trivial stakes mute the value-expressive channel because group-status signals are not at stake); (f) the voice channel is structured and bounded (open-ended venting can blur into the frustration-effect risk); (g) repeated-game / ongoing-relationship contexts where the relational signal compounds over time. Effect is weakest or reversed when: voice is post-decisional, voice is invited but visibly ignored, participants hold strong moral mandates that disqualify procedural buffering (Skitka 2002), or the decider is widely distrusted.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: High fit, with one structural twist that needs explicit design attention. The GetToIt case has all the standard voice-effect activators: 2-6 person group, mid-stakes outcome, single committed verdict, at least one member whose preference will lose. The product already provides voice via the quiz — each member's structured inputs (preferences, vetoes, intensities, mood/budget constraints) ARE the voice. This is structurally a near-ideal voice intervention because: (a) it is private and parallel, defusing the social-friction face cost that would suppress voice in offline conversation; (b) it is pre-decisional by construction (quiz fires before the algorithm commits to a verdict); (c) it is structured/bounded, avoiding the frustration-effect risk of unbounded venting; (d) it captures intensity, distinguishing real indifference from polite indifference. The structural twist: the decider is an algorithm, not a person, so the value-expressive / relational channel does not run through 'an authority recognizes me as a full group member' — it has to run through 'the system / the group registered my input as legitimate'. This means the verdict screen must do the work of acknowledging that each member's voice was received and weighted. If the quiz is the voice but the verdict screen presents the result as a black-box pronouncement, the value-expressive channel collapses and only the instrumental channel remains — and the instrumental channel only satisfies winners, not the loser whose follow-through the product depends on. The fix is straightforward: the verdict screen must visibly enact 'we heard you' for every member, not just the ones whose preference won.

**dominance_estimate**: High — likely a top-2 lever for verdict acceptance and follow-through, on par with informational justice (verdict explanation). Rationale: (a) the voice effect operates strongest precisely on the outcome-loser, who is the highest-risk no-show and therefore the bottleneck for the follow-through metric (Brockner & Wiesenfeld 1996 PJ × outcome interaction is most relevant exactly here); (b) the product already collects voice through the quiz, so the marginal cost to surface acknowledgment on the verdict screen is small relative to the leverage; (c) voice effect interacts multiplicatively with the chosen P1 framework (EBA + Satisficing) — the EBA elimination steps each represent a structured voice point per member, and the verdict screen can recap them as voice receipts; (d) it directly defuses the dominant P2 cause (Misaligned_Preferences) by transmuting unmet preference into 'at least heard' fairness, and the P2 Social_Friction cause is also partially addressed because private voice is face-safe. Caveat: voice effect alone does not fix outcome-favorability disparities — it must combine with informational justice (an account of why the verdict is what it is) and ideally Leventhal-style correctability. Voice alone with no explanation risks frustration effect; voice plus 'and here is why X won given your inputs' is the combined intervention that should dominate.

**group_size_specificity**: Moderately amplified at the GetToIt 2-6 band relative to dyads or large groups. In dyads (n=2), voice is implicit because each party hears the other directly — the procedural mechanic is collapsed and the effect must compete with direct outcome exchange. In 3-6 person groups, voice becomes structurally necessary because not everyone can dominate airtime; the value-expressive channel is most diagnostic in this range because individuals are most at risk of being talked over or having their preference invisibly subsumed under a coalition. In groups larger than ~7, voice opportunities become token-rationed (not everyone can speak), so the marginal value of being heard rises but the probability of actually being heard falls; this is the 'town hall' regime where voice mechanics are most likely to slide into frustration-effect territory if not carefully managed. The GetToIt band of 2-6 is a structurally favorable zone: small enough that every member's voice can be fully captured and reflected, large enough that voice mechanics are non-trivial. The mute effect (some-but-not-all heard) is the specific risk in this band — if the verdict screen acknowledges 4 of 5 members' inputs but appears to ignore the 5th, that one member is in the worst-case fairness position.

**ingroup_friend_specificity**: Friend-context modifies both the magnitude and the channel through which voice operates. Magnitude: prior trust raises the baseline acceptance of group decisions, which can shrink the apparent voice effect (less room to move ratings up). Channel: among strangers or in formal authority contexts, the value-expressive channel runs through 'this institution treats me as a full citizen' — among friends, it runs through 'my friends actually wanted to know what I thought, not just what was easy'. The relational reading is more diagnostic of friendship quality, so the cost of failure (voice ignored, mute effect) is also higher — being silenced by friends reads as a relational signal, not just a procedural one, and may damage the relationship in ways a workplace voice failure would not. Tyler & Blader 2003 group-engagement model predicts that voice signals in identity-relevant groups produce stronger commitment effects than in instrumental groups, which would suggest amplification in friend groups for the value-expressive channel even if magnitudes are compressed by ceiling effects on baseline trust. Practical implication: among friends, voice acknowledgment is necessary to avoid relational damage on losses, even if the marginal fairness gain on wins is small. The product cannot rely on prior friendship trust as a substitute for in-product voice acknowledgment.

### Verdict-Screen Implication

**design_signal_to_express**: The verdict screen must visibly enact three signals: (1) Voice-received receipt — every member sees concrete evidence that their specific quiz inputs were registered (their top picks, their vetoes, their intensity weights). This is the value-expressive channel. (2) Voice-weighted reasoning — the verdict screen surfaces how each member's input shaped (or pushed against) the chosen verdict ('Sam's veto on sushi eliminated 2 options; Alex's high-weight on quick-service shortened the list to 3'). This is the instrumental channel and the informational-justice bridge. (3) Loser-name acknowledgment — the verdict screen names which member did NOT get their top preference and what their preferred option was, with an explicit sentence acknowledging the trade-off. This activates voice effect strongest where it matters most (the outcome-loser, per Brockner & Wiesenfeld) and prevents the loser from feeling silently overruled. Anti-signal: do NOT add a post-decision voice opportunity (a 'how do you feel about this verdict?' field after the lock-in) — this risks the post-decisional voice backfire / silence effect. Voice must be banked pre-decision, then receipted post-decision; voice must not be re-solicited post-decision unless the verdict can actually be revised (correctability — see Leventhal). Anti-signal: do NOT show voice-receipts for some members and skip others — the mute effect penalty is asymmetric and the silenced member's perception drives the no-show.

**copy_framework_implications**: (1) Name the inputs — copy must reference each member by name and reference at least one of their concrete quiz inputs ('Sam said no Italian', 'Alex weighted speed highest'). Generic 'the group's preferences' fails the value-expressive test. (2) Name the rule — copy must state the aggregation logic in plain language ('we eliminated options no one would commit to, then picked the highest-rated of what remained' — the EBA + Satisficing pipeline narrated). (3) Name the loser — copy must explicitly acknowledge whose top preference did not win and why ('Maya's first pick was The Park Diner, but two members vetoed it'). Naming the loser is the highest-leverage move and the one most products avoid because it feels confrontational; the procedural-justice literature is clear that naming protects the loser by validating their voice. (4) Use second-person for receipts — 'You said you'd be disappointed by Indian tonight. We registered that.' addresses the value-expressive channel directly. (5) Avoid algorithmic distancing — 'the algorithm picked' attributes the decision to a non-relational entity and weakens the effect; 'the group's quiz produced' or 'based on what each of you said' preserves the relational frame. (6) Avoid post-decision voice prompts that cannot be acted on. (7) Tone must be matter-of-fact, not apologetic — apology copy ('sorry your pick didn't win') triggers post-decision negotiation; voice-receipt copy ('your veto eliminated three options') treats the loser as a full participant whose contribution shaped the outcome.

**ux_mechanic_implications**: (1) Per-member voice receipt card on the verdict screen — small chip or row per member showing one or two concrete inputs they registered (top pick, veto, key constraint). This is the structural acknowledgment mechanic. (2) Decision-trail / 'why this won' affordance — collapsible or default-visible reasoning surfacing how votes and vetoes shaped the funnel (EBA elimination visualized as steps, with each step attributable to specific member inputs). (3) Loser-spotlight, not loser-hide — the member whose top preference lost gets a short personalized line, not a hidden treatment. (4) NO post-decision voice field — do not add 'how do you feel about this?' after lock-in unless paired with a real correctability path. (5) Optional correctability window — a brief, time-bounded window in which any member can flag a verdict for re-roll; this satisfies Leventhal's correctability rule and gives voice a real instrumental backstop without re-soliciting endless input. The window must be short (minutes, not hours) and the trigger threshold must be clear (e.g., one veto re-rolls; multiple do not stack). (6) Public ratification mechanic — every member taps to commit (1 tap each), surfacing voice-as-commitment and converting the value-expressive recognition into instrumental lock-in. (7) Receipt asymmetry guard — verdict screen logic must guarantee every member gets a receipt; never render a verdict screen where one member's inputs are not visibly reflected (the mute-effect failure mode). (8) Mute-by-omission audit — when one member submits sparse quiz answers, the system should still show what they DID submit rather than rendering them as silent. (9) Avoid showing exact vote counts per option — surfacing 'Sam was the only one who wanted The Park Diner' can shift voice receipt into status exposure; abstract receipts (named inputs) are safer than tallies.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the verdict screen ignores voice acknowledgment, the predicted failure pattern is: (1) winners follow through normally; (2) the outcome-loser perceives the verdict as algorithmically imposed and shows degraded follow-through (no-show, late, low-energy attendance); (3) repeated experience teaches the loser-position member that the product silences their preference, producing learned disengagement and lower future quiz participation (gaming with 'I'm fine with anything' answers, which collapses the signal that makes the verdict good); (4) friend-group damage when silencing reads as relational rather than procedural; (5) frustration-effect activation if voice is invited (quiz is filled out) but not visibly received (verdict screen ignores inputs) — trust in the product drops below baseline. The aggregate north-star metric (follow-through %) is dominated by the loser-side margin, so failure here propagates directly into the product's primary KPI. Worst-case combined failure: ignored voice plus opaque verdict (no informational justice) plus no correctability — the verdict screen reads as an algorithmic edict and the product converges on the same low-follow-through equilibrium as offline group decisions.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Must-have for verdict screen. Rationale: voice effect operates strongest on the outcome-loser, the loser is the bottleneck for follow-through, and the voice-receipt mechanic is structurally cheap (the inputs already exist from the quiz; the work is rendering them on the verdict screen). Pairs tightly with informational justice (account of why) and Leventhal correctability (re-roll window).

**dependency_on_other_constructs**: Substitutes partially for, and amplifies, several others. (a) Substitutes for Tyler group-value model relational signals when no human authority is present — voice receipts carry the group-status signal that an authority would otherwise carry. (b) Amplifies informational justice — voice-received receipts and 'why this won' explanations are complementary halves of the same fair-process bundle; alone, voice without explanation risks frustration; explanation without voice risks alienation. (c) Amplifies Brockner outcome-favorability × PJ interaction directly — voice is the primary PJ mechanism that buffers loser response. (d) Requires Leventhal correctability as a backstop for high-stakes failures (rare verdicts the loser cannot absorb); without correctability, voice can read as decorative. (e) Conflicts with naive public ratification if ratification is forced before voice receipt (members ratify under pressure rather than after acknowledgment). (f) Substitutes for distributive-equity rules when the distribution rule itself is contested — voice can carry acceptance even when no equity rule satisfies all parties.

**interaction_with_p1_p2**: Interacts strongly and constructively with the chosen P1 hybrid (EBA + Satisficing). EBA is procedurally interpretable as a series of voice-driven elimination steps — each elimination is attributable to a specific member's veto or low rating, which makes the EBA pipeline natively narrate-able as voice. The verdict screen can present the EBA funnel as 'voice in action' ('Sam's veto removed Indian; Maya's budget cap removed steakhouse; remaining 3 options scored above the satisficing threshold; top one wins'). This is rare structural luck — the chosen framework is procedurally legible in voice terms. Satisficing's threshold logic also helps: the threshold pass/fail can be framed as 'the verdict is one we collectively said we'd commit to', another voice-grounded statement. P2 dominant causes: (a) Misaligned_Preferences — voice effect is the primary fairness-side mitigation; the cause says preferences differ and someone loses, voice effect says the loser accepts the loss when their preference was registered and visibly considered. (b) Social_Friction — voice effect via the quiz is private/parallel, defusing the face cost of voicing preferences offline; the verdict screen voice receipt then converts the private voice into public acknowledgment without the original face cost. (c) Fear_of_Regret_FOMO — voice effect indirectly mitigates regret by reframing 'I didn't get what I wanted' as 'my input shaped the outcome'; this is weaker than a direct regret intervention but additive. Net assessment: voice effect is the highest-leverage fairness construct given the chosen P1 framework, because P1 generates a verdict-pipeline that is structurally narratable as voice, and P2's dominant causes are exactly the ones voice mitigates.
