## Cognitive Load / Working Memory Limits

_Source: `Cognitive_Load.json`_

### Basic Info

**cause_name**: Cognitive Load / Working Memory Limits

**originators**: George A. Miller (working-memory capacity); John Sweller (cognitive load theory); Nelson Cowan (refined capacity estimate)

**origin_year**: 1956 (Miller); 1988 (Sweller); 2001 (Cowan)

**discipline**: Cognitive psychology (working memory, instructional psychology, decision-making)

**one_sentence_summary**: When the number of options, attributes, and constraints a chooser must simultaneously hold and compare exceeds working-memory capacity (roughly 4 chunks per Cowan, up to 7 +/- 2 per Miller), comparison breaks down, the chooser offloads or defers, and the decision stalls.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1: A decision presents multiple options, each with multiple attributes (cuisine, distance, price, vibe, dietary fit, opening hours) plus constraints from each group member. Step 2: To compare options, the chooser must hold option-attribute pairs in working memory simultaneously. Step 3: Working-memory capacity is bounded (Cowan ~4 chunks; Miller 7 +/- 2 for simpler items). Once required chunks exceed capacity, intrinsic load saturates. Step 4: Extraneous load (poorly presented information, scrolling menus, group chat noise, parallel tabs) compounds intrinsic load, leaving no germane-load capacity for actually constructing a preference. Step 5: The cognitive system protects itself by short-circuiting: chooser drops attributes, uses crude heuristics (pick familiar, pick first, pick most recent), defers (loop the question back to the group), or abandons (give up, eat what's home). Step 6: In a group, each member is independently overloaded AND must also model others' overloaded preferences, multiplying the load. The result is the classic 'I don't know, what do you want?' loop, which is a working-memory failure manifesting as decision avoidance.

**behavioral_signature**: Observable signs: (a) members verbalize the loop ('I can't think', 'too many options', 'just pick something'); (b) attribute-dropping mid-discussion (someone mentions a constraint, it gets forgotten two messages later); (c) re-asking already-answered questions inside the same session; (d) reverting to a small recently-used set ('the usual three places'); (e) decision time grows non-linearly with option count; (f) quality of comparison degrades visibly after ~4 options are on the table; (g) members offload by asking a single 'decider' to choose, despite no preference delegation having been agreed; (h) physical signs of fatigue (sighing, phone-checking) appear faster than for emotionally charged decisions. Distinguishes from paradox-of-choice because the complaint is effort ('too much to think about') not regret-anticipation ('what if I pick wrong'); distinguishes from social friction because the bottleneck is internal compute, not interpersonal hedging.

**typical_triggers**: High option count (>4-7 simultaneously visible options), high attribute count per option (cuisine + price + distance + dietary + hours + vibe), parallel constraints from multiple group members (each adds attributes to track), unfamiliar option set (no chunking possible from prior experience), end-of-workday timing (reduced executive function), text-heavy or visually noisy delivery format (long menus, scrolling group chat), simultaneous multi-tasking (deciding while still working/commuting), no shared external scratchpad (each member holds state in their own head).

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Strong and broad. Miller (1956) is one of the most cited papers in psychology (>40,000 citations). Cowan (2001, BBS) replicated and refined the bound to ~4 chunks under conditions preventing rehearsal/chunking; Cowan's review is itself heavily cited (>10,000). Sweller's cognitive load theory has 30+ years of instructional-design empirical support, including hundreds of classroom and lab studies on worked-example, split-attention, and redundancy effects (Sweller, van Merrienboer & Paas 1998, 2019 review in Educational Psychology Review). Direct application to consumer choice: Jacoby, Speller & Kohn (1974) on information overload in product choice; Malhotra (1982) on consumer information load; Lurie (2004) on information structure and choice. Decision-avoidance link: Anderson (2003, Psych Bulletin) 'The psychology of doing nothing' explicitly cites cognitive cost as a driver of choice deferral and status-quo bias. Effect sizes for working-memory limits are large and reliable across paradigms (n-back, complex span, change-detection).

**replication_status**: Miller's 7 +/- 2 was refined rather than refuted: Cowan (2001) showed the true rehearsal-free limit is closer to 4, not overturning the broader claim. Sweller's CLT has been replicated across domains (math, science, programming, language learning, medical training) and adopted into mainstream educational policy. Some specific instructional effects (e.g., expertise reversal, modality effect) are well-replicated meta-analytically (de Jong 2010; Schroeder & Cenkci 2018). Cognitive load measurement instruments (Paas scale, NASA-TLX) replicate with acceptable reliability. The construct as a whole survives the replication crisis better than most psychology constructs because the underlying capacity bound is observed in basic-perception paradigms not subject to social-priming concerns. Open debate is about precise capacity numbers and chunking mechanisms, not about whether a bound exists.

**known_limitations**: (a) Capacity is not a single fixed number: it varies with chunking expertise, individual differences, modality (visual vs auditory), and attentional state. (b) The intrinsic / extraneous / germane tripartite distinction has been criticized as hard to operationalize and partially circular; some authors (e.g., de Jong 2010) argue germane load is not separately measurable. (c) Cognitive-load theory was developed for instruction, and translating to naturalistic consumer/group decisions requires care: real-world deciders chunk, externalize (write things down, look at maps), and do not need to hold all state simultaneously. (d) Effect on decision avoidance specifically is well-established at the population level but individual variability is high. (e) Distinguishing cognitive load from related constructs (decision fatigue, ego depletion, paradox of choice) in field data is difficult; they co-occur and the manipulations overlap.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: Strong fit. The GetToIt use case is precisely the cell where cognitive load bites: 2-6 group members each carry implicit constraints (dietary, budget, distance, recent-history aversion, mood), a food vertical typically presents 5-30 candidate places once you open any aggregator, and end-of-workday timing reduces residual capacity. The number of option-attribute-by-member cells a single decider would need to mentally hold easily exceeds Cowan's ~4-chunk bound (e.g., 6 places x 4 relevant attributes x 4 people = 96 cells, not chunkable for novel options). The classic 'where do you want to eat?' loop is itself a cognitive-load failure pattern: each member tries to mentally simulate the other members' constraints, fails, and pushes the decision back. However, cognitive load is rarely the deepest cause: it is downstream of having too many options and too many silent constraints. If options are pre-pruned and constraints are surfaced, raw load drops fast. So fit is high as a proximal mechanism but the true leverage is in the antecedents (option count, constraint elicitation).

**dominance_estimate**: Medium-High. As a proximal contributor it is likely present in nearly every paralysis episode in this use case, often co-occurring with social friction and choice overload. As the dominant root cause, however, it is medium: in the going-out food context the deeper drivers are usually unspoken constraints (social friction) and option overload (paradox of choice), with cognitive load being the symptom that makes those causes feel unsolvable. Rationale: a group with 3 known-acceptable options and stated constraints decides quickly even when tired; a group with 30 options and silent preferences stalls regardless of how mentally fresh they are. Cognitive load is therefore a high-leverage defuse target because product mechanics that compress options and externalize constraints attack load directly, but it is rarely the headline cause to name.

**group_specificity**: Strongly amplified in groups. In a solo decision the decider holds N option-attribute pairs. In a group of k, each member must hold N options x A attributes x k members' implicit constraints, multiplying load roughly by k. Worse, k-1 of those constraint sets are not directly observable, so each member spends additional capacity simulating the others. Group chat as the comparison surface adds extraneous load (unread messages, threading, gif noise). Capacity is also unequal across members; the lowest-capacity or most-fatigued member often drives the deferral. Empirically, groups exhibit longer time-to-decision than the slowest individual would alone, consistent with super-linear load scaling.

**timing_specificity**: Strongly amplified by end-of-workday timing. Working memory and executive function show measurable diurnal variation and depletion effects (e.g., Sievertsen, Gino & Piovesan 2016 on time-of-day school performance; classic vigilance literature). After a workday of sustained cognitive demand, residual capacity for novel information integration is reduced; chunking still works for familiar categories but novel option sets become harder. End-of-day is also the timing in which deciders most rely on heuristics and defaults, exactly the short-circuiting predicted when load exceeds capacity. The GetToIt target window (~5-7pm food decision after a workday) is therefore a worst-case for the cognitive-load mechanism specifically.

### Quiz Signal Implication

**signal_to_extract**: Primary: option-cap (compress the option set to 2-3 finalists before the chooser holds them in mind). Secondary: hard constraints (dietary, distance, budget, hours) so the system can pre-filter rather than asking the brain to filter. Tertiary: a single mood/vibe axis (cheap-fast vs sit-down; familiar vs adventurous) so attributes collapse to one dimension. The quiz should NOT extract rich preference profiles, ranked attribute weights, or open-ended descriptions of what people 'feel like' in free text. Each of those re-imports the load the quiz is supposed to remove.

**question_design_implications**: (a) Closed, low-cardinality questions (2-4 choices) over open text. (b) One attribute per question, never a multi-attribute matrix. (c) Hard-constraint questions framed as veto/exclude (cheaper cognitively than ranking), e.g. 'anything you can't eat tonight?' rather than 'rate your interest in 8 cuisines'. (d) Use chunkable categories aligned with everyday food schemas (cuisine families, distance bands) rather than novel taxonomies. (e) Order questions from cheapest (constraints) to richest (mood) so capacity is spent where it adds most signal. (f) Carry no state across questions that the user must remember; the system holds the state. (g) Strict 5-question cap is itself a cognitive-load defuse: bounds the perceived effort and prevents per-attribute matrix blow-up. (h) Visual presentation: one question per screen, no scrolling, no backtracking required.

**mitigation_strategy**: Product mechanics that directly defuse cognitive load: (1) Option compression: pre-rank candidates from constraints and present at most 2-3 finalists, never a long list. (2) Constraint externalization: each member's vetoes/limits are stored by the system, removing the need to mentally model others. (3) Single-axis collapse: convert multi-attribute comparison into a single tonight-axis (e.g., 'low-effort vs treat-yourself'). (4) Default + opt-out: produce a verdict and ask members to ratify rather than choose. (5) Deadline / forcing function: short timer prevents the load from spiraling as members re-read the chat. (6) Anonymous parallel input (the existing parallel-quiz mechanic): each member answers in isolation, removing the load of simulating others' answers in real time. (7) Schema reuse: lean on familiar food categories rather than novel attributes. (8) No backtracking: locking each answer prevents working-memory rehearsal cost. (9) Suppress extraneous load: minimal UI, no marketing copy, no menu-browsing inside the flow.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the quiz adds rather than removes load (long questions, open text, multi-attribute matrices, big option lists shown before a decision), the verdict will be produced but follow-through collapses: members will not have actually formed preferences, only clicked through, and at commitment time the unresolved comparison resurfaces ('actually, can we do somewhere else?'). Verdicts will be technically issued but emotionally un-owned, producing renegotiation, late drop-outs, or silent no-shows. North-star metric (verdicts followed through) is directly damaged. Secondary failure: cognitively overloaded members default to the loudest member's preference, which masquerades as agreement and breaks down on arrival.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Primary defuse target (architectural). Cognitive load is not the headline narrative cause but it sets the ceiling on what the quiz mechanic can do; every product decision (option count, question count, question form, UI density) is a cognitive-load decision. Treat as a constant constraint on the design rather than a feature.

**dependency_on_other_causes**: Amplifies and is amplified by every other cause. Amplifies Paradox of Choice: large option sets first overload working memory, then trigger choice-overload regret. Amplifies Social Friction: members who can't hold the comparison default to accommodation and silence, masking preferences. Amplifies Decision Fatigue: end-of-day depletion lowers capacity, making the same option set feel harder. Substitutes for Misaligned Preferences as a perceived cause: groups will report 'too many options' when the real issue is unspoken disagreement, because cognitive-effort framing is more socially acceptable than admitting conflict. Mitigating cognitive load (option compression + constraint externalization) therefore yields compounding gains across the other causes.

## Decision Fatigue / Ego Depletion

_Source: `Decision_Fatigue.json`_

### Basic Info

**cause_name**: Decision Fatigue / Ego Depletion

**originators**: Roy F. Baumeister, Ellen Bratslavsky, Mark Muraven, Dianne M. Tice (1998 — seminal ego depletion paper); Kathleen D. Vohs et al. (2008 — extension to choice/decision-making specifically). Applied for real-world decision fatigue by Shai Danziger, Jonathan Levav, Liora Avnaim-Pesso (2011 PNAS Israeli parole judges study). Major critics and revisers: Michael Inzlicht and Brandon Schmeichel (motivation/attention reframe, 2012-2021); Evan Carter and Michael McCullough (bias-corrected meta-analyses, 2014-2015); Miguel Vadillo (glucose-mechanism takedown, 2016); Malte Friese and David Loschelder (Friese, Loschelder, Gieseler, Frankenbach & Inzlicht 2019 review of arguments).

**origin_year**: 1998 (Baumeister et al. JPSP — ego depletion); 2008 (Vohs et al. JPSP — choice as depletion); 2011 (Danziger et al. PNAS — applied/judicial). Replication-crisis turning points: 2014-2016 (Carter meta-analyses, Hagger 2016 RRR, Vadillo 2016 glucose). Most recent canonical multi-lab tests: Vohs et al. 2021 (Psychological Science) and Dang et al. 2021 (Social Psychological and Personality Science). Most recent applied null: Lindh et al. 2025 (Communications Psychology) on healthcare decisions.

**discipline**: Social psychology (self-regulation/willpower tradition); applied extensions in behavioral economics, consumer psychology, judgment-and-decision-making, and health-services research.

**one_sentence_summary**: Each act of choosing is theorized to draw down a limited self-regulation resource so that decisions made later in a sequence (or later in the day) become lower quality, more impulsive, more default-driven, or get avoided altogether — but after a decade of replication failures the strong-form 'limited willpower resource' claim no longer holds, and the everyday phenomenology of late-day low-quality choices is now more credibly explained by motivation, attention, or fatigue-affect mechanisms than by depletion of a shared resource.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Classical resource model (Baumeister/Vohs): (1) Self-control and deliberate choice draw on a single, limited, glucose-linked resource ('willpower'). (2) Each effortful choice depletes the pool. (3) As the pool drops, downstream decisions show one of three patterns: (a) avoidance/deferral ('I don't care, you pick'), (b) reliance on defaults / status-quo / heuristics, (c) impulsive or low-effort choice (cheap option, familiar option, first option). (4) Replenishment requires rest, glucose, or positive affect. Applied to GetToIt's end-of-workday food decision: a group arrives at the choice already drained from a day of work decisions, so the marginal cost of weighing one more option (cuisine, venue, dietary constraints, distance) feels disproportionately high, and the group defaults to deferral, recycling 'the usual,' or capitulating to the loudest voice. Revised motivation/attention model (Inzlicht & Schmeichel 2012; Inzlicht, Werner, Briskin & Roberts 2021 Annual Review of Psychology; Lin, Saunders, Friese, Evans & Inzlicht 2020 Psychological Science): people don't run out of willpower, they re-allocate effort away from tasks they perceive as no longer worth the cost, especially when fatigued, bored, or unrewarded — fatigue is a motivational signal, not a tank emptying. Dang et al. 2021 multi-lab data also showed self-reported fatigue (not depletion per se) was the variable that tracked subsequent self-control performance, consistent with the motivation/affect view. Newer system-level accounts (Yang et al. 2025 'depletion or activation' Acta Psychologica) propose nonlinear dynamics where activation and depletion compete based on task duration, intensity, and individual traits. Either way, the observable end-state at end-of-workday — degraded choice quality, avoidance, defaulting — is real; only the underlying mechanism is contested, and the 'shared limited resource' framing has lost the bulk of empirical support.

**behavioral_signature**: Late-in-day or late-in-sequence decisions where: (a) participants explicitly disclaim ('I don't care, just pick something'), (b) verdict converges on a familiar default rather than a deliberated optimum, (c) requests for more information drop sharply ('whatever's closest is fine'), (d) groups skip the comparison step they would normally run when fresh, (e) physical/affective signs of fatigue (yawning, slumping, irritability, hunger complaints) precede the avoidance, (f) the same group, asked the same question at lunchtime, deliberates more thoroughly. Distinguishable from Choice Overload because it is sequence/time-driven, not option-count-driven; distinguishable from Social Friction because the avoidance is articulated as 'I'm too tired to care' rather than 'I don't want to impose.' Applied/field signature in real settings: small but observable late-shift drift toward defaults — e.g., GP antibiotic prescribing increased 8.7% per 15 additional patient encounters in Australian BEACH data (Linder-style decision-fatigue pattern); in contrast, Lindh et al. 2025 found NO such pattern in Swedish telephone-triage decisions (BF0+ > 22 for the null), suggesting the signature is real in some applied contexts but absent in others.

**typical_triggers**: End of workday/workweek; after a long string of prior decisions (shopping, work, parenting); low blood glucose / pre-meal hunger states; sleep debt; high cognitive load earlier in the day; decisions stacked back-to-back without breaks. For GetToIt specifically: the canonical trigger window — 5pm-7pm Friday, group of coworkers/friends already drained from a workday, hungry, asked to pick a restaurant — is the textbook decision-fatigue scenario regardless of whether the underlying mechanism is resource depletion or motivation-shift. Trigger evidence from food-decision literature specifically: Tudoran 2025 narrative review (Nutrients) explicitly identifies sequential food choice as a domain where decision quality declines through the day, with deliberative-to-automatic shifts visible by mid-afternoon.

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Originally very strong-looking, now substantially deflated. Pre-replication crisis: hundreds of published studies, original Hagger, Wood, Stiff & Chatzisarantis 2010 meta-analysis reported d = 0.62 across 83 studies (Psychological Bulletin). Replication-corrected effect sizes from large preregistered multi-lab efforts: (1) Hagger et al. 2016 Registered Replication Report (Perspectives on Psychological Science), 23 labs, N ≈ 2,141: d = 0.04, 95% CI [-0.07, 0.15] — null. (2) Carter, Kofler, Forster & McCullough 2015 (JEP:General) bias-corrected meta-analysis: g range -0.27 to 0.24, effect indistinguishable from zero after PET-PEESE correction. (3) Dang 2018 (Psychological Research) update: argued PET-PEESE was misapplied; using only 'reliable' depleting tasks (attention essay, emotion video, Stroop) the effect appeared nonzero but based on small k. (4) Dang et al. 2021 (Soc Psych Personality Sci) preregistered 12-lab replication with Stroop -> antisaccade paradigm, N = 1,775: d = 0.10 overall (small/significant), d = 0.16 after excluding random responders, 95% CI [0.05, 0.26] — small but nonzero. (5) Vohs et al. 2021 (Psychological Science) 'Multisite Preregistered Paradigmatic Test', 36 labs, N = 3,531: confirmatory test d = 0.06 nonsignificant, exploratory d = 0.08 marginally significant; Bayesian analysis 4x in favor of null over an informed-prior alternative (δ = 0.30); described by authors and commentators as inconclusive but tilting null. (6) Lindh et al. 2025 (Communications Psychology) 'No evidence for decision fatigue using large-scale field data from healthcare', national Swedish telephone-triage data with quasi-random scheduling: BF0+ > 22 in favor of the null on all main tests — strongest applied null to date. Net: across the most recent five years, depletion is at best a small-and-fragile lab effect (d = 0.06-0.16 in best-powered tests, often null) and frequently absent in real-world applied data. The motivation/affect reframe survives better than the resource model.

**replication_status**: Strong-form 'limited willpower resource' claim has effectively failed. Timeline of milestones: (1) Carter & McCullough 2013/2014 — re-analysis flagging publication bias. (2) Carter, Kofler, Forster & McCullough 2015 (JEP:General) — bias-corrected meta-analysis, effect ~0. (3) Hagger et al. 2016 RRR (PPS) — 23-lab, Baumeister-vetted protocol, d = 0.04, null. (4) Vadillo, Gold & Osman 2016 (Psychological Science) — p-curve analysis on glucose-willpower studies; evidential value 'weak,' likely publication bias. (5) Friese, Loschelder, Gieseler, Frankenbach & Inzlicht 2019 (Personality and Social Psychology Review) — 'Is Ego Depletion Real?' analyzes whether each side's evidence would convince the other; concludes the answer for both sides is no, the field is at an impasse on the resource model. (6) Lin, Saunders, Friese, Evans & Inzlicht 2020 (Psychological Science) — strong effort manipulations reduce response caution, supporting motivation/attention not resource account. (7) Dang et al. 2021 multi-lab — d ≈ 0.10-0.16, small but nonzero (the 'depletion-survives' camp's strongest recent positive). (8) Vohs et al. 2021 multi-site (36 labs) — Bayesian evidence 4x for null. (9) Lindh et al. 2025 healthcare field data — strong null in real-world setting. (10) Yang et al. 2025 (Acta Psychologica) — argues the linear depletion model is wrong by construction; depletion and activation interact dynamically. (11) Inzlicht's own public summary ('The Collapse of Ego Depletion' Substack, 2024) confirms the field has largely abandoned the resource model. The Danziger 2011 parole-judge finding remains contested: Glöckner 2016 simulation showing the apparent fatigue pattern could arise as a scheduling artifact (favorable rulings take longer, lawyered cases cluster) was never empirically rebutted; subsequent applied studies in judicial contexts (Torres & Williams 2022 bail proceedings; pretrial-release studies 2022-2023) find at most small, inconsistent effects, and several find none. Net: weak/contested for individual depletion in lab; mostly null in well-powered applied tests; the everyday phenomenology of late-day worse choices is real but more credibly motivational/affective than resource-depletive.

**known_limitations**: (1) Construct validity — 'depletion' was operationalized inconsistently across the original literature (Stroop, attention, e-crossing, choice tasks all assumed equivalent); Dang 2018 showed effect size depended heavily on which depleting task was chosen. (2) Publication bias — original literature has small-study/file-drawer signatures (Carter et al. 2015; Vadillo et al. 2016). (3) Lab-task generalization — even when lab effects appeared, mapping them to consequential real-world choices was always speculative; Lindh et al. 2025 healthcare null sharpens this concern. (4) Glucose mechanism (Gailliot/Baumeister 2007) is widely rejected; Vadillo et al. 2016 p-curve found weak evidential value, no credible 2020+ revival in the literature. (5) Mechanism ambiguity — even researchers who still see real fatigue effects disagree on whether it's resource, motivation, attention, or mood; the field has largely conceded to the motivation/attention camp (Inzlicht et al. 2021 Annual Review). (6) Individual differences and demand characteristics — Job, Dweck & Walton 2010 showed people who don't believe willpower is limited don't show depletion effects; this is now interpreted as evidence against the resource model and in favor of expectancy/motivation explanations. (7) Linear-model assumption critiqued — Yang et al. 2025 argues depletion and activation interact nonlinearly with task duration and intensity, making constant-effect-size questions wrong-headed. (8) For groups specifically (GetToIt's case): the 2025 Frontiers systematic review of ego depletion in group-based hierarchical relations (Genç Turhan & Kuşdil, 35 articles + 4 dissertations through Feb 2022) covers intergroup contact and stereotype contexts (e.g., dominant-group members suppressing bias, subordinate-group members coping with stereotype threat) but does NOT cover small-group consensus decision-making of the sort GetToIt addresses. The applied 'shared depletion in a small friend group choosing dinner' question remains essentially unstudied empirically.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: The phenomenology fits the GetToIt scenario almost perfectly — end-of-workday, hungry, group asked to pick a restaurant, classic 'I don't care, you pick' deflection, default to the same three places. But the mechanism explanation is shaky given replication failures and the 2025 healthcare null. The actionable read: treat the late-day-low-energy-low-motivation state as a real environmental constraint the product must accommodate (because users will arrive in it), but don't build the product on the assumption that depletion is a clean dose-response willpower drain. Better framing, supported by the surviving motivation/attention literature: at end of workday, users have low motivation to deliberate, low tolerance for friction, and high willingness to accept a 'good enough' verdict — which is almost an ideal psychological state for a tool that compresses choice into a 5-question quiz and returns a single verdict. This aligns with the food-choice review evidence (Tudoran 2025 Nutrients) showing afternoon/evening decisions shift toward automatic, convenience, and habit-based patterns. Decision fatigue / motivation-fatigue is less the cause of paralysis here than the precondition that makes the GetToIt value proposition land.

**dominance_estimate**: Low-to-medium as a *cause* of paralysis (because the strong-form mechanism does not hold up under post-2020 replication evidence), but high as a *contextual amplifier* of whichever cause is actually dominant. Rationale: replication failures (Hagger 2016, Vohs 2021, Lindh 2025) mean we cannot claim depletion drives paralysis. However, the timing context (end-of-workday, post-work, hungry) is the use case, and that context reliably amplifies Social Friction (too tired to negotiate), Misaligned Preferences (too tired to compromise), and Choice Overload (too tired to compare). So decision fatigue is best modeled as a state-multiplier on other causes rather than a standalone cause to defuse. Classification verdict from P2 synthesis stands: state-multiplier, not standalone cause. The 2025 evidence (Lindh null in healthcare; Yang nonlinear-dynamics argument) reinforces rather than challenges this classification — if anything it makes the resource-depletion framing even less safe to build product mechanics on.

**timing_specificity**: Strongly amplified by end-of-workday timing — this is essentially the operational definition of the construct. The effect (real or apparent) is by construction time-of-day and sequence-position dependent. End of workday + hungry + group + going-out is the stacked condition under which whatever-is-actually-going-on shows up most clearly. Empirical support specifically for time-of-day effects in food decisions: Tudoran 2025 narrative review documents an afternoon-to-evening drift toward automatic, convenience, habit-shaped food choice; classic and current consumer research on menu fatigue confirms time-pressed evening dining produces shorter menu scans and heavier reliance on social cues. So even if the underlying mechanism is motivational rather than depletive, the timing effect is robust enough to design around.

### Quiz Signal Implication

**signal_to_extract**: Low-effort commitment threshold and default-acceptance signal. Specifically: (1) the group's tolerance for a 'good enough' verdict (vs. demand for an optimum) — high tolerance at end of day, the quiz should detect and lean into it; (2) hard constraints only (dietary, distance, budget veto) — fatigued users won't reliably surface preferences but will surface vetos; (3) commitment readiness — are they ready to lock a verdict now, or do they need a 60-second buffer? (4) energy/mood state — to calibrate option-set size and how many tradeoffs the verdict involves. The motivation/attention reframe specifically suggests probing willingness-to-engage rather than capacity-to-deliberate.

**question_design_implications**: Design questions for the fatigued/low-motivation user, not the deliberative one. Implications: (1) Bias toward veto/exclusion questions over preference/ranking questions — 'anything you absolutely can't eat tonight?' is cheaper than 'rank your top three cuisines.' (2) Use binary or thumbs-up/thumbs-down formats over sliders or rankings. (3) Surface defaults aggressively — 'last time you picked X, want to repeat?' lets fatigued users one-tap. (4) Cap question count hard (5 is right; do not let it grow). (5) Set a per-question time budget, but don't enforce it visibly (visible timers add load); use it as a signal to simplify the next question. (6) Avoid open text input late in the flow. (7) Frame the final commit as low-stakes ('you can always change next time') to reduce the cost of locking. (8) Lean into the motivation-shift framing: don't ask users to deliberate, ask them to ratify or veto a candidate the system surfaced — this matches the actual psychological state late-day users are in.

**mitigation_strategy**: (1) Option compression — return ONE verdict, not a shortlist; fatigued groups cannot rank a shortlist. (2) Deadline / forced commit — short quiz window, forced verdict at end, prevents post-quiz re-deliberation that re-opens fatigue. (3) Smart default — if signal is weak, fall back to a 'usual' or recency-based pick rather than asking another question. (4) Anonymous parallel input — removes the social-coordination cost on top of decision cost; everyone answers on their own phone, no group meta-discussion. (5) Veto-first design — collect deal-breakers before preferences; eliminates options without requiring preference articulation. (6) Mood/energy probe early — let the product detect the fatigued state and adapt question count, formality, and option-set size. (7) Pre-commit framing — 'we'll go with whatever this picks' before the quiz starts, so the verdict has social legitimacy without re-deliberation. (8) Avoid asking for justifications — fatigued users cannot generate them. (9) Frame the quiz outcome as a small reward (verdict, settled question, early start to dinner) — this directly engages the motivation account: re-recruit attention via reward signal rather than fighting depletion.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the quiz assumes a fresh, deliberative user, the product fails three ways at end of workday: (a) users abandon mid-quiz because each question feels like one more decision they didn't want to make; (b) users complete the quiz but ignore the verdict because the quiz felt like work without payoff; (c) the verdict is technically correct on stated preferences but ignores the meta-preference of 'I just wanted this to be over,' so the group revises post-verdict and follow-through drops. North-star metric (verdicts followed-through) takes a direct hit. Worst case: the product becomes one more decision-burden rather than a decision-replacer, and users churn. Note that under the motivation/attention reframe this failure mode is even more acute — fatigued users are not running out of capacity, they are actively reallocating effort away from things that feel unrewarding, so a quiz that feels like work gets disengaged from on purpose.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Secondary defuse target — frame as a state/context the quiz must respect (fatigued users, low-effort UX, veto-first questions, single verdict, fast commit) rather than a cause to debunk via the quiz mechanism itself. The primary causes to defuse with the quiz signal are likely Social Friction and Misaligned Preferences; decision fatigue's role is to dictate the *form* of the quiz (short, low-effort, default-friendly, reward-framed) rather than its *content*.

**dependency_on_other_causes**: Acts as a state-multiplier rather than an independent cause. Amplifies: Social Friction (too tired to negotiate face-saving moves), Misaligned Preferences (too tired to compromise or trade), Choice Overload (reduces tolerance for option comparison), Cognitive Load (reduces working-memory headroom). Masks: Fear of Regret (fatigued users care less about the un-chosen option, which is paradoxically helpful for commitment). Substitutes for: nothing cleanly. Practical implication: building for the fatigued state is a no-regret design move — it helps with every other cause too. The post-2020 motivation/attention reframe strengthens this: every other cause is harder to resolve when users have actively withdrawn motivational engagement, so designs that re-recruit attention (clear rewards, low friction, fast commit) attack the precondition that makes every other paralysis cause worse.

## Anticipated Regret / FOMO

_Source: `Fear_of_Regret_FOMO.json`_

### Basic Info

**cause_name**: Anticipated Regret / FOMO

**originators**: Graham Loomes & Robert Sugden (regret theory, Economic Journal 1982); David E. Bell (parallel formulation, Operations Research 1982); Marcel Zeelenberg & Rik Pieters (regret regulation theory, J. Consumer Psychology 2007); Itamar Simonson (anticipated regret in consumer choice, JCR 1992); Christopher J. Anderson (decision-avoidance review, Psych Bulletin 2003); Andrew K. Przybylski, Kou Murayama, Cody DeHaan & Valerie Gladwell (FOMO scale, Computers in Human Behavior 2013).

**origin_year**: 1982 (Loomes & Sugden regret theory; Bell regret in decision under uncertainty); 1992 (Simonson — first applied consumer-choice demonstration of anticipated regret); 2003 (Anderson decision-avoidance review consolidating regret aversion as a primary deferral driver); 2007 (Zeelenberg & Pieters regret regulation 1.0); 2013 (Przybylski FOMO scale).

**discipline**: Behavioral economics (Loomes & Sugden, Bell); consumer psychology / social psychology (Zeelenberg, Pieters, Inman, Simonson); decision-making and self-regulation (Anderson, Beattie); social-media / motivational psychology (Przybylski FOMO and the post-2013 FOMO literature).

**one_sentence_summary**: Decision-makers compare each option against the imagined best outcome of options they did not pick, and the prospective pain of that comparison — combined, in the FOMO variant, with the salience of foregone simultaneous experiences — makes them defer, hedge, or refuse to commit.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1: chooser mentally simulates outcomes of each available option, including counterfactual outcomes of un-chosen options (the regret/rejoicing term Loomes & Sugden 1982 and Bell 1982 add to expected utility). Step 2: the simulated negative affect of 'choosing X and then learning Y would have been better' is weighted asymmetrically — regret looms larger than rejoicing, parallel to loss aversion. Step 3: when options are roughly equivalent in expected value but each is uniquely best on some salient dimension, every choice produces a guaranteed counterfactual regret cell vs. some rival, generating a no-win frame. Step 4: to escape ownership of that anticipated regret, the chooser delays, defers, asks others to choose, or picks the status-quo / default (Anderson 2003 review identifies four avoidance forms — choice deferral, status quo bias, omission bias, inaction inertia — all linked to anticipated regret as a primary antecedent; Beattie, Baron, Hershey & Spranca 1994 show people prefer not to choose when choice generates regret risk; Tykocinski & Pittman 1995 inaction inertia, replicated with mixed pre-registered support by Chen et al. 2020). Step 5 (group amplification): in groups, the un-chosen options are simultaneously what other members wanted, so anticipated regret bundles with anticipated social blame ('we should have done what Sam suggested'); empirically, 3-person groups defer choice ~3.5x as often as their individual members on identical job-candidate stimuli (Nijstad 2008/2008 re-analyzed by White et al.: 42% group deferral vs. 12% individual deferral; n=222 across 74 groups), and a separate 174-group experiment found a smaller but robust effect (groups 10.5% deferral vs. individuals 8.7%, d≈0.2). Step 6 (FOMO timing layer, Przybylski 2013): when a chosen activity forecloses simultaneous alternatives (Friday night, end-of-day food window), anticipated regret over the foregone experiences is heightened by their concreteness and time-pressure — the apprehension-of-rewarding-experiences-elsewhere component of the FOMO construct. Step 7 (reversibility coupling, Gilbert & Ebert 2002 / Bullens & van Harreveld 2016 / Liu et al. 2022 PRBM): if the choice remains reversible, the psychological-immune-system rationalization of the chosen option is suppressed; counterfactual thinking and anticipated regret remain elevated, depressing satisfaction and re-opening the deferral cycle.

**behavioral_signature**: Tell-tale signs this cause is dominant rather than (e.g.) information overload or preference uncertainty: (1) the group can articulate that any of the candidate options would be 'fine' but still won't lock; (2) decision quality concerns are framed counterfactually ('but what if we go to A and B turns out to be packed/great') rather than absolutely ('I don't know if A is good'); (3) members keep adding options or re-opening the option set rather than narrowing (option-keeping as regret-hedging); (4) someone says 'you decide' / 'I don't care' to displace ownership of the future regret (responsibility-shedding, Beattie et al. 1994); (5) the group locks faster when an option becomes unavailable — closing the counterfactual collapses the regret comparison (the canonical 'restaurant is fully booked, now we're decisive' pattern); (6) post-decision regret discourse and switching/cancellation behavior, especially when reversibility is preserved (Bullens & van Harreveld 2016); (7) heightened deliberation when un-chosen outcomes will be observable ex post (will see friends' Instagram of the other party / will walk past the other restaurant); (8) verbal markers consistent with the FOMO construct — 'fear of missing', 'don't want to miss out', desire-to-stay-connected language (Elhai, Yang & Montag 2020 review).

**typical_triggers**: Roughly equivalent options each dominant on a different attribute (food: cuisine A is closer, B is novel, C is cheaper); options that foreclose each other (can't do dinner AND drinks at fully different venues in the same window); observability of un-chosen outcomes (will see friends' Instagram / will walk past the other restaurant); group composition where each member has a different favorite so any choice disappoints someone specific (social regret); end-of-day / weekend timing when the slot is scarce and non-recoverable; presence of a 'novel' option whose outcome is unknown (max regret variance); explicit deadlines visible to the group; reversible commitments that keep the counterfactual alive (Gilbert & Ebert 2002); high-FOMO individuals (top-quartile Przybylski FoMOs scale) and Generation Z / young-adult samples (Tandon et al. 2021 systematic review highlighting FoMO prevalence in Gen Z and young adults).

### Evidence Base

**empirical_support**: Strong and longitudinal across multiple paradigms with quantified effect sizes. (1) Anticipated-regret -> behavior: Brewer et al. (2016) Health Psychology meta-analysis of 81 studies (n=45,618) found anticipated regret strongly associated with intentions (r+ = .50, p<.001) and actual health behavior (r+ = .29, p<.001); Sandberg & Conner (2008) British Journal of Social Psychology meta-analysis (k=25) showed anticipated regret adds ~7% explained variance in intention beyond TPB constructs. Both meta-analyses confirm action-vs-inaction asymmetry: action-regret weakens, inaction-regret strengthens behavior. (2) Decision avoidance: Han, Quadflieg & Ludwig (2023, PLoS ONE) multilevel meta-analysis (13 studies, 59 effect sizes) — overall avoidance->lower regret was Hedges' g = -0.23 (95% CI [-0.48, 0.01], p=0.063), but status-quo preservation specifically yielded a reliable medium effect (g = -0.45, p=0.006), confirming that defaulting/deferring is regret-protective. The same review noted that no studies of pure choice deferral met inclusion, marking a gap. (3) Group deferral: White et al. (Cambridge JDM) re-analyzed Nijstad 2008 (n=222 across 74 three-person groups) showing groups defer 42% vs. individuals 12% on identical stimuli, plus a new 174-group experiment confirming the direction (10.5% vs. 8.7%, d≈0.2). (4) FOMO-internet/behavior link: Akbari, Seydavi, Palmieri, Mansueto, Caselli & Spada (2021, J. Behavioral Addictions) meta-analyzed 86 effect sizes representing 55,134 participants; trait-FoMO -> internet-use correlations ranged r=0.11-0.63, with COVID-19 the only significant moderator (strengthening the relation). (5) Inaction inertia: Chen et al. (2020, SPPS) preregistered replications of Tykocinski & Pittman 1995 supported the effect in car and ski-resort scenarios but found weak/mixed support in frequent-flyer and fitness scenarios — robust but bounded. (6) Reversibility/regret coupling: Liu et al. (2022, Psychology Research and Behavior Management) showed reversible decisions produce significantly more counterfactual thinking and anticipated regret, mediating the satisfaction drop relative to irreversible decisions. Effect sizes for the canonical regret-aversion choice-deferral paradigms are typically d ~ 0.3-0.6 (small-to-medium).

**replication_status**: Generally robust, with notable refinements rather than failures. (1) Anticipated regret -> intentions/behavior: replicated meta-analytically twice (Sandberg & Conner 2008; Brewer et al. 2016), and Brewer's larger and more recent estimate is broadly consistent with Sandberg & Conner. No replication crisis specific to anticipated regret. (2) Action-vs-inaction asymmetry (Kahneman & Tversky 1982 short-term, Gilovich & Medvec 1995 long-term reversal): replicated and refined; Brewer et al. confirm direction at meta-analytic scale. (3) Inaction inertia: Chen et al. (2020) preregistered replications in three US/Hong-Kong samples — original Tykocinski & Pittman 1995 effects replicated cleanly in two of four scenarios, partially in others; not a failure but a boundary refinement. The reference-dependence/query-theory account (Gold et al. 2023, PLoS ONE) gives the effect a stronger mechanistic foundation. (4) FOMO scale (Przybylski et al. 2013): widely replicated and translated. The original unidimensional structure has been validated in multiple samples (Can & Satici 2019; Gil et al. 2015; Correa-Rojas et al. 2020; Núñez et al. 2022); Casale & Fioravanti (2020) proposed and validated a competing 10-item two-factor model (fear-of-missing-rewarding-experiences vs. cognitions-and-behaviors-related-to-missing-out) that has been replicated in several adaptations. So factor structure is contested but the underlying construct replicates. (5) FOMO -> outcomes: Akbari et al. 2021 meta-analysis (k=86, n=55,134) confirms FOMO-internet-use link replicates broadly with substantial heterogeneity. (6) Group choice deferral: replicated by White et al. across re-analyzed and new samples. (7) Reversibility/regret: Gilbert & Ebert 2002 photo paradigm; Bullens & van Harreveld 2016 empirical overview; Liu et al. 2022 mediation replication — direction is robust though magnitude varies and individual differences (maximizers vs. satisficers) moderate it. Net: the regret-aversion -> deferral chain is one of the better-replicating areas in behavioral decision research.

**known_limitations**: (1) Original axiomatic regret theory (Loomes & Sugden 1982) predicts intransitive cycles in some setups; the formal theory has been largely absorbed into prospect-theory-with-counterfactual-comparisons rather than standing alone in modern behavioral econ. (2) Boundary condition: regret aversion attenuates when un-chosen-option outcomes will not be learned (no feedback weakens anticipated regret) — partially relevant to GetToIt because group decisions usually DO produce observable foregone outcomes (someone posts the other restaurant). (3) FOMO factor structure is contested between the Przybylski 2013 unidimensional model and the Casale & Fioravanti 2020 two-factor model; pick a side carefully if scoring. (4) FOMO discriminant validity: trait-FOMO correlates ~r=.29 with neuroticism (Elhai et al. 2020), and overlaps with social anxiety and need-to-belong; predictive validity beyond those is mixed. The Akbari et al. 2021 meta-analysis found NO meaningful FOMO link to depression, anxiety, or life-satisfaction symptoms once aggregated — suggesting FOMO predicts behaviors (checking, deferral) more than affective endpoints. (5) Han et al. 2023 meta-analysis exposed publication bias (Egger's z = -2.07, p=0.038) and noted that prior negative outcomes flip the avoidance->regret relationship strongly positive (g = 1.40), so avoidance is not regret-protective in repeat-failure contexts. (6) Cross-cultural variation in action/inaction patterns (Eastern vs. WEIRD samples) is real but not dramatic for our use case. (7) In groups, anticipated regret confounds with responsibility-aversion and blame-avoidance — the 'I don't want to be the one who picked the bad place' effect is partly social, not purely regret-theoretic. (8) Hard to distinguish in field data from preference-construction (Slovic 1995). (9) Choice-deferral specifically has thin direct meta-analytic coverage — Han et al. 2023 explicitly noted no studies of pure choice deferral met their inclusion criteria, leaving a gap our use case sits squarely in.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: Very high. The GetToIt food/going-out scenario is essentially a regret-engineering crucible: (a) options are roughly equivalent on overall expected enjoyment but each is best on some dimension, so every pick guarantees a counterfactual regret cell; (b) un-chosen outcomes are observable (we walk past the other restaurant, friends post it, the other movie's reviews surface) — maximizing anticipated regret per Zeelenberg's feedback-conditioning result; (c) group setting bundles social regret (someone wanted the other thing) with personal regret, and group choice deferral has 3.5x the rate of individual deferral on matched stimuli (Nijstad/White et al.); (d) end-of-workday / Friday-night timing makes the slot scarce and non-recoverable, intensifying FOMO over alternative uses of the evening — directly aligned with the Przybylski 2013 / Tandon 2021 / Akbari 2021 FOMO-prevalence findings in young adults; (e) mid-stakes — high enough to bother regretting, low enough that nobody wants to formally adjudicate, exactly the regime where avoidance / deferral wins (Anderson 2003); (f) groups of 2-6 hit the sweet spot where each member has a non-trivial probability of being the lone disappointed party; (g) the canonical field artifacts — Yelp browsing loops, group chats that die at the 'so where?' message, the 'idk you pick' / 'no you pick' loop — all map onto regret-aversion-driven decision avoidance. Inman & Zeelenberg (2002) on repeat-vs-switch directly applies: groups often default to last week's restaurant precisely to dampen anticipated regret of trying somewhere new (this also matches Han et al. 2023's status-quo-preservation effect, g=-0.45).

**group_specificity**: Strongly amplified in groups, with direct empirical support post-2010. Three independent amplifiers stack: (1) social regret — the un-chosen option was someone's favorite, so picking creates a person-specific disappointment, not just an abstract counterfactual; (2) responsibility shedding — anticipated regret motivates each member to refuse to be the chooser, producing the 'you pick' / 'no you pick' loop unique to groups (Beattie et al. 1994; Bullens & van Harreveld 2016 unpublished data on responsibility); (3) shared observability — all members will witness the foregone-outcome signal, so anticipated regret is itself common knowledge and harder to suppress. Direct evidence: Nijstad 2008 (n=222 across 74 three-person groups), re-analyzed by White et al. — groups deferred on 42% of trials vs. individuals on 12% with identical job-candidate information; new 174-group experiment by White et al. replicated direction (10.5% vs. 8.7%, d≈0.2). Authors specifically noted that groups give 'additional weight to individual preferences to defer choice' beyond what majority rule predicts, deferring on 32 of 43 tied votes. This is the cleanest published evidence that groups are paralysis-prone in a way individuals are not, and it lands on GetToIt's exact population.

**timing_specificity**: Amplified by end-of-workday / Friday-evening timing through four mechanisms: (a) the slot is scarce and non-recoverable, raising the cost of a 'wasted' choice and therefore regret stakes; (b) decision fatigue / motivation-shift makes counterfactual simulation feel costlier, so the chooser preferentially exits the decision (deferral) — note that the decision-fatigue strong-resource model has weak replication post-Hagger 2016 RRR, so this amplifier is better framed as motivation/attention reallocation than glucose depletion, but the observable end-state is the same; (c) social plans elsewhere are most concrete in the same window, raising FOMO over un-chosen activities (this is the original Przybylski 2013 motivating use case); (d) deadline pressure paradoxically increases regret-driven avoidance up until the cliff edge, when forced commitment finally happens — often suboptimal, often the default (consistent with Han 2023 status-quo result). Akbari et al. 2021 also found COVID-19 (a context with elevated time-and-experience scarcity) significantly strengthened the FOMO-behavior link, supporting the slot-scarcity mechanism.

### Quiz Signal Implication

**signal_to_extract**: The quiz must extract: (1) regret-collapse signal — for each member, the worst foregone alternative they could tolerate not getting (the dimension on which they would feel actual regret vs. mild disappointment); (2) commitment threshold — would the member ratify a 'good enough' verdict if it were anonymously produced and clearly final; (3) veto / dealbreaker — converting unbounded regret comparisons into a bounded constraint (anything not vetoed is regret-acceptable by definition); (4) option-cap signal — the smallest viable option set (compressing alternatives reduces the counterfactual surface and exploits Han 2023's status-quo-preservation result); (5) responsibility-laundering signal — does each member endorse the verdict-mechanism (the algorithm picks, not a person), removing the 'who chose' regret target; (6) reversibility-stance signal — does the group accept the verdict as final-for-tonight (irreversible), exploiting Gilbert & Ebert 2002 / Liu et al. 2022 — irreversibility activates the psychological immune system and reduces counterfactual rumination.

**question_design_implications**: If anticipated-regret/FOMO is dominant, quiz questions should: (a) be phrased as constraints and vetoes, not preferences ('which of these would actually ruin tonight?' rather than 'rank your favorites' — vetoes bound regret, preferences expand it); (b) ask each member privately, in parallel, so no one's preference becomes a person-shaped regret target ('we did the Thai place because Sam said so' becomes 'we did the Thai place because the quiz said so'); (c) collapse, never expand — every question should reduce the option set, never present new options mid-flow (each new option re-opens regret); (d) include a single mood / energy / proximity question that breaks ties on a non-regret dimension (regret comparisons are dimensional; one orthogonal-axis question forces a non-counterfactual basis for the verdict); (e) end with a commit question that surfaces the verdict-acceptance — pre-committing to the algorithm's output dampens post-decisional regret because the chooser is the algorithm, not the member; (f) avoid questions that ask members to imagine specific un-chosen outcomes — that activates exactly the counterfactual simulation we are trying to suppress (consistent with Liu et al. 2022 mediation finding); (g) frame the verdict as final-for-tonight (irreversible within the session), exploiting Gilbert & Ebert 2002 reversibility result for higher post-choice satisfaction.

**mitigation_strategy**: Stack of product mechanics, in priority order: (1) Option compression — quiz outputs ONE verdict, not a ranked list; un-chosen options are not displayed alongside the verdict, so the counterfactual is not made salient at commit time. Directly leverages Han et al. 2023 status-quo-preservation effect (g=-0.45). (2) Responsibility laundering — frame the verdict as the system's output, not any member's pick; defuses social regret directly (Beattie et al. 1994 responsibility-aversion result) and aligns with Bullens & van Harreveld 2016 finding that responsibility-of-decision-outcome contributes to reversibility-driven regret. (3) Hard close / irreversibility within session — once the verdict is delivered, the app does not re-open the option set; reopening is friction, not a tap. Empirically grounded: Gilbert & Ebert 2002 photo-choice study showed irreversible-decision participants rated their choice higher than reversible-decision participants; Liu et al. 2022 PRBM mediation analysis showed reversibility lowers satisfaction via increased counterfactual thinking and anticipated regret; Bullens & van Harreveld 2016 review confirms direction. So 'hard close, no re-open' is a literature-backed prescription, not just a UX preference. Caveat: maximizers (Schwartz scale) may resist or be made worse-off by irreversibility — if the user base skews maximizer, surface a single 'redo from scratch' escape hatch rather than a menu-edit. (4) Pre-commitment — final quiz step asks each member to ratify 'we'll go with the verdict' BEFORE seeing it (binds them to procedural justice; reduces post-decisional regret because they consented to the process). (5) Veto-gated, not preference-aggregated — vetoes (regret-bounded) drive elimination, preferences (regret-unbounded) only break ties on the survivors. (6) Hide foregone-outcome signal post-verdict (don't show 'you almost picked X' screens; resist the social-comparison feed pattern that maximizes FOMO — directly informed by Akbari et al. 2021 finding that COVID-context scarcity amplified FOMO behaviors). (7) Optional anticipated-regret elicitation as v2 experiment: Brewer et al. 2016 meta-analysis suggests pre-decision regret-imagination interventions can shift behavior, but in a group-deferral context this risks amplifying paralysis — gate behind A/B test, not v1. (8) Deadline / time-box — short, visible quiz length (~5 questions); forced default if abandoned. (9) Default to recent venue when signal is weak (Inman & Zeelenberg 2002 repeat-purchase regret-justifiability + Han 2023 status-quo-preservation).

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the quiz ignores anticipated regret, the predicted failure mode is: verdict produced, but follow-through rate (north-star metric) collapses. Mechanism: (a) at verdict-display time, members re-imagine the un-chosen options and post-decisional regret spikes (Liu et al. 2022 mediation chain); (b) one member proposes 'actually, what about X' — re-opens the option set, regret-keeping; (c) verdict is silently abandoned, the group ends up at the default (last week's place / nowhere / individual takeout), confirming the paralysis pattern the product was supposed to defeat. Secondary failure: members complete the quiz but treat the verdict as a 'suggestion' rather than a commitment because the design did not ladder them into ratification — verdicts get rated 'good idea!' and ignored. Tertiary failure: groups stop using the product because each session leaves a residue of post-decisional regret discourse ('the app sent us to the wrong place'), even when the verdict was objectively reasonable — exactly the reversibility-induced regret pattern Gilbert & Ebert 2002 and Liu et al. 2022 predict if the system permits silent re-deliberation post-verdict.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Primary defuse target. Rationale: highest dominance for the food/going-out mid-stakes social-decision regime, directly couples to the north-star follow-through metric, and is mechanically defusible by quiz architecture (option compression, responsibility laundering, irreversibility, pre-commitment) without requiring AI or heavy infrastructure.

**dependency_on_other_causes**: Strongly amplifies and is amplified by: (a) Responsibility diffusion / blame-aversion (groups) — co-fires with anticipated regret; the same mechanism (responsibility laundering) defuses both; Bullens & van Harreveld 2016 explicitly link decision-responsibility to reversibility-driven regret. (b) Choice / information overload (Iyengar & Lepper 2000) — more options literally manufacture more counterfactuals, so option-compression mitigation does double duty. (c) Preference uncertainty / preference construction (Slovic 1995) — when preferences are constructed at decision time, the regret comparison has no stable baseline, increasing both. Partially substitutes for: (d) Decision fatigue — at end-of-day, fatigue reduces the cognitive cost of avoidance, so what looks like regret-paralysis can really be motivation-driven deferral; the two are observationally hard to separate but the same product fixes (option compression, hard close, default-friendly UX) work for both. Mostly orthogonal to: (e) Asymmetric information across the group (someone knows a venue is closed) — that is an information problem, not a regret problem, though regret can exploit information gaps as a refusal pretext.

## Misaligned Preferences (Group Preference Aggregation Failure)

_Source: `Misaligned_Preferences.json`_

### Basic Info

**cause_name**: Misaligned Preferences (Group Preference Aggregation Failure)

**originators**: Kenneth J. Arrow (impossibility theorem); Christian List & Philip Pettit (judgment aggregation / discursive dilemma); Amartya Sen (social choice and individual values); Marquis de Condorcet (voting paradox, 1785) as historical precursor

**origin_year**: 1951 (Arrow, 'Social Choice and Individual Values'); 2002 (List & Pettit, 'Aggregating Sets of Judgments')

**discipline**: Social choice theory / welfare economics / political philosophy; with downstream applications in group decision-making, behavioral economics, and organizational psychology

**one_sentence_summary**: When group members hold different preference orderings over options, no aggregation rule can satisfy basic fairness conditions simultaneously, so the group cycles, defers, or stalls instead of converging on a verdict.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1: Each member arrives with a private preference ordering over candidate options (e.g., Alice: sushi > pizza > tacos; Bob: pizza > tacos > sushi; Carol: tacos > sushi > pizza). Step 2: The group attempts informal aggregation, typically pairwise comparison or open suggestion ('how about X?'). Step 3: Pairwise majority voting yields cycles (Condorcet paradox: sushi beats pizza, pizza beats tacos, tacos beats sushi), so no option is a stable winner. Step 4: Arrow's theorem guarantees that no rank-based rule satisfying universal domain, Pareto, IIA, and non-dictatorship exists, so any procedure the group invents either violates fairness or produces an arbitrary result. Step 5: Members sense the unfairness or arbitrariness and resist committing — the loser feels imposed-upon, the winner feels guilty, the group recycles options. Step 6: Without a legitimate aggregation rule and without a designated dictator, deliberation extends indefinitely, defaults trigger (eat at the closest place, give up, order in separately), or one member unilaterally decides and the group complies with low buy-in. Step 7: Low buy-in shows up later as low follow-through — members renege, complain en route, or sandbag the experience.

**behavioral_signature**: Observable signs: (a) cycling — the group revisits the same 2-3 options repeatedly without convergence ('we already said no to Thai'); (b) escalation of veto-talk — members switch from positive preferences to negative vetoes ('not sushi again'); (c) deference cascades — 'I'm fine with anything, what do you want?' bouncing around the group with no one committing; (d) procedural disputes — meta-arguments about *how* to decide (vote? rotate? oldest picks?) rather than *what* to decide; (e) splintering threats — 'maybe we should just do separate things'; (f) post-decision grumbling and reduced follow-through from members whose top choice lost. Distinct from Paradox of Choice (which afflicts solo deciders facing many options) and from Decision Fatigue (which affects any decider late in the day regardless of preference structure).

**typical_triggers**: Heterogeneous group composition (members with different cuisines, dietary needs, budgets, energy levels); absence of a designated decider or rotating dictatorship; flat power structure (friend groups, peer couples) where no one has standing to impose; mid-stakes decisions (high enough that members care about the outcome, low enough that nobody invests in formal procedure); synchronous text-based deliberation (where pairwise cycling is easy and commitment is cheap to defer); group sizes 3-6 (2 collapses to bilateral negotiation; 7+ usually triggers a self-appointed organizer).

### Evidence Base

**known_limitations**: (1) Arrow's theorem assumes ordinal preferences and rules out interpersonal utility comparisons; if members can express *intensity* (cardinal utility, e.g., 'I really hate sushi, mild preference for tacos'), aggregation becomes tractable (range voting, score voting evade Arrow). (2) Domain restrictions (single-peaked preferences, e.g., agreement on a spice-level axis) eliminate cycles — so Arrow's pessimism overstates real-world paralysis when preferences are correlated. (3) The theorem is about *rules*, not *outcomes* — groups routinely decide despite Arrow, via informal dictatorship, log-rolling, or norm-following. (4) Critics (Mackie 2003, 'Democracy Defended') argue empirical Condorcet cycles are rare in real elections; analogously, mid-stakes friend-group cycles may be less common than theory predicts. (5) The construct under-specifies how *much* paralysis vs. how much smooth resolution actually occurs in 2-6 person food decisions — and probably overlaps heavily with social friction (face-saving) as the proximate behavioral cause.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: Strong theoretical fit. The GetToIt use case (groups of 2-6, food vertical, mid-stakes, going-out, end-of-day) is precisely the regime where: (a) preferences are heterogeneous (different cravings, dietary restrictions, prior visits), (b) no member has legitimate authority to dictate, (c) the group has no formal aggregation procedure, and (d) the cost of formalizing one exceeds the stakes. Cycling, vetoing, and deference cascades in restaurant text threads are the canonical real-world manifestation of Arrovian aggregation failure. However, in everyday food decisions, raw preference *misalignment* often co-presents with social friction (politeness/face) — members don't surface their true preference because asserting it feels imposing, which makes the *signal* missing rather than the aggregation rule failing. Disentangling pure-misalignment paralysis from politeness-driven null-signal paralysis is hard.

**dominance_estimate**: High. For groups of 3+ with mixed cuisine preferences and no clear organizer, misaligned preferences is plausibly the single dominant cause of food-decision paralysis — more dominant than choice overload (which targets solo deciders) or decision fatigue (which afflicts any decider). For 2-person groups it weakens (bilateral negotiation usually resolves) and other causes (fatigue, social friction) take over. Net estimate for the v1 use case: high dominance for 3-6 person groups, medium for 2-person. Rationale: the structural conditions (heterogeneous preferences + flat authority + low procedural investment) match the use case almost exactly, and the behavioral signatures (cycling, vetoing, deference) are the most commonly described group-dining failure mode in lay accounts.

**group_specificity**: Strongly amplified in groups; effectively non-existent solo. This is the *only* paralysis cause on the candidate list that is intrinsically group-defined — Arrow's theorem requires 2+ agents with different preferences. Solo deciders cannot have misaligned preferences (they have one preference ordering, possibly conflicted internal selves, but not Arrovian aggregation failure). For GetToIt, this is the most group-specific cause and therefore the most product-relevant if the goal is differentiating from solo decision tools.

**timing_specificity**: Indirectly amplified by end-of-workday timing. The misalignment itself is structural (depends on preference distribution, not time of day), but end-of-day conditions amplify the *consequences*: (a) low willingness to invest in procedural negotiation, (b) lower tolerance for cycling and re-deliberation, (c) higher likelihood of defaulting to 'whatever' which is a low-buy-in resolution that hurts follow-through. So the cause is timing-neutral in origin but timing-amplified in impact for the v1 use case.

### Quiz Signal Implication

**signal_to_extract**: Two complementary signals: (1) **Cardinal preference intensity per member** — not just 'do you want sushi?' but 'how much?' on a scale that allows interpersonal comparison (range voting evades Arrow). (2) **Hard vetoes / dealbreakers** — explicit constraints (allergies, recent visits, distance, budget caps) that prune the option space *before* aggregation, eliminating the regions where cycling occurs. Secondary: a **commitment threshold** — each member's minimum acceptable score for follow-through, so the verdict picks the option that clears everyone's threshold rather than maximizing average. The quiz must surface intensity + vetoes from each member privately (so social friction doesn't mute them) and aggregate to a single committed verdict using a non-cyclical rule (score sum, approval voting, or veto-then-score).

**question_design_implications**: (a) Run the quiz **in parallel, privately** — each member answers independently before seeing others, so politeness/anchoring don't collapse the signal. (b) Use **score scales, not pick-one** — 'rate how much you want X right now' (1-5 or thumbs) lets members express intensity, evading Arrow's ordinal trap. (c) Include an explicit **veto question** ('anything you absolutely cannot/will not do tonight?') so dealbreakers are surfaced upfront and the option space is pruned before scoring. (d) Include a **constraint question** (budget, distance, time, dietary) to further prune. (e) Cap to ~5 questions to respect end-of-day fatigue. (f) Aggregate via a transparent rule (sum of scores minus any veto = winner) so members perceive the verdict as fair, increasing buy-in and follow-through. (g) Show each member that the verdict cleared their threshold — surfacing legitimacy is what converts a winner into a committed group.

**mitigation_strategy**: Product mechanics: (1) **Anonymous parallel input** — defuses social friction overlay and gets honest preferences. (2) **Score voting / approval voting / veto-and-score** instead of plurality — mathematically evades Arrow, perceived as fair. (3) **Option compression / curation** — quiz proposes a small slate (3-5 vetted options) rather than open-ended brainstorming, which reduces cycle frequency and respects working memory. (4) **Hard veto channel** — separate input for dealbreakers prevents passive-aggressive last-minute objections. (5) **Commitment step** — after the verdict, each member taps to commit; absence of unanimous commit triggers a re-roll on the second-best option. (6) **Deadline / default** — if the quiz isn't completed in N minutes the highest-scored answered option wins, killing deliberation drift. (7) **Verdict legitimacy display** — show 'this option scored X across the group, cleared everyone's threshold' so the result feels procedurally legitimate, raising follow-through.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the quiz extracts only constraints or only mood (without intensity + veto per member), the verdict will be a plausible-looking option that some member silently disprefers; that member follows through reluctantly or sandbags (arrives late, complains, suggests bailing). North-star metric (verdicts followed-through) drops. Worse failure mode: if the quiz produces a single group-level option using a plurality-like rule, it can pick a Condorcet loser — an option a majority would defeat in pairwise comparison — and members notice the unfairness, eroding trust in the product after a few uses.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Primary defuse target. Rationale: it is the most group-specific cause on the list, structurally matches the use case (3-6 person flat groups, food, mid-stakes), and dictates the core quiz architecture (parallel private input, intensity scoring, veto channel, score-based aggregation). Defusing it also partially defuses social friction (anonymous input) and choice overload (option compression).

**dependency_on_other_causes**: Strong interactions: (a) **Social Friction amplifies it** — politeness norms suppress honest preference signals, making aggregation operate on noisy/null data and worsening cycling. Anonymous parallel input defuses both simultaneously. (b) **Decision Fatigue amplifies its consequences** — tired members default to 'whatever' rather than investing in resolution, so misalignment converts to low-buy-in defaults. (c) **Paradox of Choice can mask it** — when the option set is huge, members frame the problem as 'too many options' when it's actually 'we want different things'; option compression addresses both. (d) **Anticipated Regret can substitute for it** — a member who would normally express a preference instead defers to avoid being blamed if the choice goes badly, producing the same null-signal outcome via a different mechanism. (e) **Cognitive Load is largely independent** — capacity limits affect any decider, not specifically group misalignment.

### Sources

**primary_sources**: Arrow, K. J. (1951). Social Choice and Individual Values. New York: Wiley. (2nd ed. 1963, 3rd ed. 2012 with foreword by Eric Maskin.); List, C., & Pettit, P. (2002). Aggregating sets of judgments: An impossibility result. Economics and Philosophy, 18(1), 89-110.; Sen, A. K. (1970). Collective Choice and Social Welfare. San Francisco: Holden-Day.; Condorcet, M. de (1785). Essai sur l'application de l'analyse a la probabilite des decisions rendues a la pluralite des voix.; Gibbard, A. (1973). ## Paradox of Choice / Choice Overload

_Source: `Paradox_of_Choice.json`_

### Basic Info

**cause_name**: Paradox of Choice / Choice Overload

**originators**: Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper (empirical demonstration); Barry Schwartz (popularization and theoretical synthesis)

**origin_year**: 2000 (Iyengar & Lepper JPSP); 2004 (Schwartz book)

**discipline**: Social and consumer psychology / behavioral decision research (with strong reach into behavioral economics and marketing)

**one_sentence_summary**: When the number of options grows past a manageable size, decision-makers find it harder to choose, more likely to defer or abandon the decision, and less satisfied with whatever they pick.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1: Assortment size grows beyond the chooser's ability to evaluate options pairwise (working memory and attention limits bind). Step 2: Each option triggers tradeoff thinking, increasing perceived effort and the salience of foregone alternatives. Step 3: Evaluative difficulty raises the perceived cost of choosing wrong, which inflates anticipated regret. Step 4: To avoid the regret of an avoidable bad choice, the chooser defers, randomizes, or exits the decision entirely. Step 5: If a choice is made, residual awareness of un-chosen options reduces post-choice satisfaction and increases second-guessing, which depresses follow-through and commitment to the chosen option.

**behavioral_signature**: Observable signs: long deliberation with little narrowing (cycling through the same options), repeated 'I don't know, you pick' deferrals, switching between candidates without converging, choosing the default or status-quo (e.g., 'we always go to X'), abandoning the decision ('let's just stay in / order in'), expressing dissatisfaction immediately after the verdict ('but maybe we should have done Y'), and post-hoc revision attempts. Distinguishes from anticipated regret (which fixates on a specific foregone option) and from misaligned preferences (which produces overt disagreement rather than collective drift).

**typical_triggers**: Large unfiltered option sets (e.g., scrolling DoorDash, Yelp, or an open 'where should we eat' question with no constraints applied); options that are roughly comparable in attractiveness (no obvious dominator); low pre-existing preference clarity ('I don't know what I want'); time pressure combined with high option count; novel or unfamiliar option spaces; and contexts where the chooser feels accountable for group satisfaction. Amplified when there is no curation layer and no commitment device.

### Evidence Base

**known_limitations**: 1. The unconditional effect is small to null in meta-analysis. 2. Effect depends on moderators (preference clarity, set complexity, optimizer vs. satisficer disposition, time pressure) that are often unstated in popular accounts. 3. Most studies are individual choosers in lab settings — group-level evidence is thinner. 4. Cultural moderators exist (effect varies between cultures with different self-construal). 5. The 'magic number' (e.g., 7 options) is not empirically pinned; the threshold depends on option complexity. 6. Schwartz's maximizer/satisficer distinction is real but the maximizer scale has psychometric criticisms.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: High fit to the GetToIt use case in moderated form, not the strong form. The food-going-out decision sits squarely in the high-moderator zone: option sets are large (entire city of restaurants), options are roughly comparable (most restaurants are 'fine'), preference clarity is low ('I don't know what I want'), and at end-of-workday the chooser tends toward optimization-by-anxiety rather than satisficing. Group dimension amplifies because each member projects unspoken constraints onto the full option set. The classic 'we cycled through ten places and ended up ordering pizza' failure mode is the textbook moderated choice-overload pattern.

**group_specificity**: Amplified in groups. Each additional member multiplies the constraint surface (allergies, distance, budget, mood), and each member privately enumerates a slightly different effective option set, making convergence harder. Groups also lack a single arbiter, so the 'optimizer' framing dominates ('let's find the place that works for everyone') — and optimizing across a large set is exactly the high-overload regime. Solo choosers can satisfice; groups under social pressure cannot easily satisfice on each other's behalf.

**timing_specificity**: Amplified at end-of-workday. Cognitive resources for evaluation and tradeoff are depleted (overlap with decision fatigue), preference clarity is at its lowest ('I just want food'), and the optimizer-leaning anxious mode becomes more likely because the stakes feel higher when one is tired and hungry. The combination of low preference clarity plus large option set plus depletion is the worst-case configuration for choice overload.

### Quiz Signal Implication

**signal_to_extract**: Primary signal: aggressive option-space compression via constraints and a hard option cap. The quiz must extract (a) hard constraints (dietary, distance, budget, time-window) to prune the space deterministically before any preference question, (b) mood or cuisine direction at coarse granularity (not fine ranking), and (c) a commitment threshold (would you accept this if it cleared X bar?). The signal is not 'what does each person prefer most' — it is 'what set of options can we eliminate with confidence' plus 'what is the lowest-acceptable bar each person will commit to.'

**question_design_implications**: If choice overload is dominant, questions must (1) be elimination-oriented rather than ranking-oriented (cheaper cognitively, narrows the space faster), (2) use coarse buckets not fine sliders ('hot food / cold food / either' beats a 1-10 cuisine score), (3) front-load constraints before preferences (pruning the set first reduces the load on every later question), (4) avoid showing the full option set during the quiz (showing 50 restaurants while asking preferences re-creates the overload), and (5) end with a commitment question that locks the verdict before the brain re-opens the option space. Five questions is well-matched: roughly 2 constraint, 2 mood/direction, 1 commitment.

**mitigation_strategy**: 1. Aggressive option compression: target a final candidate set of 3-5, not 10+. 2. Hidden option set during quiz — never show the long list. 3. Single-verdict output — present one answer with a soft fallback, not a top-3 menu (a ranked list re-creates the overload at verdict time). 4. Commitment device at the end — explicit yes/no lock-in question that ends the decision. 5. Default rule for ties — pre-committed tiebreaker (random, last-time-rule, host-picks) prevents re-deliberation. 6. Anonymous parallel input prevents one person's enumeration from re-opening the space for others. 7. Time-box the quiz to short, prevents drift back into open-ended browsing.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: If the quiz extracts only preferences without constraints and option-cap, the verdict will surface a candidate from a still-large effective option set; users will see the verdict and immediately think of an un-considered option, re-open the decision, and either abandon (order in) or override the verdict. Follow-through tanks. The verdict feels arbitrary because the user has not experienced the option space being narrowed — only being sampled. The product becomes a suggestion engine rather than a decision-killer.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Primary defuse target. The product's core mechanic (5-question quiz to single verdict) is essentially a choice-overload defuser; this cause must be designed against directly.

**dependency_on_other_causes**: Substrate for: anticipated regret (large option set is what makes the foregone option salient), decision fatigue (large set amplifies depletion costs), and misaligned preferences (large set means more dimensions on which to disagree). Masked by: social friction (the group may attribute deadlock to politeness when the underlying cause is option-space size). Substitutes for: cognitive load in the working-memory sense — they share a mechanism but choice overload adds the affective regret-anticipation layer. Defusing choice overload partially defuses the others; defusing the others without defusing choice overload leaves the core paralysis intact.

## Social Friction (Taste-Imposition Aversion)

_Source: `Social_Friction.json`_

### Basic Info

**cause_name**: Social Friction (Taste-Imposition Aversion)

**originators**: Erving Goffman (face-work, presentation of self); Penelope Brown and Stephen Levinson (politeness theory, FTA / face-threatening acts); Jerry B. Harvey (Abilene paradox); Floyd H. Allport, Daniel Katz and Richard Schanck, Hubert O'Gorman (pluralistic-ignorance line); Steven Pinker, Martin Nowak, James Lee (game-theoretic logic of indirect speech); Nicole Y. J. Kim, Yonat Zwebner, Alixandra Barasch, Rom Y. Schrift (no-preference communication in joint decisions)

**origin_year**: 1955 (Goffman 'On Face-Work'); 1959 (Goffman 'Presentation of Self'); 1978/1987 (Brown & Levinson 'Politeness'); 1974 (Harvey 'Abilene Paradox'); 1985 (Stasser & Titus hidden profile); 1988 (Matsumoto critique of universality); 1993 (Prentice & Miller pluralistic ignorance); 2000 (Ariely & Levav sequential group choice); 2008 (Pinker, Nowak & Lee logic of indirect speech, PNAS); 2023 (Kim, Zwebner, Barasch & Schrift no-preference communication)

**discipline**: Sociology / symbolic interactionism (Goffman); sociolinguistics and pragmatics (Brown & Levinson, Pinker-Nowak-Lee); social psychology (Harvey, pluralistic-ignorance line, Asch tradition); consumer behavior / marketing (Ariely & Levav, Kim et al.); group decision research / OB (Stasser & Titus hidden-profile paradigm); HCI / CSCW (anonymous-input studies, Doodle-poll work)

**one_sentence_summary**: Group members suppress or soften their real preferences to protect each other's 'face' and avoid the appearance of imposing taste, so the group receives a false-null signal and either drifts to a default no one wants or stalls without a verdict.

### Mechanism

**causal_mechanism**: Step 1 (antecedent): a group must pick a single jointly consumed option (restaurant, plan) where preferences are visible to peers and the chosen option is taste-attributable to whoever proposed it. Step 2 (face-stake): each member privately holds a preference but recognizes that asserting it (a) commits them to the outcome's quality, (b) risks imposing a taste others may dislike, and (c) threatens the negative face (autonomy) of others, in Brown & Levinson's terms. Step 3 (FTA mitigation): per Brown & Levinson 1987, members downgrade the face-threatening act via hedging ('I'm easy', 'whatever', 'I could do anything'), question-deflecting ('what are you feeling?'), or off-record indirectness; Pinker, Nowak & Lee 2008 show in a game-theoretic frame that indirect speech delivers plausible deniability when relationship type is uncertain. Step 4 (signal collapse): the collective output is a stack of low-information utterances; no proposal has anchor support. Step 5 (cascade via pluralistic ignorance): each member, observing others' apparent indifference, infers (incorrectly, per the pluralistic-ignorance literature from Allport through Prentice & Miller 1993) that asserting their preference would be uniquely impositional, so they suppress harder. Step 6 (paralysis or drift): either no decision is reached (stall), or the group converges on a least-objectionable default (often a place no one actively wanted), reproducing the Abilene-paradox pattern; Kim et al. 2023 add the empirical wrinkle that recipients of 'I have no preference' do not take it at face value but infer hidden, dissimilar preferences and choose options the silent partner likes less, so the polite hedge directly degrades outcome quality. Step 7 (low follow-through): because no member's actual preference was satisfied and no one feels ownership of the verdict, motivation to actually show up is low.

**behavioral_signature**: (1) High frequency of hedged, deferring utterances ('I don't care', 'whatever', 'you pick', 'I'm down for anything') — the diagnostic linguistic signature, and the exact form Kim et al. 2023 study under the label 'no-preference communication'. (2) Preference reversals when polled privately vs publicly (a member who said 'I don't mind' privately ranks options strongly). (3) Long latency to first concrete proposal; lots of meta-talk about deciding rather than substantive proposals. (4) Recursive deflection loops ('what do you want?' / 'whatever you want' / 'no really, you pick'). (5) Post-decision regret expressed privately but not in-group ('I didn't really want sushi but didn't want to be difficult'). (6) Convergence on bland or default options (the chain restaurant, the same place as last time). (7) Vetoes appear only when preferences are extreme or when a member feels safe (closer relationships, repeated games). (8) In sequential ordering (Ariely & Levav 2000, JCR vol. 27), members take a 'road less traveled' to avoid duplicating earlier orders, then reliably enjoy that order less — the same uniqueness-/face-driven distortion shows up in real restaurant tables. (9) Use of indirect, deniable speech acts (questions, conditional statements) where direct requests would be more efficient — Pinker, Nowak & Lee 2008 frame this as the strategic-speaker signature.

**typical_triggers**: Mixed-status or mixed-familiarity groups (new dating partners, coworkers, friend-of-friend hangs); groups of 3+ where coalition costs rise; decisions where outcome quality is observable and attributable (restaurant choice, activity choice); cultures or contexts with high politeness norms (Matsumoto 1988 documents stronger face-protection in Japanese, but the basic pattern shows up across the Brown-Levinson cross-cultural sample of English, Tzeltal, and Tamil); situations where one member is the de facto host / inviter (others defer); end-of-day timing when cognitive resources for diplomatic phrasing are depleted; ambiguous mid-stakes (trivial stakes don't activate face concerns, high stakes force preferences out, so mid-stakes is the dead zone where suppression dominates); asynchronous text threads where indirectness is cheaper than face-to-face. Friend-vs-stranger composition is non-trivial: McKelvey & Kerr 1988 found subjects in stranger groups conformed to incorrect group norms more frequently than subjects in friend groups, suggesting close friends suppress their preferences less — but other work (Sciencedaily 2023 on adolescents with few friends) shows that when relationship maintenance is the dominant goal, friends conform more on small choices to avoid friction. The two findings are reconcilable: friends are more willing to express counter-preferences on substantive matters, but more willing to defer on low-stakes 'where to eat' choices because relationship maintenance > getting your way on dinner.

### Evidence Base

**known_limitations**: (1) Politeness/face theory is partly culture-specific; magnitude varies (East Asian contexts often show stronger face-protection — Matsumoto 1988 argues Japanese honorifics are not even FTA-redress; Northern European contexts show more direct preference assertion; Doodle-poll cross-national data show collectivist countries reach consensus on fewer options than individualist ones). (2) Effect is moderated by group closeness in opposite directions for different stakes — McKelvey & Kerr 1988 show stranger groups conform more than friend groups on factual tasks, but adolescent and adult relationship-maintenance research shows friends conform more on low-stakes choices to preserve harmony. (3) Hard to distinguish from genuine indifference; 'I don't care' sometimes really means 'I don't care'. Kim et al. 2023 specifically show recipients refuse to take the statement at face value, but the underlying-state ambiguity is real and the core measurement problem for any product. (4) Abilene paradox specifically has been criticized as overstated — many cases of group convergence are genuine compromise, not mutual misreading. (5) Ego-depletion / decision-fatigue literature on which 'fatigue raises politeness costs' arguments lean has weak replication: Hagger et al. 2016 multilab N=2,141 found d = 0.04 [-0.07, 0.15], not different from zero; so timing-amplification claims for social friction must rest on cognitive-load arguments rather than ego-depletion. (6) Pluralistic ignorance requires that members misread the distribution of others' preferences; in repeated groups with known preferences, the mechanism weakens. (7) Brown & Levinson's universal-face claim is contested; first/second-order politeness distinctions (Watts, Locher) reframe what counts as the relevant phenomenon. (8) Self-report methods are vulnerable to demand effects. (9) Gender, status, and power differentials interact strongly; suppression is asymmetric in mixed-power groups (lower-status members suppress more), which the construct alone does not fully model. (10) Anonymity remedies are contested: Postmes & Lea 2000 meta-analytic work on computer-mediated communication found no reliable benefit of anonymity on decision quality, idea volume, or satisfaction in aggregate — the SIDE-model literature shows anonymity sometimes increases conformity to a salient group identity rather than freeing dissent. So 'just make the input anonymous' is not a guaranteed defuse; the design has to do more work than that.

### GetToIt Fit

**fit_to_use_case**: High fit. The GetToIt core scenario (groups of 2-6, mid-stakes, food/going-out, end-of-day) is the prototype case for taste-imposition aversion. The diagnostic linguistic signature ('whatever you want', 'I'm easy', 'you pick') is the meme-level common experience these decisions trigger and the exact form Kim et al. 2023 study experimentally. Group size 2-6 is in the band where coalition costs are nontrivial but individual voices still matter, maximizing face stakes; Ariely & Levav 2000 demonstrate the effect at table sizes in this range. Food choice is taste-attributable (a bad pick reflects on the proposer), going-out has observable outcome quality, and end-of-day timing depletes the cognitive budget required for diplomatic, indirect preference-signaling — though the depletion mechanism should be argued from cognitive-load theory rather than ego-depletion (which failed to replicate). The mid-stakes nature is critical: trivial stakes don't activate face concerns, high stakes force preferences out; mid-stakes is exactly the dead zone where suppression dominates. Friend-group composition (the GetToIt target) means face concerns are reduced on substantive disagreements but elevated on low-stakes relationship-maintenance choices — exactly where 'where to eat' lives. The fact that follow-through is the north-star metric is also diagnostic: per Kim et al. 2023, the polite hedge produces a verdict the deflector likes less, which directly predicts low show-up.

**dominance_estimate**: High. Likely the most use-case-specific paralysis cause for GetToIt, and one of the top two overall (the other strong candidate being choice-overload / Schwartz-style option paralysis). Rationale: (a) the canonical 'where do you want to eat?' / 'I don't know, you pick' loop is precisely this construct in lay form, and Kim et al. 2023 establish experimentally that this hedge backfires by making the chooser select what the deflector likes less; (b) it explains both the no-decision and the wrong-decision-with-low-follow-through outcomes the product targets; (c) it is uniquely amplified by the group-first product positioning (solo decisions don't trigger face stakes); (d) it interacts multiplicatively with mid-stakes ambiguity and end-of-workday cognitive load, both core to the use case. Caveat: dominance varies by group composition — very close groups may be more constrained by choice overload than face concerns on dinner specifically; mixed/new groups will be more constrained by face. The product likely needs to defuse this for the broader market and choice-overload for the tight-group market.

**group_specificity**: Strongly amplified in groups; effectively absent in solo decisions. This is the most group-specific of the major paralysis causes — face stakes require an audience, and a self-audience does not generate FTA mitigation. Within groups, the cause is amplified by (a) lower familiarity (McKelvey & Kerr 1988 stranger > friend conformity on substantive disagreement), (b) mixed status/power, (c) larger size up to a point (more faces to protect, until coalition formation in groups of 6+ reduces individual face-load), (d) public/observable choice, (e) text/async channels where indirectness is easier (Pinker-Nowak-Lee plausible-deniability mechanism is cheaper in writing than face-to-face). It is muted but not eliminated in tight-knit groups — friends suppress less on substantive matters but may suppress more on low-stakes relationship-maintenance choices like dinner, since relationship value > meal value.

### Quiz Signal Implication

**signal_to_extract**: Private, anonymous, structured preference signal — specifically: (1) latent ranked or scored preferences each member would not voice publicly, (2) hard vetoes / dietary constraints that members under-report in public ('I'm fine with anything' often hides 'actually I really don't want Indian tonight'; Kim et al. 2023 confirm this is the modal pattern), (3) intensity / threshold information (how much does this member care vs not care — distinguishing real indifference from polite indifference, the central measurement problem), (4) commitment / follow-through likelihood per option (the regret-preview signal), (5) mood/vibe constraints (energy level, want-to-be-seen, want-quiet) that members hide because they sound demanding. The quiz must convert face-threatening public utterances into face-safe private inputs, then surface only the aggregated verdict, not the individual votes — the structural feature that the no-preference-communication literature implies is necessary, since the act of communicating no-preference itself degrades the joint outcome.

**question_design_implications**: (1) Parallel/private input — every member answers independently before seeing others' answers; this is the single most important design move and the structural defuse for the no-preference-communication backfire identified by Kim et al. 2023. (2) Forced choice / forced ranking — 'pick one' or 'rank these three' beats 'do you want X?' because the latter invites the hedge. (3) Veto-with-no-justification — let members kill an option without explaining why, removing the face cost of justifying a constraint. (4) Intensity capture — each preference should carry a weight or threshold ('I'd be disappointed' vs 'I'd be fine'), not a binary, so polite indifference is distinguishable from real indifference. (5) Constraint questions framed as logistics (budget, time, distance) rather than taste — easier to assert without face cost, since logistical constraints don't trigger the FTA frame. (6) Mood / energy question — captures the suppressed 'I want something low-key' signal. (7) Commitment-check at end — 'will you actually go?' surfaces face-saved fake-yes. (8) Avoid open-ended 'what do you want?' — it is the exact prompt that triggers the hedge in real life and is the prompt the Kim et al. 2023 deflectors are responding to.

**mitigation_strategy**: (1) Parallel anonymous input — the structural defuse, removes face cost of asserting; note the Postmes & Lea 2000 caveat that anonymity alone is not magic, so the rest of the mechanic has to do work too. (2) Aggregation surfaces a single verdict, not individual votes — preserves face after the fact and prevents the post-decision face-saving cascade. (3) Option compression — narrow the field to roughly three candidates (consistent with the EBA + Satisficing P1 hybrid) so members face a tractable forced-choice rather than open hedging space; this also reduces the choice-overload interaction. (4) Veto budget — each member gets one or two free vetoes, no justification required, normalizing constraint-assertion. (5) Default with deadline — if quiz signal is weak, the system commits to a default and shows a deadline, forcing members to escalate only if they genuinely care; this also separates polite indifference from real indifference. (6) Regret preview — show each member 'how you'd feel if X wins' before locking in, surfacing suppressed preferences. (7) Commitment check — explicit yes/no on follow-through after verdict, raising the cost of polite fake-agreement and predicting show-up directly. (8) Asymmetric reveal — show the verdict without showing who voted what, so members can publicly deny a preference they privately registered, preserving face. (9) Reframe as 'what would you regret missing' rather than 'what do you want' — flips the face calculus. (10) Avoid replicating the Doodle-poll 'open vs hidden' problem (Zou, Meir & Parkes 2014, CSCW): keep all individual responses hidden from other respondents during input, since open responses invite strategic and conformist deviation from true preferences.

**failure_mode_if_ignored**: Verdicts technically reached but with low follow-through. The quiz produces a winner, members agree publicly (because the same face dynamics that suppressed input still operate at confirmation), but the chosen option does not match anyone's real preference strongly, so motivation to actually show up is low. Predicted failure pattern: high quiz-completion rate, high verdict-agreement rate, low show-up rate. A second failure mode: the quiz becomes another surface for hedging — if questions are open-ended or ask 'what do you want?', members hedge inside the quiz too, and the system inherits the same false-null signal it was meant to defuse (Kim et al. 2023 imply this directly). A third failure mode: members start gaming the quiz by submitting strategic 'fine with anything' answers to avoid imposing, replicating the offline pattern; the Doodle-poll strategic-voting literature (Zou, Meir & Parkes 2014) is the cautionary case.

### Recommendation

**priority_for_v1**: Primary defuse target. Rationale: it is the most use-case-specific cause (solo-decision paralysis causes don't apply to GetToIt's group-first positioning), it directly threatens the north-star metric (follow-through) via the Kim et al. 2023 backfire mechanism, and the mitigation (parallel private input + intensity capture + veto budget) is structural to the quiz mechanic, not a feature add-on.

**dependency_on_other_causes**: Amplifies and is amplified by several others. (a) Pluralistic ignorance: directly causal — face-saved hedges create the misperception that no one cares, which deepens suppression in others (mutually amplifying); the Frontiers 2023 century-review treats this as the same family. (b) Choice overload: when option set is large, hedging is cheaper than ranking, so overload masks face-suppression and they compound — defusing one helps the other. (c) Decision fatigue / end-of-day depletion: the cognitive-load mechanism (not the failed-to-replicate ego-depletion mechanism) lowers the budget for direct-preference assertion, intensifying suppression. (d) Abilene paradox: a downstream consequence — the group converges on an option no member privately wanted because each suppressed their preference; Kim et al. 2023's no-preference-communication backfire is the modern experimental cousin. (e) Hidden profile / biased information sampling (Stasser & Titus 1985; Lu, Yuan & McLeod 2012 meta-analysis): the same dynamic operating on factual information rather than preferences — both produce a group output that under-weights minority signal. (f) Conflict-avoidance / topic-avoidance research (Caughlin & Afifi 2004): same family, broader scope — covers face-saving plus relational-maintenance motives. Defusing social friction also partly defuses pluralistic ignorance and the Abilene paradox; defusing it does not address overload or fatigue, which need their own mechanics.
